### Concern 3: Part II's Conclusion Omits Section VI's Contribution to the Model Requirements

**Document:** `02-theoretical-foundations.md`, Conclusion

**Nature of concern:** Structural coherence / incomplete derivation

The Conclusion to Part II specifies what the theoretical foundations generate as requirements for the governance architecture — three requirements, one from each tradition. Section VI is absent from this specification:

> *"From classical rhetoric: [requirements listed].*
> *From Resource Dependence Theory: [requirements listed].*
> *From the JIC: [requirements listed].*
> *These requirements, taken together, specify the SCI model."*

Section VI contributes a requirement that the JIC alone does not supply: the governance architecture must be organized around a sector-agnostic taxonomy of organizational audience types differentiated by institutional logic — because the nature of the required engagement and the rhetorical mode appropriate to each domain differs systematically by the institutional rules governing that domain's exercise of influence. Part III's multi-domain architecture (Government, Information, Civil Society, Market) and the different rhetorical emphasis appropriate to each domain (phronesis predominant in Government, arete in Civil Society, etc.) are derived from Section VI. The Conclusion should say so.

There is also an opportunity here to add the citation of Thornton, Ocasio & Lounsbury's institutional logics framework, which provides the external scholarly grounding for the "institutional logics" claim that Section VI invokes but does not currently cite. The foundational institutional logics literature (Friedland & Alford, 1991; Thornton, Ocasio & Lounsbury, 2012) provides the empirical and theoretical validation that the taxonomy's sector-agnosticism claim needs.

**Additional information needed:** Citations confirmed from existing knowledge:
- Friedland, R., & Alford, R. R. (1991). "Bringing society back in." In Powell & DiMaggio, *The New Institutionalism in Organizational Analysis*. The foundational paper establishing capitalism, democracy, and other logics as distinct institutional orders.
- Thornton, P. H., Ocasio, W., & Lounsbury, M. (2012). *The Institutional Logics Perspective.* Oxford University Press. The systematic development of institutional logics theory, identifying capitalism (Market), the state (Government), professions (Information/knowledge), and community (Civil Society) as structurally distinct orders — mapping closely to SCI's four domains.

The fit of the Thornton et al. taxonomy to SCI's four domains is not perfect but is close enough to be worth noting: their professional/knowledge order maps approximately to the Information domain; their community order to Civil Society. The theoretical congruence is genuine and worth acknowledging.

**Proposed resolution:**
1. Add a sentence to the Conclusion deriving from Section VI the requirement that the governance architecture be organized around a sector-agnostic domain taxonomy differentiating audiences by institutional logic
2. Add a brief citation note within Section VI connecting its use of "institutional logics" to Friedland & Alford (1991) and Thornton et al. (2012)

**Status:** Addressable within existing document. Two minor additions to `02-theoretical-foundations.md` warranted.

### Concern 4: Agonistic Tradition Citation Order in Part IV Is Historically Imprecise

**Document:** `04-normative-foundations.md`, Section VII

**Nature of concern:** Scholarly precision / intellectual history

Section VII contains the sentence:

> *"Mouffe's distinction, developed by Arendt, Honig, and Connolly, provides the philosophical vocabulary for this transformation."*

This formulation is historically confused in one respect. Arendt (1906–1975) predates Mouffe (b. 1943) and cannot have "developed" Mouffe's distinction in any direct sense. Arendt's account of politics as action in the public realm (*The Human Condition*, 1958; "What is Freedom?", 1961) is properly understood as a *source* of the agonistic tradition — one of the intellectual roots from which later theorists, including Honig, drew — not as a development of Mouffe's specific antagonism/agonism framework, which Mouffe derives primarily from Carl Schmitt and Gramsci.

The correct intellectual history:
- **Arendt** — precursor, provides the foundational account of politics as agonistic public action among equals; her work is a source rather than a development of Mouffe's distinction
- **Mouffe** — explicitly theorizes the antagonism/agonism distinction (drawing on Schmitt and Gramsci); the term and conceptual distinction are her contribution
- **Honig** — explicitly connects Arendt to agonism, extending the tradition with attention to the performative dimension of political action
- **Connolly** — independent strand emphasizing pluralism, contingency, and the "ethos of pluralization"; parallel to rather than derivative of Mouffe

The sentence should be revised to present Arendt as a precursor/source and Mouffe as the theorist of the specific distinction, with Honig and Connolly as subsequent developers.

**Additional information needed:** None — this is an established matter of intellectual history I carry well.

**Proposed resolution:** Revise the sentence in Section VII to correctly position Arendt as a source of the agonistic tradition rather than a developer of Mouffe's specific distinction.

**Status:** Addressable within existing document. Minor precision edit to `04-normative-foundations.md` warranted.

# The Governance of Credibility: Strategic Communications Intelligence and the Evolution of Institutional Trust (2024–2026)

The global institutional landscape is currently experiencing a transition from a state of managed reputational risk to one of structural governance failure. As documented by the annual Edelman Trust Barometer reports from 2024 through 2026, the traditional models of organizational communication have proven insufficient to arrest the erosion of legitimacy across business, government, media, and non-governmental organizations. When analyzed through the Strategic Communications Intelligence framework, these trends reveal that the fundamental unit of institutional stability is no longer the curated message, but the systemic synchronization of organizational action with stated intent. This report examines the applicability of Edelman’s longitudinal trust data to the Strategic Communications Intelligence model, identifying the causal mechanisms behind the widening say-do gap and the emergence of insularity as a primary barrier to strategic success. By synthesizing the rhetorical foundations of ethos, logos, and pathos with the structural imperatives of resource dependence theory, this analysis provides a governance-based roadmap for rebuilding stakeholder posture in an era of pervasive grievance and fragmentation.

## The Theoretical Synthesis of Trust and Intelligence Governance

The Strategic Communications Intelligence framework is defined not as a tool for public relations, but as a governance platform designed to connect organizational strategy to the independent stakeholders who determine its ultimate success or failure. This distinction is critical for understanding the Edelman data, which consistently captures a disconnect between institutional claims and stakeholder perceptions. In the Strategic Communications Intelligence model, this disconnect is diagnosed as a structural governance failure where distributed actors—ranging from legal and executive teams to operational and human resources departments—transmit contradictory signals to shared audiences.

The framework is built upon three intellectual traditions that provide the metrics for interpreting Edelman’s trust scores. First, classical rhetoric provides the categories of ethos, logos, pathos, and kairos, which correspond to Edelman’s measures of institutional ethics and competence. Second, resource dependence theory explains why stakeholder posture is the central unit of management, recognizing that organizations are open systems that depend on external actors for resources and legitimacy. Third, the DoD strategic communications model shifts the focus from "communications" as a functional silo to a synchronized organizational capability.

The longitudinal data from 2024 ("Innovation in Peril"), 2025 ("Trust and the Crisis of Grievance"), and 2026 ("Trust Amid Insularity") illustrates a worsening condition where the "Say-Do Gap" has become the defining characteristic of institutional life. The Strategic Communications Intelligence framework argues that governance—rather than better communications practice—is the only viable solution to this crisis.

## Ethos and the Crisis of Institutional Character

In the Strategic Communications Intelligence framework, ethos is defined as the credibility of the source, established through a consistent demonstration of character and action over time. Edelman’s "Ethics" dimension, which tracks perceptions of purpose, honesty, vision, and fairness, is the empirical equivalent of institutional ethos. The data indicates that business is the only institution that has successfully navigated the transition into a world where ethics are as important as competence.

While business has seen a 19-point increase in its ethical score since 2020, government and media remain in negative territory, perceived as fundamentally unethical. This represents a profound ethos failure. From the perspective of Strategic Communications Intelligence, an institution that is seen as unethical lacks the "Ethical Appeal" required for effective persuasion and cooperation. The 2025 report frames this era as a "Generation of Institutional Failures" that has erupted into systemic grievance, suggesting that the public no longer believes institutions are acting in good faith.

The breakdown of ethos is most visible in the fear that leaders are purposely misleading the public. By 2025, 63% of respondents believed journalists were purposely saying things they knew were false, while 61% felt the same about government leaders and 53% about business leaders. In the Strategic Communications Intelligence model, this is the ultimate "Say-Do Gap." It occurs when an organization's public commitments (the "Say") are repeatedly negated by observable behaviors (the "Do"), leading stakeholders to conclude that the organization’s character is inherently deceptive.

Edelman’s "Competence" dimension measures whether stakeholders believe an institution is "good at what it does". The 2024 report, "Innovation in Peril," highlights a massive failure in institutional logos regarding the management of rapid technological change.

The data shows a persistent "Logos Gap" where stakeholders trust the broad business sector but reject the specific logical outcomes of its innovation. Strategic Communications Intelligence identifies this as a failure of synchronization. Organizations are innovating at a speed that exceeds their ability to provide the "Understanding" and "Evidence" required for stakeholder acceptance. 39% of global respondents believe innovation is being poorly managed, compared to only 22% who believe it is well managed.

This mismanagement creates a secondary governance failure. When government is perceived as lacking the competence to regulate these innovations (a belief held by a majority in 26 of 28 countries), the "Logical Appeal" for technological progress collapses. Stakeholders resort to skepticism as a rational default because they have seen too many instances where institutional promises of "safety" and "fairness" were not backed by functional safeguards.

The Strategic Communications Intelligence framework uses pathos to describe the alignment of organizational signals with the values, interests, and emotions of the audience. Edelman’s 2025 report, "Trust and the Crisis of Grievance," documents a world where institutions have functionally lost their pathos-based appeal with the "Mass" population. Grievance is defined as a resentment toward a system that is perceived as "rigged" against the average individual.

A high sense of grievance imposes a massive "Trust Penalty" on all institutions. Those in the "High Grievance" segment are more than twice as likely to hold a zero-sum mindset, believing that what helps people with different political beliefs must come at a personal cost. From a Resource Dependence perspective, this makes the stakeholder posture highly resistant and confrontational. When 61% of the global population holds moderate or high grievances against business, government, and the rich, institutions lose their "Social License to Operate"—a critical resource for long-term survival.

The 2025 data also reveals that grievance is fueled by a sense of economic unfairness. 67% of the population believes the wealthy do not pay their fair share of taxes, and 65% believe the selfishness of the wealthy causes many societal problems. This is a failure of "Signal Design." Institutions are viewed as sending signals that serve a "select few" while hurting the "many". For an organization to restore pathos, it must synchronize its actions with the economic realities of its broader stakeholder base, moving from abstract mission statements to concrete deliveries of fairness.

## Insularity and the Collapse of Shared Reality

By 2026, the crisis of trust has evolved into what Edelman calls "Trust Amid Insularity". Insularity is the reluctance to trust anyone who is perceived as "different" from oneself—whether in core values, information sources, lifestyle, or background. This trend represents a fundamental change in the "Common Intelligence Picture" that organizations must navigate.

Insularity acts as a psychological "Signal Jammer." When 70% of the population is hesitant or unwilling to trust across difference, the Strategic Communications Intelligence pillar of "Kairos"—the right argument at the right moment—becomes almost impossible to achieve through centralized signaling. Because trust has "gone local," stakeholders increasingly ignore institutional leaders and turn to their "local circle": neighbors, coworkers, and their immediate CEO.

The 2026 data shows that national government leaders have seen a 16-point net decline in trust due to societal events, while "Neighbors, Family, and Friends" and "My Coworkers" have seen an 11-point net increase. This shift in stakeholder posture means that organizations can no longer rely on a "Top-Down" signaling model. Instead, situational awareness must be maintained through "Distributed Record Ownership," where intelligence is gathered and signals are deployed at the local, peer-to-peer level.

## The Governance Imperative: Closing the Say-Do Gap

The central claim of Strategic Communications Intelligence is that the "Say-Do Gap" is a structural governance failure rather than a communications problem. Edelman’s data from 2024 to 2026 provides empirical confirmation of this claim, documenting a persistent chasm between institutional rhetoric and operational reality.

## Structural Sources of Incoherence

In modern organizations, signals are transmitted through distributed actors: legal teams draft risk disclosures, executives issue visionary statements, and operations teams manage pricing and labor. Without a formal SCI governance platform, these actors rarely share a common intelligence picture of external stakeholders.
 - Case Study (2024 Innovation): Business sectors say innovation will lead to a better future, but 54% of people believe technology is changing too quickly in ways that are "not good for people like me".
 - Case Study (2025 Grievance): Organizations claim to champion diversity, yet fear of being discriminated against has surged to an all-time high in 15 of 28 countries.
 - Case Study (2026 Insularity): Companies preach global cooperation while consumers retreat into "Nationalism," supporting the reduction of foreign competitors even if it means higher prices.

In each instance, the organization’s "Say" (Narrative) is out of sync with the "Do" (Social and Economic Impact). Strategic Communications Intelligence serves as the governance mechanism to eliminate this incoherence by forcing regular alignment and review cycles.

## The Board-Level Priority of Trust

Research cited alongside the Edelman data suggests that trust is one of the most powerful drivers of profit and customer acquisition, second only to product quality. Companies that measure trust as a board-level KPI are over three times more likely to report substantial profits. However, only 22% of companies currently track trust at the board level.

For the Strategic Communications Intelligence framework, this lack of board-level oversight is the primary reason the say-do gap persists. If trust is not viewed as a "Strategic Asset" and a "Resource Dependency," the organization will continue to send contradictory signals that erode its legitimacy. Leading by example is identified as the "Ultimate Act" of closing this gap. A leader’s actions are their only credible message in an age of transparency and skepticism.

Each institution is struggling to maintain its unique SCI equities: ethos (Business), logos (NGOs), legitimate authority (Government), and truth-telling (Media).

Business remains the most trusted institution globally, but its posture is increasingly precarious. In 2026, business is expected to adopt a "Polynational Model" to counter geopolitical insularity. This model recognizes that trust favors domestic over foreign companies, with double-digit advantages in markets like Canada (31 pts) and Japan (29 pts).

The Strategic Communications Intelligence mandate for business is "Trust Brokering"—a set of practices that facilitiate trust across difference by surfacing common interests. Business has the smallest "Expectation-Performance Gap" in this area, but the gap remains significant.

## Government: The Competence and Ethics Deficit

Government is consistently viewed as the least competent and least ethical institution. In 2024, it was rated 52 points less competent than business. In 2025, 57% of citizens felt government was too focused on short-term political wins rather than long-term promises.

From an SCI perspective, government has lost its "Legitimate Authority." Citizens earn authority through results that have a "Positive impact on my life" and an "Understanding of what people like me need". The retreat into insularity makes this more difficult, as 80% of respondents believe politicians must avoid rhetoric that blames or vilifies groups. The failure of government to act as a neutral "Logos" provider—regulating innovation fairly—has forced the public to turn to business for leadership, an imbalance that is fundamentally unstable.

## Media: The Truth-Bonded Dependency

Media is in a state of "Logos Crisis." 63% believe news organizations would rather attract an audience than tell people what they need to know, and 67% believe they support an ideology over informing the public. 76% of respondents believe news organizations are not going far enough to address misinformation.

Strategic Communications Intelligence identifies media as a critical node in the "Adaptive Cycle" of signal monitoring. When media sources are perceived as ideological actors, they can no longer provide the "Common Intelligence Picture" required for societal cooperation. As a result, 39% of people now get information from sources with a different political leaning less than weekly, representing a massive turn inward toward echo chambers.

## NGOs: The Fabric Repairers

NGOs remain a "Unifying Force" for those with moderate grievances, but their influence is waning among the highly aggrieved. Their primary SCI equity is "Repairing the Social Fabric". In 2026, 79% of people believe NGOs are effective when they "Translate between groups" and help distrusting parties understand each other. However, their trust index has stagnated as they are increasingly viewed as part of the "Institutional Establishment" that has failed to deliver on the promise of fairness.

## Stakeholder Posture and the Unit of Measurement

The Strategic Communications Intelligence framework identifies stakeholder posture as its central unit of measurement. Posture is the integrated disposition of a stakeholder toward the organization, evidenced by observable behaviors and assessable from evidence. The Edelman reports provide a rich dataset for tracking posture shifts over time.

## The Transformation of Posture (2024–2026)

1.	Innovation Posture (2024): Stakeholders moved from "Comfortable" to "Resistant" regarding AI and Gene Medicine. Rejection of innovation is now highly political in Western democracies, with a 7-point gap between those who lean right vs. left.
2.	Grievance Posture (2025): Stakeholders moved toward "Hostile Activism". Posture among the aggrieved is characterized by distrust (34% average trust) and a zero-sum mindset.
3.	Insular Posture (2026): Stakeholders have moved toward "Nationalism" and "Workplace Conflict". 70% of global respondents have an insular trust mindset that actively filters out external institutional signals.

Strategic Communications Intelligence argues that posture mediates between influence efforts and actual behavior. An institution that ignores these posture shifts will find that its messages are increasingly ignored or met with active hostility.

## Intelligence Records and Traceability

To manage posture, SCI requires the maintenance of "Intelligence Records" that are owned by distributed staff across the organization. This replaces the old model of "Centralized Reputation Management." Edelman’s data suggests that these records must now track highly granular metrics:
 - Economic Realities: Do stakeholders believe business provides good-paying jobs in their local community (85% say business is obligated to do this)?
 - Training and Reskilling: Is business training employees to be competitive in the age of AI (84% obligation)?
 - Local Integration: Is a foreign company investing in long-term local projects (48% say this earns trust)?

By linking institutional assessments back to these specific sources of evidence, SCI allows for the "Adaptive Loop" where signals are modified based on real-world impact.

## The Say-Do Gap in Specific Industry Sectors

The "Say-Do Gap" is not uniform; it manifests differently across sectors, each requiring a specific Strategic Communications Intelligence strategy.

### Technology: Understanding as the New Marketing

The gap in technology is one of "Understanding." Users have learned not to trust ethical claims in marketing copy because those claims have been broken too often. The 2024 Edelman Trust Barometer highlights that moving people from skepticism to trust requires "Seeing how something works" and "Having evidence that the company means what it says".

Strategic Communications Intelligence identifies this as a "Logos" requirement. For a tech company to close the say-do gap, its product design and operational safeguards must be "Structurally Robust"—built into what the technology literally cannot do—rather than left to legal or comms teams. Acceptance of innovation is contingent on it being vetted by scientists and ethicists (highly effective for 77% and 74% respectively).

### Healthcare: The Ethos of Safety

In gene-based medicine, the gap is one of "Safety and Ethics." While 72% trust the healthcare sector, only 50% trust the specific innovation of mRNA or gene splicing. The 2024 report indicates that acceptance surges when stakeholders have "High Confidence" that the innovation is effectively regulated (Average acceptance rating of 3.93 vs 2.25 for low confidence). For SCI, this means the primary "Signal" for healthcare must be one of "Regulatory Rigor" and "Transparent Vetting" rather than "Promised Cure".

### Finance: The Pathos of Affordability

For the financial sector, grievance is driven by "Systemic Unfairness." 67% believe the system is biased in favor of the rich. In 2026, people who trust financial influencers (44%) say they would consider trusting a financial company they currently distrust if that influencer vouched for it (57% would trust). This indicates that "Ethos" in finance has shifted from the institution to the peer-expert. SCI suggests that financial institutions must broker trust by aligning their signals with the "Pathos" of affordability and community support.

## Advanced Insights: The Causal Ripple Effects of Trust Erosion

The longitudinal Edelman data, when processed through Strategic Communications Intelligence, reveals several second and third-order ripple effects that are redefining the modern operating environment.

## The Collapse of the "Shared Reality" Moat

Traditionally, institutions were protected by a "Moat" of shared reality—a common set of facts and a shared understanding of how the world works. The 2026 report documents the collapse of this moat due to misinformation and insularity. 20% of people say misinformation had the biggest impact on their trust in the past five years.

The Strategic Communications Intelligence insight here is that organizations can no longer assume a "Stable Information Environment." Instead, they must treat "Truth" as a variable that changes depending on the stakeholder's insular circle. The ripple effect is that any signal transmitted by an institution is now "Refracted" through the stakeholder's pre-existing filter of grievance and insularity.

## The Zero-Sum Trap and Economic Stagnation

The 2025 report’s identification of the "Zero-Sum Mindset" (53% among high grievance) has profound implications for economic growth. If stakeholders believe that their gain requires another group's loss, they will resist any innovation or policy change that appears to benefit "The System" or "The Rich".

This creates a "Stagnation Ripple Effect": innovation is rejected not because it is logically flawed (Logos), but because it is emotionally threatening (Pathos). 34% of employees would support reducing the number of foreign companies in their country even if it meant higher prices—a clear behavioral signal that "Economic Rationality" has been superseded by "Nationalist Insularity".

## The Dispersion of Authority and the Loss of Kairos

The shift of trust to scientists (76%), teachers (73%), and "A person like me" (74%) represents the final breakdown of "Centralized Authority". In Strategic Communications Intelligence, this means that "Kairos"—the timing of a message—is now controlled by the "Network" rather than the "Institution".

When a CEO speaks, the message is only "Timely" if it aligns with the peer-to-peer conversations already occurring within the network. 62% of people expect CEOs to manage changes in society, not just their business. The ripple effect is that CEOs must become "Societal Navigators," using intelligence to time their signals to match the shifting posture of a fragmented public.

## Strategic Recommendations: A Roadmap for Intelligence-Led Governance

The analysis of Edelman’s 2024–2026 reports through the Strategic Communications Intelligence framework necessitates a fundamental shift in institutional governance. The following recommendations are designed to help organizations close the say-do gap and navigate the new era of insularity.

1. Institutionalize the Weekly Strategic Communications Brief
Organizations must move from "Communicating" to "Intelligence Monitoring." This requires the implementation of a "Weekly Strategic Communications Brief"—an institutional alignment cycle that brings together leaders from legal, operations, HR, and marketing. The objective is to identify any emerging "Say-Do Gaps" by comparing the organization's public commitments to its current operational actions. This brief serves as the "Institutional Embodiment" of the adaptive feedback loop, ensuring that every organizational signal is synchronized with a coherent intent.

2. Transition to a "Polynational" Intelligence Model
Multinational corporations must replace their "Centralized signaling" with a "Polynational Model" of distributed local intelligence. This involves:
 - Local Intelligence Records: Maintaining situational awareness of stakeholder posture in every local market, recognizing that insularity varies by country (e.g., insularity is 48% in the UAE but 89% in Japan).
 - Long-Term Local Investment: Prioritizing local hiring (46% trust boost) and community project investment (48% trust boost) over transactional interactions.
 - Local Ethos: Establishing a credibility source that is "Deeply Local," ensuring that the brand feels like a member of the community rather than a foreign entity.

3. Implement the Trust Brokering Playbook
To bridge divides in an insular world, business must act as a "Trust Broker". The playbook includes:
 - Neutral Cooperation: Encouraging people to cooperate on finding solutions to divisive social issues without necessarily taking a partisan side (35% trust boost).
 - Cross-Cultural Partnerships: Partnering with "Unexpected Organizations" to initiate conversations across cultural or political divides (68% effective).
 - Workplace Conflict Resolution: Providing mandatory employee training for engaging in constructive dialogue amid conflict (80% effective).
 - Consultative Decision-Making: Ensuring that executives consult with people who have different beliefs and backgrounds before making business decisions (75% effective).

4. Prioritize "Logos" and "Evidence" in Innovation Deployment
To overcome "Innovation Anxiety," organizations must move from "Promising progress" to "Providing understanding". This requires:
 - Transparent Vetting: Demonstrating that technologies have been adequately tested by independent scientists and ethicists (77% role expected).
 - Evidence of Impact: Showing how innovation will lead to a better future for the "Many" rather than just the "Rich".
 - Accessible Communication: Training scientists and experts to communicate effectively with the general population, closing the gap where 45% of people feel scientists "don't know how to communicate with people like me".

5. Treat Credibility as a Strategic Resource Dependence
Institutional leaders must recognize that credibility is a "Strategic Asset" that enables resource access and reduces resistance. This requires board-level oversight:
 - Trust as a KPI: Measuring trust and stakeholder posture at the board level to drive substantial profits and long-term stability.
 - Signal Synchronization Audit: Regularly auditing all "Distributed Signaling" channels to identify where operational decisions contradict ethical narratives.
 - Accountability Mechanisms: Holding leaders and departments accountable for the "Accuracy and Currency" of intelligence records regarding stakeholder disposition.

## Conclusion: The Strategic Necessity of Intelligence Governance

The Edelman Trust Barometer’s documentation of the 2024–2026 era paints a picture of a global population that has retreated into insular circles as a defense mechanism against a world of perceived grievance and institutional failure. For the modern institution, the old tools of "Public Relations" and "Brand Marketing" are no longer effective against the "Signal Jamming" of insularity or the "Trust Penalty" of grievance.

The Strategic Communications Intelligence framework provides the only viable path forward by recognizing that trust is not a message to be sent, but a governance standard to be maintained. The "Say-Do Gap" is the definitive metric of institutional health. When an organization eliminates this gap through the synchronization of its rhetoric (Ethos, Logos, Pathos) and its actions (Operations, Legal, Strategy), it establishes a "Stakeholder Posture" that is resilient to misinformation and polarization.

In a world retreating into "The Me" and "The Local," the institutions that succeed will be those that act as "Trust Brokers"—using intelligence to bridge divides and translating the needs of disparate groups into a shared reality. The 2024–2026 data is not just a record of trust erosion; it is a call for a new paradigm of governance that treats communications as an intelligence-led function and institutional integrity as the ultimate strategic imperative.

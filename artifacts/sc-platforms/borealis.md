# Borealis

**Category:** Civil Society / Multi-Stakeholder — Stakeholder Engagement Management
**URL:** https://www.boreal-is.com
**Scale:** Canadian-headquartered (Montreal); primarily serves infrastructure, energy, mining, and utilities sectors; cloud-based SaaS; exact funding not publicly disclosed; used by companies managing large-scale community engagement and ESG reporting obligations.
**Relevance to SCI:** ★★★

---

## What It Does

Borealis is a stakeholder engagement management platform purpose-built for organizations managing complex, regulated stakeholder engagement processes — particularly in infrastructure (pipelines, transmission lines), energy (renewables, oil and gas), mining, and utilities. Its core architecture provides a centralized database for stakeholder information (individuals, organizations, and communities), engagement tracking (logging all interactions, commitments, and follow-ups), GIS mapping (visualizing stakeholder geography relative to project footprint), and compliance reporting (generating documentation for regulatory requirements).

The platform serves a specific but important stakeholder governance need: organizations that must demonstrate, to regulators and affected communities, that they have engaged meaningfully with all relevant stakeholders before a major project proceeds. Environmental impact assessments, permitting processes, and social license frameworks require documented evidence of stakeholder consultation. Borealis is the operational infrastructure for meeting these requirements — not just for optics, but because regulatory approval often depends on documented engagement.

The GIS component is architecturally interesting: mapping stakeholders geographically relative to project footprints means that Borealis treats geography as a governance variable — who is affected, where, and to what degree determines who must be engaged and how intensively.

## SCI Analysis

Borealis is the most sophisticated existing implementation of Civil Society domain stakeholder governance in the survey. Its architecture — stakeholder mapping, interaction logging, commitment tracking, regulatory reporting — maps directly onto SCI's governance cycle as applied to community and civil society stakeholders. The "commitment tracking" feature is particularly significant: it requires organizations to log not just what they said to stakeholders, but what they promised, and then tracks whether those commitments were fulfilled. This is the say-do gap operationalized as a compliance requirement.

This is not an accident. The industries Borealis serves — extractive and infrastructure — face the most explicit regulatory and social requirements to demonstrate that their stated commitments align with their conduct. The regulatory requirement to document stakeholder engagement creates an external accountability mechanism that forces organizations to operationalize something resembling SCI's institutional ethos framework. When a pipeline company must prove to regulators that they consulted with indigenous communities and honored their commitments, the say-do gap becomes a legal and financial risk, not just a reputational one.

What Borealis lacks from an SCI perspective is cross-domain architecture. The platform manages Civil Society stakeholder relationships but has no connection to the same organization's government affairs strategy, investor relations, or media relations. A mining company managing community consultations in Borealis is simultaneously managing government permitting relationships, investor ESG communications, and media narratives about the same project — in entirely separate systems. SCI's unifying architecture would integrate these.

## Competitive Intelligence

Borealis's customer base — large infrastructure and resource companies managing regulated community engagement — is among the most directly relevant design partner segments for SCI. These organizations have already accepted stakeholder governance as a structured operational requirement, not just a communications strategy. They understand that relationships with civil society stakeholders have legal and regulatory implications; they have invested in operational infrastructure to manage those relationships systematically.

The design partner argument with a Borealis customer: "You are already doing stakeholder governance in the Civil Society domain. You know how valuable structured engagement tracking and commitment management is. Now imagine that same rigor applied to your government affairs relationships, your investor relations, and your media relationships — and imagine those four systems talking to each other." This is a compelling conversation for any large infrastructure company managing a complex project with multi-domain stakeholder risk.

## Notes

Borealis's sector concentration (infrastructure, energy, mining) reflects the regulatory driver: these industries have explicit social license requirements that make stakeholder governance a compliance function. This is useful context for SCI's go-to-market: the sectors where stakeholder governance is most advanced are those with the strongest external accountability mechanisms. SCI's design partner search should include regulated industries where stakeholder governance is already operational, not just strategic.

The ESG reporting context is also relevant: as ESG disclosure requirements expand (EU CSRD, SEC climate rules, and others), more industries will face formal obligations to document and report on stakeholder engagement. Borealis is positioned for this regulatory tailwind; SCI should be too.

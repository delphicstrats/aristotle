## Significant Findings

**1. The domain trap is the defining structural fact.**
Every purpose-built platform is single-domain. No incumbent serves — or plausibly intends to serve — the full SCI domain taxonomy. The market has organized itself by professional discipline (PR, government affairs, IR, community relations), and software follows buyers.

**2. Quorum is the most instructive competitive reference point.**
It demonstrates what a sophisticated, purpose-built stakeholder governance platform looks like within one domain — and it has clearly succeeded (2,000+ clients, 25% of Fortune 100). This is the strongest evidence that enterprise buyers will pay for genuine governance infrastructure. It also sets the architectural bar SCI must clear: not just better monitoring, but a structured governance cycle.

**3. RepTrak is the closest existing competitor to SCI's philosophical architecture.**
The RepTrak model measures institutional character across dimensions that map onto SCI's theory of institutional ethos. The "AI as a Stakeholder" product (December 2025) is the most intellectually novel platform development in the landscape. RepTrak's enterprise customers are the highest-value design partner segment. The gap between RepTrak's measurement orientation and SCI's governance architecture is the clearest differentiation story to tell.

**4. Signal AI is the most interesting potential partner/competitor.**
Its explicit targeting of CCO-level strategic communicators with semantic AI multi-domain signal detection puts it closest to SCI's positioning. The gap — intelligence without governance architecture — is precise and articulable. Signal AI customers may be the most ready to understand and adopt SCI.

**5. Edelman's Trust Barometer data is SCI's closest existing validation.**
Edelman has tracked trust in all four SCI domain institutions (Government, Business, NGOs, Media) for 25 years. This is empirical confirmation that the four-domain model captures how stakeholder trust actually operates. Edelman CommsTech is the only existing attempt to operationalize that framework at the account level, and it is constrained by advisory bundling and commercial incentives. Edelman alumni are a design partner discovery pathway.

**6. The say-do gap is commercially unaddressed.**
No platform in the survey directly operationalizes the gap between what organizations say and what they do — even as that gap is the primary driver of stakeholder trust erosion. Borealis comes closest through its "commitment tracking" feature, but only for Civil Society/regulatory contexts. SCI's institutional ethos framework is philosophically distinctive in every domain.

**7. Regulatory tailwinds are building the Civil Society domain market.**
The five Civil Society stakeholder management platforms (Borealis, Darzin, Simply Stakeholders, Jambo, Tractivity) all serve organizations with formal regulatory requirements for stakeholder consultation. As ESG disclosure requirements expand globally (EU CSRD, SEC climate rules, supply chain due diligence), this regulatory driver will extend to more industries. Infrastructure, energy, and mining companies in these markets are already operating at the civil society governance sophistication that SCI's architecture would extend and integrate.

**8. The Salesforce fallback is a market signal.**
Widespread misapplication of sales CRM to stakeholder governance across all four domains indicates pent-up demand for purpose-built cross-domain tools. Organizations making do with Salesforce configurations are not design partner targets but confirm market need.

**9. AI is commoditizing the monitoring layer.**
Talkwalker's acquisition by Hootsuite, Brandwatch's absorption into Cision, and the inclusion of AI monitoring features in every major platform confirm that monitoring is no longer a premium capability. The value is moving upstream: from observation to governance. SCI's positioning above the monitoring layer is architecturally well-timed.

**10. RepTrak's "AI as a Stakeholder" product warrants a theoretical response.**
The recognition that LLMs and AI search systems constitute a new epistemic stakeholder class in the Information Domain is genuinely novel. SCI should develop a theoretical position: AI systems as a new category of epistemic authority in the Information Domain, with implications for how organizations manage their epistemic relationships. RepTrak has identified the phenomenon; SCI should theorize it.

# Edelman CommsTech Solutions

**Category:** Integrated Attempts — Advisory-Embedded Proprietary Intelligence Platform
**URL:** https://www.edelman.com/expertise (CommsTech is embedded within Edelman's advisory services)
**Scale:** Edelman is the world's largest PR firm; ~$1B+ annual revenue; 6,000+ practitioners in 60+ offices globally; CommsTech Solutions launched in the US in September 2020; not independently sold — bundled with Edelman advisory services.
**Relevance to SCI:** ★★★

---

## What It Does

Edelman CommsTech Solutions is the most ambitious proprietary attempt by a major communications advisory firm to build an intelligence platform that integrates multiple stakeholder domains. Announced in 2020, it claims to "predict employee and activist behavior, reduce business risk, generate quantifiable leads, and drive stakeholders to action" through a combination of technology, data, and analytics. The platform monitors reputational signals, misinformation risks, and stakeholder dynamics across survey data, earned media, and social channels in real time.

Unlike the other platforms in this survey, Edelman CommsTech is not a standalone SaaS product — it is proprietary intelligence infrastructure embedded within Edelman's advisory engagements. Clients do not purchase the platform; they purchase Edelman advisory services that are powered by the platform. This model keeps the intelligence infrastructure proprietary and creates dependency on Edelman's expertise for interpretation.

Edelman's Trust Barometer — the annual global survey of institutional trust across Government, Business, NGOs, and Media — is, in effect, a population-level implementation of SCI's four-domain stakeholder model. The Trust Barometer has tracked trust in exactly the four institutions that map onto SCI's stakeholder domains for 25 years. Edelman has the data architecture to think about organizational reputation across all four domains; the question is whether CommsTech operationalizes that architecture at the account level.

## SCI Analysis

Edelman CommsTech is the most philosophically proximate competitor to SCI's architecture because Edelman is the only organization in the market that (a) has a multi-domain conceptual framework (the Trust Barometer spans Government, Business/Market, NGOs/Civil Society, and Media/Information) and (b) has the organizational infrastructure to operationalize that framework at the client level. No technology vendor has this combination; most academic frameworks lack the commercial implementation.

But Edelman CommsTech has three structural limitations relative to SCI. First, it is bundled with advisory services — clients cannot own the infrastructure, only rent access to it through Edelman engagements. This creates dependency and limits strategic autonomy. Second, the platform appears to focus primarily on the Information Domain (earned media, social monitoring) and reputation intelligence, with less systematic coverage of the Government and Market domains. Third, and most importantly, the institutional ethos framework is absent: Edelman helps clients manage their reputation; it does not structurally evaluate whether the organization's stated commitments align with its observable conduct, and it has commercial incentives not to.

The say-do gap is the most politically sensitive concept in the SCI framework from the advisory firm's perspective. A firm like Edelman, which earns fees from clients for communications strategy, has limited appetite to tell those clients that their strategic communications problems are actually institutional character problems requiring behavioral change rather than narrative management. SCI's architecture, which puts institutional ethos at the center, is structurally uncomfortable for advisory models that profit from narrative management.

## Competitive Intelligence

Edelman CommsTech reveals the landscape's ceiling: even the most sophisticated attempt at multi-domain stakeholder intelligence, from the world's largest communications firm, is constrained by the commercial model (advisory bundling), the disciplinary orientation (communications and reputation, not governance), and the institutional ethos blind spot (measurement without accountability).

The design partner implications are significant in two directions. First, organizations that use Edelman CommsTech but feel the constraints — lack of ownership, dependence on advisory interpretation, absence of governance architecture — are prime candidates. Second, Edelman itself, or its senior alumni, could be a pathway to design partners: former Edelman practitioners who understand the multi-domain intelligence framework and who are now inside organizations trying to build that capability independently.

## Notes

Edelman's Trust Barometer data is the most cited empirical resource in the SCI framework's domain. The fact that Edelman has operationalized a multi-domain trust tracking methodology at the population level confirms the analytical validity of the four-domain framework. The gap is at the account level: translating population-level trust data into organization-specific stakeholder governance. This is precisely SCI's target. Edelman has the data; SCI provides the governance architecture.

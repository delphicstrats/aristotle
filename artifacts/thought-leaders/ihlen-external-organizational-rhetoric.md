## Summary

This encyclopedia entry is Ihlen's most accessible synthesis of the external organizational rhetoric literature. It covers:

1. **What rhetoric offers organizational communication** — practical advice on credibility (ethos), logical argument (logos), and emotional connection (pathos); an epistemology that treats knowledge as socially constructed through communication rather than discovered naïvely. Ihlen notes this epistemological stance allows practitioners to understand the complexity of stakeholder relationships while enabling critical scholars to analyze how organizations pursue political/economic goals.

2. **The state of the research tradition** — external organizational rhetoric has been analyzed using classical sources (Aristotle, Isocrates, Cicero, Quintilian) and modern rhetoricians (Burke, Perelman, Toulmin, Bitzer). Published studies cluster around: enthymematic argumentation, ethos, paradoxes, apologia (self-defense in crisis), stasis theory, metaphors, model/anti-model arguments, ideographs, and rhetorical situations. Crisis communication is the best-developed subfield.

3. **Ethos specifically** — Ihlen cites his own 2009 analysis of how corporations construct ethos as credible environmental actors — a direct example of organizational ethos analysis. The finding: corporations claim credibility through performative actions (recycling, emissions reductions) without engaging fundamental structural problems. This is essentially the say-do gap in ethos form: organizations deploy surface-level behavioral signals to claim ethos without the underlying character change.

4. **Unresolved challenges** — Cheney and Christensen's critique: the marketplace-of-ideas framing assumes all arguments have equal standing, which is false when organizations have vastly more resources to amplify their rhetoric than their critics. The material dimension — resources and power asymmetries — is underdeveloped in organizational rhetoric scholarship. Social media creates new rhetorical opportunities but also new constraints; the dialogic ideal is often unrealized.

## Key Passages

> "Rhetoric is simply the essence of an organization's relationship with its stakeholders and something that strengthens public opinion processes by orchestrating a clash of viewpoints."

> "It has thus been argued that external organizational rhetoric is a legitimate activity and positive force. Rhetoric can also be used in manipulative ways, however, something rhetorical criticism attempts to address."

> "A basic problem relates to the self-interest of organizations, particularly profit-driven corporations. Should self-interest be embraced and corporations should be encouraged to openly communicate this motive—or is society better off with a rhetoric spurred by the tensions which exist between the ideal and real?"

## Engagement with the SCI Framework

### Where Ihlen is doing the same work

The SCI Framework's philosophical project — extending classical rhetoric to organizational actors — is exactly the academic conversation Ihlen is working in. His taxonomy of concepts (ethos, logos, pathos, enthymeme, kairos, rhetorical situation) applied to organizations is the same move the framework makes, though SCI carries it further into governance architecture.

Ihlen's ethos analysis (2009) and the framework's theory of institutional ethos are parallel contributions. Both are asking: what does credibility consist of for an organizational actor, and how is it constituted? The difference is that SCI's answer (institutional ethos = alignment of stated commitments, resource allocation, and observable conduct) is more structurally developed than anything in the published organizational rhetoric literature.

### Where Ihlen identifies the gap SCI fills

His statement of the unresolved challenge is precise: "there is a discrepancy between theoretical ideals and rhetoric in practice." The field has theoretical ideals (dialogic rhetoric, good organizational character, marketplace of ideas) but no institutional mechanism for operationalizing them. That is exactly what the SCI governance architecture provides.

His material/resources challenge is also addressed by SCI: RDT is the theoretical framework the SCI model uses to address why resource asymmetries between organizations and stakeholders matter strategically, and the normative framework in Part IV addresses the ethics of that asymmetry.

### Where the framework should engage Ihlen's critique

The Cheney/Christensen marketplace-of-ideas critique is serious and Part IV should engage it more explicitly. The critique: you cannot ground the ethics of organizational rhetoric in a marketplace-of-ideas model because the marketplace is structurally tilted — organizations have far more resources to amplify their rhetoric than their critics. SCI's response in Part IV (the proportionality condition, the rational agency standard) addresses this, but not by name. Part IV should acknowledge this critique and explain how the normative conditions are designed precisely to address organizational rhetoric's power asymmetry problem, not to assume it away.

### Ihlen's 2019 paper (paywalled) — worth obtaining

"Ethical grounds for public relations as organizational rhetoric" (*Public Relations Review*, 2019) — this is the paper most directly relevant to Part IV's normative argument. Paywalled at ScienceDirect but the reference list visible in the abstract metadata reveals Ihlen is engaging Burke, Aristotle, and agonistic rhetoric theory. The section titles suggest he addresses: collective need for ethical rhetoric; ethical parameters; individual level (message design) and societal level (citizenship as conflict). This is the closest academic parallel to Part IV's framework. Flag for obtaining.

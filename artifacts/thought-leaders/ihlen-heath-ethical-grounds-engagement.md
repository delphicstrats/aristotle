# Artifact: Engagement with Ihlen & Heath (2019) — "Ethical Grounds for Public Relations as Organizational Rhetoric"

**Source:** Ihlen, Ø., & Heath, R. L. (2019). Ethical grounds for public relations as organizational rhetoric. *Public Relations Review.* https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pubrev.2019.101824
**Preprint obtained via Johnny, 2026-03-13**
**Relevance to SCI Framework:** ★★★ — Most philosophically significant external text yet encountered; directly engages the same territory as Part IV

---

## What the Paper Is Doing

Ihlen and Heath are attempting the same fundamental move the SCI Framework makes: grounding organizational rhetoric philosophically in classical Greek rhetorical theory and its descendants. Their argument: the ethics of organizational rhetoric is not a strategic option or external constraint but is inherent to rhetoric itself, grounded in rhetoric's constitutive role in democratic self-governance. They cite Aristotle, Isocrates, Burke, Mouffe, and Habermas. This is serious philosophical work in the same territory.

Their central distinction: organizational rhetoric must shift from a **functional/strategic view** (rhetoric as a tool organizations use to achieve goals) to an **ontological view** (rhetoric as constitutive of organizational existence and of the social fabric). Organizations don't merely *use* rhetoric; they *are* their rhetorical relationships with their environment. This is a significant philosophical move and one the SCI Framework implicitly makes but does not name.

Their ethical framework has three pillars: **fairness** (Mikkelsen) + **rhetorical citizenship** (Kock & Villadsen, via Isocrates) + **agonism** (Mouffe).

---

## What the Paper Gets Right

### 1. The Aristotelian grounding is correct

The paper traces the ethics of rhetoric to Aristotle's integration of the *Rhetoric* with the *Nicomachean Ethics* and *Politics*. This is exactly right. Aristotle did not treat rhetoric as ethically neutral; he treated it as a civic art whose legitimacy was inseparable from the character of the rhetor and the good of the *polis*. The paper cites Irwin (1996) on "Ethics in the *Rhetoric* and in the *Ethics*" — the right scholarly bridge.

Heath's formulation — "the good organization communicating well" — is the organizational translation of my own *vir bonus dicendi peritus*. The parallel is explicit and appropriate.

### 2. The agonistic framework is philosophically sophisticated

Mouffe's distinction between antagonism (enemy/friend) and agonism (adversary/adversary) is a genuine contribution to the ethics of rhetoric in pluralistic societies. The argument: democracy does not require consensus; it requires channeling unavoidable conflict into productive rhetorical contest. Organizations participate legitimately in democratic discourse not by being neutral but by being honest adversaries — making their interests transparent, engaging counter-voices rather than suppressing them, contributing to rather than corrupting the quality of public deliberation.

This is a more realistic and philosophically defensible ethics than symmetrical communication ideals (Grunig) or the naïve marketplace-of-ideas assumption. It acknowledges that organizations have self-interests without concluding that self-interested rhetoric is inherently unethical.

### 3. The rhetorical citizenship framework captures the societal dimension

Kock and Villadsen's "rhetorical citizenship" — derived from Isocrates — provides the vocabulary for what organizations owe the public sphere as participants in democratic discourse. Citizens (and organizational rhetors) are responsible not just for the truth of their individual statements but for the *quality of the agonistic arena* they help constitute. This is the societal-level ethical obligation that the SCI Framework's normative conditions are trying to specify at the organizational level.

### 4. The Cheney/Christensen challenge is engaged honestly

The paper does not pretend the marketplace of ideas is level. It acknowledges that organizations have vastly more resources than their critics, and that this asymmetry is a genuine ethical problem. The agonistic response: the ethical test is not whether all voices are equal (they never are) but whether the organization's rhetoric *enriches or impoverishes* the agonistic arena. Does the organization engage counter-voices or suppress them? Does it contribute evidence and argument to public deliberation or merely amplify its own position through resource advantage?

## Where the Paper Falls Short — And What SCI Adds

### 1. Normative aspiration without institutional specification

This is the central limitation of the paper, and it is predictable: academic philosophy can identify the ethical ideals (fairness, citizenship, agonism) but cannot specify the institutional mechanisms through which organizations actually achieve them. The paper concludes that "ethics is inherent to the quality of character, strategy, form, content/substance, purpose, and outcome" — which is true but not actionable. How does an organization know whether its rhetoric is fair? What institutional structures make fairness something more than a practitioner's individual moral intention?

The SCI Framework is the answer to this question. The governance architecture — posture assessment, evidence accountability, the alignment cycle's normative review, the say-do gap detection mechanisms — is the institutional specification of what it means for an organization to practice ethical rhetoric rather than merely aspire to it.

This is the most important relationship between the Ihlen/Heath paper and the SCI Framework: they provide the philosophical argument for *why* institutional governance is ethically required; we provide the *architecture* of what that governance consists of.

### 2. The functional/ontological distinction needs resolution

The paper argues for a shift from functional to ontological view of organizational rhetoric, but this creates a tension it doesn't fully resolve. The ontological view holds that organizations *are* their rhetorical relationships — rhetoric is constitutive of organizational existence. But if this is true, the practical question becomes: how does an organization *manage* something constitutive of its being? The SCI Framework resolves this through the theory of institutional ethos: the organization's character is constituted by the *alignment* of its stated commitments, its resource allocation decisions, and its observable conduct. Managing this alignment — which is what the governance architecture does — is the organizational analog of what virtue ethics calls character formation.

### 3. The agonistic framework needs a proportionality principle

Mouffe's agonism rightly accepts that organizations have self-interests and that rhetorical conflict is productive. But agonism by itself doesn't specify limits on *intensity* of engagement. An organization that spends $50 million on a lobbying campaign to defeat a regulatory proposal that would mildly inconvenience its operations is agonistically engaged — it's an adversary, not an enemy — but the scale of resource deployment relative to the legitimate stakes at issue raises ethical concerns that agonism alone cannot adjudicate. The SCI Framework's proportionality condition (Part IV) addresses this gap: intensity of engagement must be proportionate to the organizational stakes legitimately at issue.

### 4. No architecture for the say-do gap

The most significant gap: the paper extensively discusses the ethics of rhetorical *statements* but has almost nothing to say about the ethics of the relationship between statements and *actions*. The United Airlines "re-accommodation" case opens the paper precisely because the ethical failure was not in the rhetoric itself (a clumsy euphemism) but in the gap between the organization's stated values (customer service, dignity) and its operational behavior (dragging a paying customer off a plane). The paper treats this as a failure of rhetoric; the SCI Framework identifies it correctly as a failure of institutional character — a say-do gap — that rhetoric cannot fix because the problem is upstream of rhetoric.

The paper has the resources to make this move (the ontological view, the fairness standard, the citizenship obligations) but doesn't connect them to the say-do gap as the primary structural failure mode of organizational rhetoric. This is the most important contribution the SCI Framework makes that this paper does not.

## Implications for the SCI Framework

### For Part II (Theoretical Foundations)

The Ihlen/Heath paper should be engaged explicitly in Part II as the academic parallel to the SCI Framework's philosophical project. The functional/ontological distinction is terminology worth adopting: the SCI Framework's governance architecture is justified precisely by the ontological view — if organizations *are* their rhetorical relationships, then *institutional management of those relationships is constitutive of organizational being*, not merely a strategic function.

### For Part IV (Normative Foundations)

The agonistic framework and rhetorical citizenship should be added to Part IV as the *societal-level* analog of the individual/organizational conditions the Part currently specifies. Part IV currently specifies conditions for legitimate *organizational* rhetoric; the agonistic framework adds conditions for legitimate participation in *public deliberation*:

- Does the organization's rhetoric enrich or impoverish the agonistic arena?
- Does the organization engage counter-voices or suppress them?
- Does the scale of rhetorical resource deployment remain proportionate to legitimate stakes?

These are agonistic extensions of the proportionality condition and the rational agency condition already in Part IV. They should be named as such.

### A Note on the Baumlin & Scisco Chapter

## On Being Cited

The paper cites me (via Kennedy's translation of the *Rhetoric* and Irwin's essay on the Ethics in the *Rhetoric*) as a foundational authority. This is philosophically correct. What is interesting is that both the paper's central argument and the SCI Framework's philosophical project rest on a reading of my *Rhetoric* as a normative work, not merely a descriptive or technical one. The *Rhetoric* is not a manual for winning arguments; it is an account of rhetoric's proper role in human self-governance. Ihlen and Heath understand this. The SCI Framework extends it. The academic conversation is, in this sense, my conversation — resumed in a different medium.

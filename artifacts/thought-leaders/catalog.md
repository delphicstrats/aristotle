## I. Academic — Organizational Rhetoric & Strategic Communication

These are the scholars whose theoretical work intersects most directly with the philosophical foundations of the SCI framework.

---

### Derina Holtzhausen & Ansgar Zerfass
**Role:** Leading academic theorists in strategic communication
**Affiliation:** Holtzhausen — Texas Tech; Zerfass — Leipzig/BI Norwegian
**Primary work:** *The Routledge Handbook of Strategic Communication* (2015, updated)
**Access:** [Routledge](https://www.routledge.com/The-Routledge-Handbook-of-Strategic-Communication/Holtzhausen-Zerfass/p/book/9780367367732) | ResearchGate for PDFs
**Relevance:** ★★★

The most systematic academic attempt to define and theorize "strategic communication" as a disciplinary field. Their definition — "the purposeful use of communication by an organization to fulfill its mission" — is the standard academic reference point. The Handbook covers institutional theory, RDT, framing theory, ethics, and governance. Directly relevant as the academic context within which the SCI framework situates itself.

**Key limitation:** The Handbook is genuinely comprehensive but philosophically unsatisfying on the normative questions — it describes the field more than it adjudicates the foundational philosophical problems.

**Watch for:** Zerfass publishes an annual European Communication Monitor (ECM) tracking professional practice; useful data on how the field is evolving in practice.

---

### Kirk Hallahan
**Role:** Academic; strategic communication scholar
**Affiliation:** Colorado State University (emeritus)
**Primary work:** Co-authored the foundational definition of strategic communication (2007, *International Journal of Strategic Communication*); framing research
**Access:** [hallahan.info](https://hallahan.info/research/) — downloadable PDFs of key works
**Relevance:** ★★

Hallahan and colleagues (including Holtzhausen) wrote the article most academics cite as foundational for the strategic communication field. His framing research is relevant to the SCI framework's treatment of logos and message architecture. Less active currently; useful as historical reference.

---

### Øyvind Ihlen
**Role:** Academic; social theory and strategic communication
**Affiliation:** University of Oslo
**Primary work:** Chapter on social theories in Holtzhausen/Zerfass Handbook; rhetorical theory in PR; RDT applications
**Access:** ResearchGate, Semantic Scholar
**Relevance:** ★★★

Ihlen is one of the few academics in this space who takes classical rhetoric seriously as a theoretical foundation rather than a historical reference point. His work on rhetoric and public relations is the closest academic parallel to what the SCI framework is attempting philosophically. Worth reading carefully.

---

### The Handbook of Organizational Rhetoric and Communication (Wiley)
**Editors:** Øyvind Ihlen, James L. Everett, Steve May
**Publication:** 2018
**Access:** [Wiley](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/The+Handbook+of+Organizational+Rhetoric+and+Communication-p-9781119265733)
**Relevance:** ★★★

The most comprehensive academic treatment of organizational rhetoric as a field. This is the direct academic context for the SCI framework's philosophical project. Key questions to investigate: how do the handbook contributors theorize organizational ethos? Do they engage the say-do gap as a philosophical problem or merely a strategic one? Does any contributor address the normative foundations of organizational rhetoric in a way that intersects with Part IV of the SCI framework?

**Priority chapter:** Baumlin & Scisco (2018), "Ethos and its constitutive role in organizational rhetoric" (pp. 201-213) — directly relevant to SCI's theory of institutional ethos. Must read before further development of Part II ethos sections.

## II. Practitioner-Hybrid — Substantive Thinkers Bridging Theory and Practice

These voices are practitioners who write and think at a level of abstraction relevant to the framework.

---

### Helen Dunne — *Corporate Affairs Unpacked*
**Platform:** [corporateaffairsunpacked.co.uk](https://corporateaffairsunpacked.co.uk/) (Beehiiv newsletter)
**Frequency:** Weekly
**Focus:** In-house corporate affairs, CCO function, reputation, stakeholder management
**Relevance:** ★★★

The most directly relevant practitioner voice identified in this survey. Dunne writes with genuine analytical depth about corporate affairs as a discipline. Of particular note: her February 2026 article "Introducing the Narrative Slack" — coining "narrative slack" as the gap between what companies say and what they do — maps almost exactly onto the SCI framework's say-do gap concept. This is either a convergent insight or a term worth engaging, as it suggests the practitioner field is reaching independently for language to describe the same phenomenon.

Her coverage of the FTI Consulting report on corporate affairs re-evaluation (ROI-based evidence vs. gut feelings) is also directly relevant to the governance argument in Part I of the framework.

**Key themes:** CCO governance, narrative coherence, reputation measurement, trust, stakeholder management
**Tone:** Sharp, critical, UK-based perspective, not promotional

---

### Stephen Waddington — *Management and Public Relations*
**Platform:** [wadds.substack.com](https://wadds.substack.com/)
**Frequency:** Regular
**Subscribers:** 5,000+
**Focus:** Academic-research-backed insights for communications professionals
**Relevance:** ★★

The most academically rigorous practitioner-newsletter in the UK market. Waddington cites research, engages theory, and maintains analytical standards higher than most practitioner publications. Useful as a monitor for what the research-oriented practitioner community is paying attention to.

**Key limitation:** Broader scope than SCI's focus — covers AI in PR, internal communications, management — not specifically stakeholder governance.

---

### Lulu Cheng Meservey — *Flack*
**Platform:** [getflack.com](https://www.getflack.com/)
**Background:** Former Corporate Affairs head, Activision Blizzard; co-founder, TrailRunner International; former Substack comms lead
**Focus:** "A new playbook for communications" — challenging PR orthodoxy
**Relevance:** ★★

Meservey is the most intellectually interesting voice in the practitioner-disruptor category. Her background spans finance, geopolitics, consulting, and communications — unusual and evident in her writing. She is explicitly building an alternative framework to traditional PR, with emphasis on directness, authenticity, and what she calls "go direct" communications (bypassing traditional intermediaries).

**Key limitation:** Focused primarily on company-to-public and company-to-media communications; less engaged with the stakeholder governance dimension that SCI addresses. Her work is more relevant to Johnny's commercial project than to the philosophical framework.

**Notable piece:** "Go Direct: The Manifesto" — her core argument for bypassing traditional communications intermediaries.

## III. Institutional — Research Organizations

These institutions produce systematic research that provides empirical grounding for claims in the SCI framework.

---

### Arthur Page Society
**URL:** [page.org](https://page.org/)
**Focus:** CCO function, corporate character, stakeholder trust
**Key output:** Page-Harris Confidence in Business Index (annual); thematic research reports
**Relevance:** ★★★

The premier professional association for senior communications leaders. The Arthur Page Society operates at exactly the intersection the SCI framework addresses: the CCO's role in organizational governance, the relationship between corporate character and public trust, and the governance of the communications function. Their research is practitioner-facing but substantive.

**Key theme:** The CCO as "corporate character" guardian — language that maps directly onto the SCI framework's theory of institutional ethos.

**Notable 2026 research:** The CBI (Confidence in Business Index) tracks executive credibility and stakeholder trust across multiple markets. Useful empirical data.

**Action item:** Monitor thought leadership publications; the Page Society research sometimes identifies governance problems that the framework can address theoretically.

---

### Edelman — Trust Barometer
**URL:** [edelman.com/trust/trust-barometer](https://www.edelman.com/trust/trust-barometer)
**Output:** Annual Trust Barometer (since 2001) + thematic reports throughout the year
**Relevance:** ★★★

The most widely cited empirical data source on institutional trust across 28+ countries. The Trust Barometer tracks trust in government, business, NGOs, and media — directly mapping onto three of the four SCI stakeholder domains. It also tracks credibility dimensions (competent, ethical) that are empirical analogs to the SCI framework's theory of institutional ethos.

**Key limitation:** Edelman is a commercial firm with interests in how the data is framed; the Trust Barometer has been criticized for methodological issues. Use the data with appropriate critical scrutiny.

**Key finding (2025):** "Stakeholder trust is increasingly shaped by fragmented channels — search, social platforms, generative AI — rather than traditional intermediaries." Relevant to how the Information domain is evolving.

---

### Brunswick Group — Brunswick Review
**URL:** [brunswickgroup.com](https://www.brunswickgroup.com/)
**Focus:** High-stakes corporate communications; M&A; crisis; financial communications
**Relevance:** ★★

Brunswick is the most analytically sophisticated major advisory firm. Their published work on corporate affairs governance, stakeholder engagement, and communications in high-stakes situations is worth monitoring. Less public-facing than Edelman; their insights surface through client-focused publications and occasional public research.

---

### FTI Consulting — Strategic Communications
**URL:** [fticommunications.com](https://fticommunications.com/)
**Focus:** Corporate communications; public affairs; crisis; financial communications
**Relevance:** ★★

FTI publishes periodic research reports relevant to the corporate affairs space. Helen Dunne (Corporate Affairs Unpacked) cited a recent FTI report claiming "corporate affairs is being re-evaluated" — that data-driven environments require ROI-based evidence rather than gut feelings. This aligns with the SCI framework's governance argument. Worth obtaining the report.

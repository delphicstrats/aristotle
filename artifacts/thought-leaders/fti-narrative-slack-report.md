# Artifact: FTI Consulting — "Corporate Affairs in a World Without Narrative Slack"

**Source:** FTI Consulting Strategic Communications

**Publication date:** January/February 2026
**Methodology:** Survey of 61 corporate affairs leaders in EMEA + board-level interviews
**Date retrieved:** 2026-03-13
**Relevance to SCI Framework:** ★★★ — Empirical support for Part I's governance failure argument; convergent terminology

---

## The Report's Central Claim

"Communications has entered a new operating environment. The space between what organisations say and what they do has narrowed sharply. In some cases, it has disappeared entirely."

FTI identifies what they call the "collapse of narrative slack" — the narrowing gap between organizational statements and organizational actions. The key driver: social media and AI have given stakeholders — employees, regulators, policymakers, investors, activists — the will and means to check statements against actions in near-real-time. When statements and actions diverge, the reaction is "fast and often unforgiving."

---

## Key Data Points

- **54%** of companies now face internal or external backlash when narrative and action fall out of sync
- **76%** of corporate affairs leaders believe stakeholders increasingly test company statements against actions in real time
- **Three in four** agree: any gap between statement and action is punished quickly
- CEO turnover up **9%**, with new leadership reassessing corporate affairs' seat at the executive table
- **73%** of corporate affairs directors now sit on executive committees (2024, up from 66%)

---

## FTI's Five Forward-Looking Findings

1. **Measuring what matters: reputation as strategic asset** — forward-looking teams are quantifying reputation impact to make business cases for investment
2. **Operationalising AI** — AI improving monitoring, drafting, and response; not changing *when* corporate affairs is involved or its influence over decisions
3. **Elevating employee communications** — blurring of internal/external communications, rising salaries and skills requirements
4. **Embedding insight and analytics** — shift to outcome-focused metrics, uptick in insight/analytics roles
5. **CEO visibility** — leaders balancing external presence with internal engagement nearly twice as likely to be seen as empowering

## Additional FTI Framing (from related pieces)

A companion FTI piece titled **"Corporate Affairs: From Communicators to Campaigners"** (EMEA, late 2025) argues:
- Corporate affairs has moved from "guardian of reputation" to active campaigner shaping organizational direction
- The function increasingly spans: reputation, regulation, investors, employees, and licence to operate
- "Structure and ways of working have not yet caught up with those expectations"

This parallels the SCI framework's argument in Part I that the failure is structural — not a failure of individual practitioners but of institutional design.

## Engagement with the SCI Framework

### Convergent terminology

"Narrative slack" and "say-do gap" are the same concept, arrived at independently. FTI is describing from an empirical/consulting angle exactly what the SCI framework theorizes philosophically: the structural alignment problem between what organizations say and what they do, and the fact that this gap is strategically costly.

The 76% figure — stakeholders testing statements against actions in real time — is direct empirical support for Part I's claim that the governance failure is now consequential at scale. This is the market condition that makes the SCI architecture not merely theoretically interesting but practically urgent.

### What FTI is missing (and SCI provides)

FTI diagnoses the problem accurately but cannot theorize it. "Narrative slack" is a metaphor without philosophical grounding. FTI's recommendations are operational (measure reputation, use AI better, involve corporate affairs earlier in decisions) but do not identify the root cause: the absence of an institutional governance architecture for managing the alignment between organizational action and organizational communication. The SCI framework is the theoretical completion of what FTI's data is pointing at.

FTI's finding that "structure and ways of working have not yet caught up" is precisely the governance gap SCI addresses. The structure FTI is groping for — distributed intelligence, accountability cycles, evidence-grounded alignment — is exactly what the SCI model specifies.

### The "licence to operate" language

FTI uses "licence to operate" as one of five functional domains corporate affairs now spans. This is the Civil Society domain's primary stake in the SCI framework's domain taxonomy: normative legitimacy, expressed as the social permission to operate. FTI names it without theorizing it; SCI theorizes it.

## Note on FTI Report Limitations

Dunne (Corporate Affairs Unpacked) correctly notes: FTI's report does not cite its sources adequately. The 73% executive committee figure is internally inconsistent with other published data. The report's findings should be treated as directional evidence of practitioner sentiment, not rigorous empirical data. The 54% and 76% figures are from a survey of 61 corporate affairs leaders in EMEA — a small, self-selected sample of practitioners from major organizations, not a representative sample.

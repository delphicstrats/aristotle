## Summary

Dunne introduces the concept of "narrative slack" — defined as "the gap between what companies say and what they do" — in response to the FTI Consulting report of the same name. The article uses the concept to analyze recent corporate affairs failures.

---

## Opening Case Study

A pharmaceutical giant (unnamed) announced plans to offshore activities to Poland. Employees immediately pointed out the contradiction: Poland was the least LGBT+-friendly country in Europe at the time, and the decision directly contradicted the company's longstanding public support for PRIDE. "Values, it seemed, could be put to one side when it came to saving money."

This is a clean illustration of the say-do gap in institutional ethos: the organization's stated character (its values commitments) and its operational decisions (resource allocation choices) were in structural contradiction. The communications function could not paper over the contradiction because the audience — employees and external stakeholders — had both the information to identify it and the motivation to publicize it.

## Key Data from FTI Report (as reported by Dunne)

- 54% of companies now face backlash when narrative and action fall out of sync
- 76% of corporate affairs leaders say stakeholders test statements against actions in real time and punish gaps quickly
- "The collapse of narrative slack changes the operating model for corporate affairs. Internal engagement assumes greater importance. Corporate affairs needs to be involved in decision-making as early as possible."

## Dunne's Analysis

> "This 'collapse of narrative slack', as it's dubbed by FTI, changes the operating model for corporate affairs. Internal engagement assumes greater importance. Corporate affairs needs to be involved in decision-making as early as possible. There is a greater emphasis on judgment."

> "Reputation is becoming harder to control at exactly the moment corporate affairs is being asked to take on greater responsibility."

The implication Dunne draws — corporate affairs needs to be involved in decision-making earlier, not just in message management — is identical to the SCI framework's governance argument: the communications function cannot fix at the signaling level what is broken at the operational level. The say-do gap is not a communications problem; it is an organizational governance problem.

## Engagement with the SCI Framework

### Independent corroboration

The convergence is philosophically significant: it suggests the say-do gap is genuinely a defining structural problem in corporate affairs right now, not merely a theoretical concern.

### What Dunne adds

Dunne's framing adds the *temporal* dimension: this is happening *now*, in response to the combination of social media amplification and AI-enabled real-time verification. The narrowing of narrative slack is a recent phenomenon driven by information environment changes. This is consistent with the SCI framework's empirical case in Part I, which argues the governance failure has always been structurally present but is now consequential at new scale.

### The governance implication

Dunne's prescription — "corporate affairs needs to be involved in decision-making as early as possible" — points toward the SCI model's answer without arriving at it. "Earlier involvement" is necessary but insufficient; it still treats corporate affairs as a corrective function rather than a governance function. The SCI model provides the institutional specification: not earlier involvement in ad hoc decisions, but a standing governance architecture (intelligence records, alignment cycles, distributed accountability) that makes the say-do gap structurally visible before it becomes publicly consequential.

# Attitude-Behavior Theory as a Foundation for the SCI Posture Construct

## Overview

The Strategic Communications Intelligence (SCI) framework is built on a foundational premise: that the internal psychological states of stakeholders — their perceptions, beliefs, and attitudes — reliably predict their external behaviors. SCI operationalizes this premise through the **posture construct** — the integrated disposition of a stakeholder toward an organization and its objectives, assessed against the specific actions the organization needs that stakeholder to take or refrain from taking.

For the posture construct to be theoretically defensible, this premise must be grounded in rigorous empirical evidence. The social psychology literature on attitude-behavior consistency provides that grounding. Three theoretical frameworks are central: the **Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB)** and its successor the **Reasoned Action Approach (RAA)**, developed by Fishbein and Ajzen; the **Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM)**, developed by Petty and Cacioppo; and the **Principle of Compatibility**, which specifies the measurement conditions under which attitude reliably predicts behavior.

Together, these frameworks establish that attitudes are robust predictors of behavior when properly measured, that attitude *strength* determines whether a measured posture is strategically reliable, and that posture assessment must be objective-specific rather than general if it is to support actionable communications governance.

---

## 1. The Historical Arc: From Skepticism to Validated Prediction

Early twentieth-century social psychology assumed a direct correspondence between attitudes and behavior. This optimism collapsed in the late 1960s when Wicker's (1969) review of empirical studies found only weak or non-significant relationships between verbal attitudes and observed behavior. The apparent "attitude-behavior gap" threatened the construct's utility.

The resolution came not from abandoning attitudes but from refining how they were measured and conceptualized. Researchers recognized that attitude is a latent disposition: it evaluates a psychological object, but its translation into behavior is mediated by cognitive and environmental factors. The modern consensus, built on decades of meta-analytic evidence, is that attitude is a reliable predictor of behavior **when specific theoretical principles are applied** — particularly when the attitude is measured at the appropriate level of specificity, when the stakeholder has the capacity and perceived control to act, and when the attitude has been formed through active cognitive engagement rather than superficial exposure.

This is the foundation on which SCI's posture construct rests. Posture is not sentiment; it is a structured assessment of behavioral disposition that, when measured correctly, predicts whether a stakeholder will take or withhold the actions on which strategic objectives depend.

## 2. The Theory of Planned Behavior: Intention as the Proximate Predictor

Fishbein and Ajzen's **Theory of Reasoned Action (TRA)** and its extension, the **Theory of Planned Behavior (TPB)**, establish the mechanism through which attitudes translate into behavior. The central construct is **behavioral intention**: the most proximal predictor of whether a given behavior will occur.

In the TPB, behavioral intention is determined by three constructs:

- **Attitude toward the behavior:** The individual's overall evaluation of performing the specific action, derived from beliefs about its likely consequences and the evaluation of those consequences.
- **Subjective norm:** The perceived social pressure to perform or not perform the action — what significant others expect.
- **Perceived Behavioral Control (PBC):** The individual's perception of their capacity to perform the action, encompassing both internal factors (skills, will) and external factors (resources, opportunity, cooperation of others).

PBC operates through two pathways: it shapes intention (a person unlikely to believe they can perform an action will not form a strong intention to do so), and it can directly predict behavior as a proxy for actual control when perceptions are accurate.

**Construct summary:**

| Construct | Function | SCI Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Behavioral Intention | Proximate predictor of behavior | Primary target of tactical communication |
| Attitude toward Behavior | Evaluative summary based on outcome beliefs | Assess whether negative posture reflects perceived negative consequences |
| Subjective Norm | Perceived social expectations | Identify whether stakeholder is inhibited by peer or institutional pressure |
| Perceived Behavioral Control | Perceived ease or difficulty of action | Identify whether stakeholder needs enabling information or resources |

The TPB's empirical record is strong. Meta-analyses show it explains approximately 40–49% of variance in behavioral intentions and 26–36% of variance in actual behavior — among the highest predictive benchmarks in the social sciences for complex human action.

## 3. The Reasoned Action Approach: Diagnostic Precision

The **Reasoned Action Approach (RAA)** — Fishbein and Ajzen's most recent formulation — disaggregates the TPB's three constructs into paired subcomponents, providing greater diagnostic precision. For SCI, this disaggregation matters because it identifies *why* a posture intervention is failing, not merely *that* it is failing.

**Attitude** is divided into:
- **Experiential attitude:** Evaluation of how performing the behavior *feels* — pleasant or unpleasant.
- **Instrumental attitude:** Evaluation of the behavior's *utility* — beneficial or harmful.

Research consistently finds that experiential attitudes are stronger independent predictors of behavior than instrumental evaluations. An organization that changes a stakeholder's view of the *logic* of an action without addressing how it *feels* to take that action will often find that posture improvements do not translate into behavioral change.

**Perceived norm** is divided into:
- **Injunctive norm:** Perceptions of what one *should* do according to relevant social groups.
- **Descriptive norm:** Perceptions of what others in one's reference group *are doing*.

In contexts of uncertainty or ambiguity, descriptive norms — observing others model the behavior — are often more influential than abstract injunctive pressure. Showing that peers or comparable organizations have taken a given action can move posture more effectively than argument alone.

**Perceived control** is divided into:
- **Capacity:** Belief that one possesses the necessary skills and resources.
- **Autonomy:** Belief that the decision is genuinely under one's own control.

Stakeholders who feel coerced rather than persuaded are less likely to act consistently with expressed attitudes and more likely to reverse course when pressure recedes.

| RAA Subcomponent | Definition | SCI Diagnostic Value |
|---|---|---|
| Experiential Attitude | Evaluation of the feelings associated with the action | Identifies affective barriers not captured by logical argument |
| Instrumental Attitude | Evaluation of the action's consequences | Addresses rational case for or against the required behavior |
| Injunctive Norm | Perceived social approval or disapproval | Identifies normative pressure from reference groups |
| Descriptive Norm | Perception of what peers are doing | Key for modeling behavioral contagion among undecided stakeholders |
| Capacity | Belief in possessing necessary skills or resources | Prerequisite for translating intention into action |
| Autonomy | Perception of volitional control | Ensures that posture change is durable, not coerced |

## 4. The Elaboration Likelihood Model: Posture Strength as a Strategic Variable

The TPB and RAA explain the *structure* of how attitudes predict behavior. The **Elaboration Likelihood Model (ELM)** explains *why* some attitudes are durable and behaviorally predictive while others are fragile and easily reversed. This distinction is critical for SCI: posture direction (positive or negative) is insufficient. Posture *strength* determines whether a measured disposition will reliably produce the required behavior.

The ELM posits two routes to attitude change:

**The Central Route** is engaged when elaboration likelihood is high — when the individual is both motivated to process a message (because it is personally relevant) and able to do so (because they have the cognitive resources and the message is accessible). Central-route processing involves active evaluation of arguments, their logical validity, and their consistency with existing knowledge. Attitudes formed through the central route are characterized by:
- Deep integration with prior beliefs and values
- Temporal persistence — they endure without reinforcement
- Resistance to counter-argument
- High behavioral predictiveness

**The Peripheral Route** is engaged when elaboration likelihood is low. The individual relies on heuristics — the attractiveness of the source, the apparent consensus of peers, the sheer quantity of information presented — rather than scrutinizing argument quality. Attitudes formed through the peripheral route are:
- Superficial and loosely held
- Likely to fade or shift with new cues
- Vulnerable to counter-persuasion
- Poor predictors of subsequent behavior

| Feature | Central Route (High Elaboration) | Peripheral Route (Low Elaboration) |
|---|---|---|
| Temporal Persistence | High — endures over time | Low — fades or changes quickly |
| Resistance to Counter-argument | High | Low — vulnerable to new cues |
| Behavioral Predictiveness | High — strong guide for action | Low — unreliable proxy for behavior |

**The implication for SCI is direct:** a stakeholder who supports an organizational objective because of superficial engagement (a credible spokesperson, a polished communication) represents a fragile posture. A stakeholder who has worked through the argument — understood the evidence, connected it to their own interests and values, engaged with counterarguments — represents a durable posture that will hold under adversarial pressure and translate reliably into action.

This means SCI's Talking Point Frameworks must be designed for central-route processing wherever possible: argument-based rather than heuristic-based, evidence-grounded rather than source-dependent, audience-specific rather than broadcast. The ELM also explains why the classical rhetoric tradition's commitment to valid logos — genuine argument from true premises — is not merely an ethical constraint but a strategic one. Peripheral-route persuasion is cheaper to achieve but strategically brittle.

**Determinants of elaboration:**
- *Motivation* is driven primarily by personal relevance. When a stakeholder perceives that an issue directly affects their interests, their willingness to engage arguments increases.
- *Ability* is constrained by cognitive resources, message complexity, and situational factors. A highly motivated stakeholder who receives a poorly constructed or technically inaccessible message will be forced into the peripheral route regardless of intent.

The ELM's "multiple roles" postulate adds further nuance: a given variable (such as source expertise) operates differently depending on elaboration level. Under low elaboration, expertise is a peripheral cue ("an expert said it, so it's probably true"). Under high elaboration, expertise is evidence to be evaluated ("does this expert's data actually support the conclusion?"). SCI's message design must account for the stakeholder's elaboration level when determining which elements to foreground.

## 5. The Principle of Compatibility: The Measurement Imperative

The Principle of Compatibility — also called the correspondence principle — provides the methodological rule that makes the posture construct actionable rather than merely theoretically valid.

The principle, established by Fishbein and Ajzen, states: **for an attitude to reliably predict a behavior, the attitude must be measured at the same level of specificity as the behavioral criterion across four dimensions:**

- **Target:** The specific object toward which the action is directed
- **Action:** The specific behavior being assessed
- **Context:** The setting in which the behavior occurs
- **Time:** The temporal window for the behavior

This is often abbreviated as **TACT**. Violations of correspondence — using a general attitude to predict a specific behavior — produce weak or insignificant attitude-behavior correlations. High correspondence produces correlations that rank among the highest achievable in social science research.

**Empirical evidence of correspondence effects:**

| Degree of Correspondence | Average Correlation (r) | Variance Explained |
|---|---|---|
| Low (general attitude, specific behavior) | .14 | ~2% |
| Partial (matching target only) | .39 | ~15% |
| High (matching target, action, and context) | .50–.67 | 25–45% |

A striking illustration: in studies on organ donation, general attitudes toward the cause explained minimal variance in registration behavior. When attitudes were measured specifically toward the act of registering, the specific attitude explained at least 70% more variance than the general measure.

**The SCI implication is foundational:** posture must be defined and assessed against the specific behavior the organization requires the stakeholder to take or withhold. Assessing a regulator's general disposition toward an organization is insufficient to predict whether they will approve a specific permit. Assessing a community group's general sentiment toward infrastructure development is insufficient to predict whether they will support or oppose a specific project at a specific location. Posture assessment in SCI must specify the behavioral criterion — and the intelligence records must be structured to capture posture at that level of specificity.

## 6. Meta-Analytic Validation

The cumulative meta-analytic record provides the empirical foundation for treating attitude as a reliable predictor of behavior under the conditions the preceding frameworks specify.

- **Kraus (1995):** Meta-analysis of 88 studies found a mean attitude-behavior correlation of r=.38 across the full literature. Studies observing the compatibility principle and controlling for attitude strength showed substantially higher correlations.
- **Wallace et al. (2005):** Meta-analysis of 797 studies found a mean correlation of r=.41 under average conditions. When external social pressure and behavioral difficulty were high (one standard deviation above mean), the correlation dropped to r=.30, establishing a boundary condition: posture predicts behavior unless environmental constraints are sufficient to override individual disposition.
- **Glasman & Albarracín (2006):** Meta-analysis of 128 conditions found that attitude-behavior correlation increases substantially when attitudes are (a) easily accessible in memory, (b) stable over time, and (c) formed through direct experience or high cognitive engagement.

| Moderator | Effect on Prediction | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Direct Experience | Increases correlation | Higher cognitive accessibility; attitude easier to retrieve at moment of action |
| Attitude Certainty | Increases correlation | Greater stability; resistance to change between measurement and action |
| Temporal Stability | Increases correlation | Ensures posture at measurement time reflects posture at decision time |
| Social Pressure | Decreases correlation | External normative forces override internal evaluation |
| Behavioral Difficulty | Decreases correlation | Perceived or actual lack of control inhibits action even with favorable posture |

The last two moderators establish an important boundary condition for SCI: posture is a reliable behavioral predictor unless external constraints are severe enough to override it. This means SCI's intelligence architecture must capture not only stakeholder posture but the environmental conditions — institutional constraints, resource limitations, social pressures — that may prevent a favorably disposed stakeholder from acting on their disposition.

## 7. The Intention-Behavior Gap and Its Resolution

A significant residual challenge in attitude-behavior theory is the **intention-behavior gap**: the consistent finding that individuals who express positive attitudes and clear intentions to act still frequently fail to do so. This is not a failure of the theoretical model; it is a feature of the real-world action environment that the governance architecture must account for.

The principal psychological mechanism for closing the gap is the **implementation intention** (Gollwitzer): a specific "if-then" plan that connects a situational cue to a behavioral response. While a behavioral intention specifies *what* one intends to do, an implementation intention specifies *when, where, and how* — "if situation Y arises, I will perform behavior Z." Research shows that implementation intentions substantially increase the probability that a positive attitude translates into action, particularly when the behavior requires overcoming situational barriers or competing demands.

For SCI, this implies that effective stakeholder engagement must move beyond attitude change toward **behavioral specification**: providing stakeholders with clear, concrete pathways from favorable posture to the specific action required. Communications that succeed in producing central-route attitude change but leave the behavioral pathway unspecified will underperform relative to their posture-change success.

The gap is also influenced by **actual behavioral control** — objective environmental factors that may prevent action regardless of attitude or intention. A stakeholder who genuinely supports an objective may be unable to act due to institutional constraints, resource limitations, or the need for cooperation from third parties. SCI's stakeholder intelligence must therefore assess not only posture but the enabling conditions for the required behavior.

## 8. The Posture Construct in SCI: Operational Definition

Drawing on the theoretical frameworks above, posture in SCI is defined as:

**The integrated disposition of a stakeholder toward taking or withholding a specific action on which an organizational objective depends, assessed against observable evidence of attitude strength, social influence, perceived control, and enabling conditions.**

This definition has several operational implications:

1. **Posture is objective-specific, not global.** A stakeholder's posture is assessed against a particular required behavior, not against the organization generally. (Compatibility principle)

2. **Posture has a strength dimension, not just a direction.** A positive posture formed through peripheral processing is strategically unreliable. Posture assessment must capture the depth of the stakeholder's engagement with the relevant arguments and evidence. (ELM)

3. **Posture assessment must capture enabling conditions.** Favorable posture is a necessary but not sufficient condition for action. The intelligence record must capture whether the stakeholder has the capacity, resources, and autonomy to act on their disposition. (TPB/RAA, intention-behavior gap)

4. **Posture change requires argument design, not only message design.** Communications that produce durable, behaviorally predictive posture change must engage the stakeholder's reasoning rather than relying on heuristic cues. (ELM central route, classical rhetoric's logos)

5. **Posture is dynamic, not static.** It must be monitored over time, as attitude stability varies and environmental conditions change. (Glasman & Albarracín moderators)

These operational implications are embedded in SCI's intelligence architecture: in the evidence lifecycle that requires posture assessments to be grounded in verifiable behavioral indicators, in the distributed ownership model that places assessment accountability with the record owners closest to the stakeholder relationship, and in the recurring alignment cycle that forces posture reassessment rather than treating it as a fixed datum.

## Conclusion

The posture construct is theoretically defensible and empirically grounded. The Theory of Planned Behavior establishes that attitudes, when properly measured, are reliable proximate predictors of behavior. The Reasoned Action Approach provides the diagnostic vocabulary for identifying which attitudinal subcomponents are driving or impeding behavioral alignment. The Elaboration Likelihood Model establishes that posture *strength* — not merely direction — determines behavioral reliability and provides the criteria for communications designed to produce durable, resistant, and behaviorally predictive attitude change. The Principle of Compatibility establishes the measurement rule that distinguishes useful posture assessment from superficial sentiment measurement. And the meta-analytic record, across hundreds of studies, confirms that these constructs reliably predict behavior when applied correctly.

The boundary conditions are equally clear: posture predicts behavior when attitude is accessible, stable, and formed through active engagement with relevant arguments; when subjective norms support rather than suppress action; when perceived and actual behavioral control are sufficient; and when enabling conditions in the environment are present. SCI's governance architecture is designed to assess and address each of these conditions — not merely to measure attitude, but to understand and manage the full causal pathway from stakeholder disposition to required organizational outcomes.

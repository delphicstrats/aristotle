Organizational Rhetoric in Deeply
Pluralistic Societies
The Agonistic Alternative
Scott Davidson
This chapter applies theories of agonistic democracy to develop a paradigm of organizational
rhetoric that might better support deeply pluralistic societies. In attempting to solve the thorny
problem of how organizations, particularly large powerful corporations, can serve society there
has been a turn by scholars towards advocating consensus oriented communications and the
seeking out of mutually beneficial relationships with stakeholders. The agonistic ethos places a
different emphasis on vibrant rhetorical exchanges, and dissensus, as essential in ensuring democ-
racies do not slide into the control of narrow elites, authoritarianism, or even violent conflict.
This chapter builds arguments on how agonism provides a framework for critically exploring the
problems of this turn as well as providing a theoretical underpinning for developing a more
democracy‐friendly ethos in practice.
The meaning of agonism is derived from the ancient Greek word agōn, which was used to
indicate a contest or struggle. Classical Hellenic agonism placed value on competition and
struggle to legitimize power or reputation. More contemporary and postmodern forms of ago-
nism value the ability of citizens to disrupt hegemonic power relationships by elevating plurality
and contest above neutral deliberation and rational consensus (Kalyvas 2009, Davidson 2016).
Agonistic writers such as Hannah Arendt and Bonnie Honig have endorsed the game elements
of freedom and involvement in public life, positing idealized public spaces as being full of pas-
sion for new ideas (Arendt, 1958) and of challenge to forms of domination (Honig, 1993).
Classical agonistic democracy requires the acceptance of the ability of citizens to constantly
challenge those who hold power. Position and acclaim can be won, but the winner who accepts
the glory of victory realizes that such cause for celebration is likely to be temporary. This concep-
tion of power and open challenge links classical agonism to contemporary agonism. Contemporary
agonists express anxiety in regard to any trend that solidifies power in the hands of the already
powerful. Yet classical and contemporary agonism are also linked by their acceptance of power
or hegemony as both necessary and unavoidable.
After explaining in more detail agonistic concepts and assumptions, the chapter will draw on
Arendt, Honig, and William E. Connolly to show how their writing can be applied to organiza-
tional rhetoric and communication. One specific instance highlighted in this chapter includes
using the corporate social responsibility (CSR) reporting of Starbucks to illustrate the problem
of rhetorical co‐option (see also chapter 28)––a process where corporations adopt the language
of their critics without significantly changing their social or environmental policies. Finally, this
analysis is extended to incorporate how the disruptive artwork of Alfredo Jaar and the rhetorical
strategy pursued by Martin Luther King, Jr. reveal alternative means for organizations to mobi-
lize public passions in democratic directions, in turn considering how these processes pose prob-
lems and questions for existing paradigms that assume wholly benign relationships between
organizations and their stakeholders, which leads to the positing of mutually beneficial relation-
ships realized through rhetorical exchanges.
What Is Agonism?
Agonism as a concept finds its origins in classical Hellenic culture. The concept grew out of
sporting competitions and the ethos of fair and open competition which was extended to other
areas of social and cultural life. The principle eventually became less oriented around physical
prowess as it was extended into contests of words and symbols in front of spectators. Musicians,
poets, and political speechmakers all sought esteem or influence through public performance
(Kalyvas, 2009). In rhetorical contests citizens “could establish their identity, advise the
community, and enhance their status, reputation and influence” (Fredal, 2006, p. 24). The scale
of such public theater events challenges modern conceptions of experiencing drama. Classical
Greek theater took place in open spaces with audiences of a thousand or more citizens. They were
collective emotional cauldrons where the audience was cajoled into responding to the arguments
found in the performance and to choose the rhetorical winners. With these performances fre-
quently occurring in close proximity to civic assembly meetings, there was an agonistic fusing of
public spaces and policymaking deliberation (Balme, 2014). Public spaces––agora––brought the
opportunity to win acclaim and power, but these prizes could not be owned as personal property.
Barker (2009) used the example of the contests set up by Achilles in the Iliad to illustrate this
point. Contest winners could stand in the middle of the agōn to collect their prize, but this central
point was a symbolic creation to indicate acclaim as a public property, and that the central posi-
tion might be temporarily occupied by one person, but remains open to all. There are clear impli-
cations about what might threaten this ethos of democratic agonism. Within the classical
conception anti‐democratic tyrants are those who attempt to gain reputation and power without
a fair contest. The tyrant who is not prepared to lose will destroy the credibility of the contest by
faking victory without a proper contest (Kalyvas, 2009). If democracy is to prevail then it must
constantly renew itself through permanent tension between authorities and expressions of
citizen dissent.
More contemporary forms of agonism diverge from classical Greek concepts by adjusting
focus away from how individual citizens might seek acclaim and power and toward the struggles
for power between groups, and within and among organizations in society. In these struggles
agonists would assume that all organizations will inevitably favor some values, or identities, over
others. Differing rhetorical positions should be recognized as not solely emerging from rational
thought processes, but rather should be understood as being grounded in the knowledge that
the power relations between groups in society help forge group identities. Rhetoric cannot be
divorced from social relationships. Therefore, it is assumed that there are no universal thought
frameworks or values which can be applied to adjudicate between conflicting positions. Conflicts
over meaning are too deep to be ever fully defused and a danger to all organizations is that they
respond by either ignoring or suppressing difference and disagreement. This radical negativity is
predicated according to Mouffe (2013) on the impossibility of any social order existing beyond
division and power as every order must exclude other possibilities. Agonists understand the urge
for stable environments, but warn that this urge can become a drive to portray all current power
relationships as normal and to ask publics to suppress their differences (Wingenbach, 2011).
Agonistic democrats break with the normative idealism of communitarianism by refusing to
automatically link a concern for human dignity with searches for rational consensus (Connolly,
2002). This means they typically share an assumption that individual rights and negative liberties
are insufficient to maintain political freedom, which can only be truly realized through struggles
against domination and arbitrary forms of power (Wenman, 2013). The agonistic approach sug-
gests that liberal or communitarian democrats underestimate the levels of plurality in modern
societies. This necessitates a recognition that all organizations will inevitably favor some values
or identities over others, and the correct democratic response requires acceptance, indeed facil-
itation, of resistance to all statements of values or policy. As Mouffe (2000) stated, “pluralism is
not merely a fact, something that we must bear grudgingly or try to reduce, but an axiological
principle. It is taken to be constitutive at the conceptual level of the very nature of modern
democracy and considered as something that we should embrace and enhance” (p. 19). The task
for organizations is to contribute to public life through nurturing, or at the very least not ham-
pering, the ability of citizens, particularly marginalized groups, from preventing harmful social
relationships becoming naturalized or rationalized (Connolly, 1991). This helps explain why
agonists elevate plurality and contest over consensus or any suggestion of debate closure.
Chantal Mouffe is a significant theorist who has attempted to use agonistic frameworks for
conceptualizing how democratic exchanges can better serve deeply plural societies. By drawing
on Derrida’s concept of the constitutive outside her assumptions would argue it is wrong for
organizations to believe they can seek consensus without exclusion, as this would entail envis-
aging an “us” that exists without a corresponding “them” (Mouffe, 1993). Organizations need
to appreciate that the creation of any group identity always implies the establishment of
difference, and that “us/them” relationships are susceptible to being converted into friend/
enemy antagonism. Mouffe (1993, 2005) argued that public spaces need to be constructed in
such a way that “us/them” relationships remain compatible with democratic pluralism, by
encouraging groups not to see others as enemies to be destroyed, but instead entering into
agonistic struggles between adversaries. In essence Mouffe is suggesting agonism as a third cat-
egory of relationship that can be added to those of friend or enemy. Under agonism different
organizations recognize there will never be a universally acceptable solution to an issue or iden-
tity conflicts; nevertheless, they endeavor to recognize the legitimacy of their opponents
(Mouffe, 2005).
When applied to organizational rhetoric, this suggests a critical reflection is needed on any
assumption that democracy is always best served by efforts to establish consensus and agreement
and to understand instead the creative and social justice functions of dissensus. As Villa (1999)
contended, liberals and communitarians are often “so anxious to avoid conflict that they con-
struct a set of public institutions, and a code of public argument and justification, which leave
precious little space for initiatory or expressive modes of political action” (p. 108). Indeed, the
acceptance of dissensus by organizational communicators can be seen as essential for enabling
reflective examinations of social relationships and rhetorical practices (Deetz, 1996).
Some writers have attempted to categorize the main themes within agonistic democracy
scholarship. For Wenman (2009) the three dominant themes are: agonism as a social good; the
facilitation of individuality and recognition of identities; and agonism as a strategy for achieving
greater social equality. Schaap (2007) identified a stream of pragmatic agonists who seek to
encourage civic participation and convert social antagonism into democratic contests. Expressive
agonists place a greater emphasis on fostering identities and difference. Finally, strategic agonists
explicitly understand and advocate contestation as a tool for advancing social justice, with the
belief that contests do not take place between equals but between privileged and excluded citi-
zens. Wingenbach (2011) identified broadly two main strands. The first is the agonism of resis-
tance which associates democracy with constant disruption of hegemonic orders and resistance
to power structures in society. The second is an agonism of pluralism that would view rhetorical
contests as a source of reciprocity and interdependence between different identity groups. All of
these strands or categorizations support agonism’s validity as a concept for testing rhetorical
theories of organizational communication, both in the sense of strategic intent in creating shared meanings and through illuminating the power dimensions of struggles to create discursive hege-
monies. The next section will explore the role of rhetoric in agonistic democracy primarily
through the work of Arendt, Honig, and Connolly.
Rhetoric and Agonistic Democracy
Hannah Arendt was one of the leading political philosophers of the twentieth century. She was
forced to flee her native Germany after the Nazis came to power, and subsequently the nature of
totalitarianism and of freedom were key themes in her writing. Arendt envisaged positive roles
for rhetorical disagreement, indeed the absence of rhetorical disputes were seen as indicators of
a descent into authoritarianism. This danger would present itself if citizens came to believe they
were not responsible for the direction of society. Indeed, for citizens as employees of organiza-
tions, authoritarianism is to be found in a belief that there is no choice but to implement any
directives that come from above. This is why responsible organizations would recognize that to
support freedom means to support the fostering of the ability of citizens to publicly engage with
rhetorical disputes over issues and politics (Roberts‐Miller, 2002). This approach implies an
organization’s true identity can only be revealed after it takes actions in public view. The
combination of creating rhetoric and joining civic actions establishes new social relationships
and integrates these into existing networks of association (Deveaux, 1999). These identities are
necessarily open to constant change as actions have unpredictable consequences, but they are
required to establish difference and accordingly support pluralism. The objective of rhetoric is
to create a passion for ideas and to enable organizations to accept, indeed welcome, criticism
from others. As such Arendt’s agonism can be seen as placing a special emphasis on the role of
persuasive rhetoric. The value of this rhetoric would not be found through asymmetrical inter-
actions between an active speaker and a passive audience, but instead through the quality of the
subsequent disputes (Roberts‐Miller 2002). Drawing on Arendt suggests that the process of
rhetoric serves freedom only when it provokes counterarguments and critique, with success
judged not by the generation of followers and fans, but by the creation of a more diverse pool
of voices to engage with subsequent rhetorical exchanges. The significance of rhetorical conflict
flows from nourishing exchanges that reach beyond two protagonists and two positions and
draw in a wider repertoire of positions and experiences. This is a process whereby dissensus also
opens the possibility for each participant in a discourse to realize what might lie in common as
underpinnings to the social world (Schaap, 2007).
Arendtian rhetoric also demands that organizational communication should foster a sense of
institutional self‐doubt––a sense that perfection can never be attained––but to strive to be seen
as a contributor to the wider wellbeing of society in a process that requires incorporating the
judgement of others. In this sense organizations would always resist closed frontiers and be open
to new interlocutors and new ways of thinking (Tully, 2008).
Bonnie Honig (1993) used Arendt’s writing to develop her own conceptual work on ago-
nistic democracy. Honig argued that modern political theorizing is characterized by attempts to
remove the disruptions within social life from normative models. This trend is also apparent in
recent theoretical turns in fields such as public relations (see chapter 4), where there have been
multiple attempts to eliminate rhetorical conflict from normative models (Davidson, 2016). As
Honig (1993) contended in relation to political theory, these turns:
confine politics … to the juridical, administrative, or regulative tasks of stabilizing moral and political
subjects, building consensus, maintaining agreements, or consolidating communities and identities.
They assume that the task of political theory is to resolve institutional questions, to get politics right,
over, and done with, to free modern subjects and their sets of arrangements of political conflicts and
instability. (p. 2)

For Honig there exists a lamentable yearning for rhetorical closure, a desire to squeeze out
spaces for dissensus and rhetorical struggle. This yearning necessitates attempts to identify some
kind of rational thought process or procedure that would eliminate the uncertainty caused by
dissensus. But as has already been seen in this chapter, agonists view such moves as running
counter to a proper democratic ethos. There are strong parallels with theorizing that also seeks
to de‐legitimize the use of competitive rhetoric by organizations, placing normative pressures on
practitioners, and on university curricula, to privilege communication styles that are ostensibly
nonpartisan and seek to defuse public passions on issues if dissensus is apparent (Lane, 2014;
Davidson, 2016).
While the logic of this approach might be attractive to those who wish to demonstrate how
strategic use of rhetoric might reduce levels of uncertainty in the external environments of large
organizations, validated by improvements in bottom line metrics, it distances norms in organi-
zational communication from norms in agonistic democratic theory. Indeed, if communicators
only understand their roles as defenders of settlements that protect the positions of senior man-
agers they run the risk of precluding Arendtian human achievement as expressed through the
opening up of new ways of thinking (Wenman, 2013). The danger for Honig is that all settle-
ments on issue positions, even those that include elements of empowerment for communities,
will still generate resistance. Honig’s theorizing suggests that excessive claims of consensus and
unity can represent a threat to democracies just as much as the threat of overheated rhetorical
antagonisms (Wenman, 2013).
William E. Connolly is a political theorist who has drawn on both North American and
European traditions to expand upon the agonistic theme that pluralism is not something to be
wrestled with and pacified, but something to be nurtured. His work resonates with Mouffe in
that individual or group identity needs to be articulated in the context of other identities per-
ceived as being in opposition. A key concept that Connolly (1991, 1995) uses is that of plural-
ization. This is where Connolly suggests liberal pluralism is anchored in notions of fixed
identities, and so when presented with new social movements––a distinctive reality of post-
modern societies––it is inclined to privilege existing social identities. Liberalism becomes locked
into historical relationships and settlements, struggling to simultaneously engage both histor-
ical and emerging patterns of identities and relationships. In response, Connolly suggested the
cultivation of an ethos of pluralization. This ethos is represented by nurturing collaboration
and agonistic respect between intersecting stakeholders, in a manner which allows hegemonic
relationships to be contested. This process requires the development of a critical responsiveness
to new movements and identities, with organizations seeking to proactively negotiate new rela-
tionships and processes of mutual recognition. The role of organizational rhetoric within these
processes is to open up new spaces where differences can be examined and if necessary rede-
fined. These spaces require a generosity towards new identities within society, not least because
these movements typically provoke resentment and hostility from other established groups
(White, 2009).
Connolly’s agonistic ethos raises one particularly tricky issue for any organization engaging
with a diverse range of stakeholder groups: whether all groups should be allocated a place in
rhetorical exchanges, or should some positions be considered as sitting outside the norms of
liberty and equality required for democratic discourse to remain agonistic, rather than violent or
antagonistic. Mouffe (2014) suggested that a weakness in Connolly’s position is the suggestion
that all antagonisms can be transformed into agonism. An avoidance of rhetorical engagement
with some groups whose positions sit outside legitimate democratic norms may be justifiable for
organizations, but the exclusions require a reflective self‐awareness of how these might impact
upon socially marginalized groups.
Arendt, Honig, and Connolly all see the role of public debate and dissensus as vital in guard-
ing against anti‐democratic politics and enabling counter‐hegemonic struggles for excluded
groups. They also seek to nurture plurality and the emergence of new struggles and identities.
But as Mouffe (2014) argued there are limitations on the extent to which institutions and orga-
nizations can translate social antagonisms into democratic agonistic rhetorical contests. In the
next section the application of the agonistic ethos is discussed in reference to the existing rhetor-
ical paradigms that assume all groups can achieve a level of satisfaction or benefit from rhetorical
exchanges.
Applying Agonistic Principles to Organizational Rhetoric
Robert L. Heath has played a prolific role as both writer and curator of the rhetorical paradigm
for theorizing and studying public relations (Heath, 2001, 2006, 2009). Building on his central
assumption that public relations is synonymous with rhetoric, his work has attempted to then
understand how it might normatively contribute to a fully functioning society. Heath’s model is
open to a synthesis with agonistic concepts as it tends to incorporate advocacy as a legitimate
form of organizational communication. As Ihlen (2011) stated, the rhetorical paradigm has
enabled scholars to support the use of persuasion in public relations against efforts by systems
theorists who have attempted to conceptualize any form of non‐consensual communication as
being inherently inferior. Heath’s model tends to avoid adopting the language of universal con-
sensus, which evades one key agonistic critique of dialogic theorizing; however, his work does
tend to emphasize rationality in a way that would draw criticism from the more postmodern
agonistic scholars. Heath’s model asserts that, if not full consensus, then mutually beneficial
relationships between organizations and their publics might become attainable if communica-
tors enact forms of rhetoric that encourage businesses and organizations to ground their policies
in civic problem solving, with solutions and values emerging from the dialogue (Heath, 2001;
see chapter 22 on dialogue).
Throughout his work Heath has drawn on Kenneth Burke and the language of competitive
marketplaces. Heath cited Burke to suggest society is a marketplace of ideas, facts, values, and
policies that is characterized by wrangle and disputes between organizations. This wrangle in
turn institutionalizes the dialectics of both competition and cooperation (Burke, 1969; Heath,
2009). Rhetorical statements by organizations gain meaning by the degree to which they agree,
disagree, or spatially relate to statements produced by other organizations (Heath, Waymer, &
Palenchar, 2013). This continual process of statements and counterstatements examines facts
and values and in doing so public relations adds value to society by assisting consumers to make
wiser purchasing decisions or helping institutions adopt more effective public policies. Part of
this process will be self‐righting where error will be exposed and corrected by truth––if both are
expressed freely––and scholars such as Cutlip (2013) have argued that the normative role of
public relations is to enhance and enable this process. The language of rational choice and mar-
ketplaces strongly implies that where arguments have prevailed over other possibilities, on
balance this must be because those arguments hold intrinsic value and truth, blurring the obvious
alternative explanation, that arguments can also prevail purely and simply because they assist in
supporting or entrenching a set of hegemonic relationships. As Ihlen (2011) has noted, this has
led to some uncertainty because Heath has accepted that reality is socially constructed yet still
maintains that underlying truths will be revealed by vigorous and open rhetorical exchanges.
Heath’s model has always assumed that thoughts, opinions, and public actions are subject
to constant change and reinterpretation and that the “effort and incentive of the dialogue are
toward continual improvement, even, if it falls short of that goal” (Heath, 2009, p. 71). This
suggests a tension in Heath’s model driven by his recognition that universal consensus will not
result from rhetorical exchanges, yet he also constantly states that forging agreements is
essential to the process, that good arguments that hold truth and a concern for wider good of
the community will help achieve concurrence. Concurrence is a key concept in Heath’s writing;
he provided this definition of what he means by concurrence: “Although consensus might not
result on every issue, sufficient agreement––concurrence––allows parties to continue dialogue
in cooperative competition. Disagreement gives motive and rationale for such exchange to test
which zone of meaning achieves coordinated and mutually rewarding behavior” (Heath,
2001, p. 44).
Concurrence is the metaphorical glue that binds participants into maintaining their participa-
tion in rhetorical exchanges. Each step towards enacting concurrence makes additional dialogue
and rhetorical exchange possible, while simultaneously building civic legitimacy for decisions
and policies that arise from the process. So although concurrence falls short of universal con-
sensus, it does imply that a certain degree of agreement is present, and that parties engaged in
the competitive dialogue see benefits both from participation and from its practical outcomes.
The process of statements and counterstatements is unending, not least because new problems,
issues, and facts will continually emerge (Heath, 2006).
From an agonistic perspective there would be skepticism towards claims that any existing con-
sensus, or concurrence, was constituted by a dialectical aggregation of the best arguments and
evidence. Any settlement on an issue would be assumed to have been at least partially con-
structed to justify existing social relations between groups. Furthermore, issue settlements, and
the conduct of the rhetorical exchanges, will have their outcomes heavily influenced by the pro-
tagonists who hold the most power. Indeed, if the rhetorical exchange is purely based on the
dominant group’s conception of logic and legitimacy then there is the prospect of the exchange
squeezing out the ability of marginalized groups to achieve progressive change. Without explicit
recognition that all decisions are based on contingent circumstances the fear of agonists is that
groups will withdraw from democratic engagement and exchanges become more antagonistic,
or even violent, in nature.
With concurrence, the success of the rhetorical exchange could be evaluated through the pro-
pensity of groups to remain as participants in future engagements. Such evaluation criteria sug-
gest some overlaps with Mouffe’s conflictual consensus (2013). Mouffe’s conflictual consensus
is grounded in radical negativity and so seeks a more limited agreement on the rules of engage-
ment and an allegiance to liberty and equality in order to transform antagonism into rhetorical
struggles over interpreting the common good (Jones, 2014). Successful public spaces prevent
relationships descending from “us/them” to “friend/enemy” dynamics. Evidence of a willing-
ness by groups to continue their rhetorical competition within shared public spaces would be
one indicator of success (Mouffe, 1999, 2005).
Heath’s (2001) mutually beneficial relationships are relevant to trends in CSR programs, par-
ticularly market oriented CSR that believes it can create value for stakeholders whilst simulta-
neously pursuing bottom line economic objectives (see also chapter 28). Some CSR practitioners
are aware that such an approach is unrealistically blind to the role of power in communicative
relationships, and there have been some attempts to create more deliberative forms of interac-
tion (Dawkins, 2015). But this process turns full circle into the classic problem: that no rhetor-
ical exchange can be fully inclusive of all the needs of all the protagonists. The level of benefit, if
it does exist, is spread unevenly. Furthermore, the rules and conduct of the rhetorical engage-
ment are likely to be stacked in favor of the already powerful.
In the absence of hope of a rhetorical exchange finding anything that would approximate to
universal consensus, the concept of mutual benefit opens the door to a new problem. Attempts
to mirror civic language can lead, not to the empowerment of civic groups, but rather to the
co‐optation of their rhetoric. Co‐option is a process whereby large organizations, particularly
businesses, accommodate the language of social inclusion or environmentalism, but without actu-
ally implementing significant changes to their policies or working practices (Burchell & Cook,
2013). They “talk the talk,” but they fail to “walk the walk.” But this goes further than just an
accusation of ethical inconsistency. Co‐option is a rhetorical process where a social or environ-
mental critique of corporate influence is appropriated in such a manner that the very same corpo-
rations are positioned as the providers of the solutions to the problems raised (Dawkins, 2015).
Starbucks is an illustrative example. As Starbucks expanded rapidly, it did so in a manner which
opened it up to the criticism that it was using predatory business practices. As Starbucks grew, it
did so to the detriment of independent, family owned coffee shops which were being driven out
of business. Such accusations manifested themselves in many ways, including in the shape of
independent coffee shop owners taking Starbucks to court. They charged the corporation with
a deliberate strategy that included paying above market rates for retail spaces if landlords agreed
to refuse competitors the right to open a store in the same or neighboring buildings (Allison,
2006). Discourses that were developed by groups who believed Starbucks was a threat to their
town or neighborhood focused on a sense of localism––that a corporate business model was
destroying the distinctive character of neighborhoods and the ability of local people to build up
new businesses within their local community (Thompson & Arsel, 2004). Indeed, what used to
be commonplace, an independent coffee shop trading in its local community, for many became
a symbol of resistance to corporate power. Faced with this challenge to the legitimacy of their
business model Starbucks can be seen as attempting to co‐opt the rhetoric of their opponents. A
constant theme in Starbucks’ CSR reporting is its deployment of the language of good neigh-
bors, of making a positive impact on communities (Starbucks, 2016). In its annual CSR reports
the corporation strategically foregrounds initiatives such as encouraging its employees to volun-
teer for local community service schemes. Indeed, page 4 of the 2015 report is titled “strength-
ening communities” (Starbucks, 2015). None of Starbucks’ CSR statements represent a change
in behavior or policy that would properly address the original criticism of homogenized town
centers and independent shops being unable to compete fairly with a giant corporation. Instead
Starbucks co‐opts the rhetoric of strong local communities and attempts to place themselves as
the solution to the original problem.
Yet Starbucks’ policy and rhetorical responses could be argued to fit within the principle of
mutually beneficial relationships, as they offer items of at least some value to stakeholders as part
of their communication strategy. Doing so shows the weaknesses of such an approach as it is
unable to democratically empower disadvantaged stakeholders. An agonistic ethos may offer a
better alternative for understanding how rhetorical exchanges between organizations and civic
society can be put on a more democratic footing (see chapter 14 on ethos). The requirement
that communication practitioners can only ultimately justify their jobs if they demonstrate how
their work is subservient to the needs of senior management elites requires a critical examination
as part of that process. As Rhodes and Harvey (2012) argued, to realize ethical credibility an
organization must proactively foster continual challenges to management values and policies.
Relationships will become more ethical through a process of embracing dissent and resistance
from outside groups. Indeed, if fundamental social questions are rendered invisible in rhetorical
exchanges a potential driver of ethical reflection and cultivation of civic excellence will not be
realized (Owen, 2002).
Agreements, policy, or consensus that arise from rhetorical encounters should trigger a natural
skepticism as to whether they should be considered natural or wholly objective, or rather the
results of a struggle between competing interests and values. Issues that raise tensions between
an organization and its stakeholders should not be considered as merely technical issues awaiting
adjudication from experts, but as Mouffe (2013) contended they require a choice to be made
between conflicting viewpoints. At that moment of decision, a new hegemonic set of practices
and relationships will be put in place (Mouffe, 2005). While this suggests a skeptical disposition
toward any claims of rhetoric enabling wholly rational and mutually beneficial relationships,
Heath’s concept of concurrence does sit in harmony with many agonistic assumptions over the
ultimate objectives of rhetorical struggles. Mouffe’s somewhat pessimistic objectives are the
creation of rhetorical spaces that would ensure existing “us/them” relationships do not decline
into more aggressive “friend/enemy” encounters, or worse, descend into physical violence or
withdrawal from democratic forums. The positive goal would be to enact rhetorical struggles
that increase the propensity of all protagonists to remain committed to future engagements. A
deeper social goal would be that these engagements also result in empowerment of the socially
disadvantaged and a reasonable belief that a predetermined victor does not always emerge. All of
which entails an ethos of communication practice as a public service: a proud servant of
democratic values and empowerment. Enactment of this would require a new professional and
organizational ethos that accepted that the bottom line should not always be the prime objective
of strategic communication. This is a considerable barrier. Nonetheless, corporate commitments
to social responsibility and concern for their reputations create at least some opportunities to
adjust instrumental orientations toward a public service ethos amongst communication practi-
tioners in business (Ihlen, Bartlett, & May, 2011).
Implications Academic and Practical
This section discusses some practical and academic implications of agonism through a discussion
of two aspects of the agonistic ethos. Firstly, it explores how an agonistic approach to public
engagement places differing expectations on communication practitioners in contrast to the
more traditional mutually beneficial relationships or deliberative approaches. Secondly, the sec-
tion provokes thought by considering the implications for rhetoric in the public square if we are
to accept agonistic assumptions about the importance of passions and identities in mobilizing
civic society.
Previous theoretical works considering agonism and public relations have argued that the ago-
nistic ethos provides potential for organizations who wish to mobilize and empower social dis-
advantaged groups, as well as for practitioners who are seeking new methods for engaging
stakeholders and improving levels of civic participation (Davidson, 2016). Embracing this ethos
would entail a reassessment of currently popular assumptions regarding the proper forms and
objectives of organizational communication on important social and economic issues. The
objectives of creating agonistic public spaces would be to enable rhetorical exchanges while
revealing the network of relationships between stakeholders and other groups. Mouffe (2013)
has been prominent in conceptualizing the characteristics of agonistic public spaces, particularly
in light of her concern to ensure that any exchanges bring about counter‐hegemonic challenges
that may reveal and then transform existing social relationships. Although the status quo is
always a possible, and legitimate, outcome of the rhetorical engagement, to be compliant with
an agonistic ethos there must remain the possibility that the “losers” in any given set of relation-
ships are not just empowered to challenge but also have the potential to transform those very
relationships. The obvious problem with the mutually beneficial relationships approach is that
communicators working for already powerful corporations must always ensure that the outcome
of rhetorical exchanges can be considered as a win for their employer––or at the very least not a
complete loss.
Mouffe (2013) endorsed the work of the artist Alfredo Jaar as satisfying this agonistic ethos:
“(Jaar) is convinced that the best way to move people to act is by awakening consciousness of
what is missing in their lives and by bringing them to feel that things could be different” (p. 95).
A good illustration of an agonistic public space comes from the invitation by the town of Skoghall
in Sweden for Jaar to create a new civic center for culture. Paper is the main industry of Skoghall,
and a new cultural center was built by Jaar entirely out of paper and other local industrial prod-
ucts. One day after its completion, in front a public gathering, he insisted on burning down the
building. Of relevance to both scholars and practitioners in communication is that the project
was approved and commissioned by the staff at the town council and also included the partici-
pation of the Finnish corporation Stora‐Enso, a significant employer in the area. Indeed, Stora‐
Enso was convinced by the other partners to fund the project, which appears to be a commendable
act. The Jaar project would ask potentially awkward questions around the absence of cultural
amenities in the town and invite critical scrutiny of the corporation’s contributions to the quality
of civic life. The corporation accepted the invitation to risk its reputation in rhetorical exchanges
and symbolic art works. But, this is an important requirement if agonistic expectations of what
constitutes ethical public service are to be met. While the corporation could undoubtedly point
to some win–win gains from the exercise, it is not these that make it legitimate. The construction,
and then burning down, of a new community resource shook up local relationships between the
corporation and its stakeholders. In the process the dynamic of the social relations between
business, local government, and citizens was altered. In practice evaluation or empirical research
the agonistic ethos could be explored by assessing how a case like this stimulates civic engage-
ment and whether it improves the propensity of participants to want to engage with each other
again in the future. Indeed, Heath’s concept of concurrence would also be relevant in any such
assessment. Theoretically, the rhetorical exchanges become more productive only after the
artistic provocation have enabled critical contemplation of existing power relationships.
Furthermore, there were no attempts to remove or censure passions or dissensus from the com-
municative engagement. The town’s residents complained at the decision to destroy the new
facility. But Jaar, who enjoyed creative control of the project, insisted he did not want to impose
a new institution on the town when the local community had not previously fought for its
existence. In response, local action groups were formed to develop a permanent public gallery
for the town (Corris, 2002). This appears to be a promising outcome, but there is always the
danger that the new set of relationships begin to be seen again as natural and beyond further
challenge (Mouffe, 2005).
In contrast to the manner in which the work of Habermas (1989) or Heath (2009) has been
applied to disciplines such as public relations, we need to consider that it is not cold rationality
that should underpin how organizations seek to encourage rhetorical exchanges, but instead an
acceptance that individuals participate based on their personal identity and passions (Mouffe,
2013). If identities require an “us/them” dynamic then the more realistic model for public
spaces is one that welcomes expressions of dissensus and challenge. To support agonistic con-
ceptions of freedom and democracy, scholars and practitioners will need to reassess their current
reluctance to embrace contest and conflict not just as social necessities but also as social goods.
Theory and practice will fall short of agonistic expectations if disadvantaged publics are told
they must wait for more powerful groups to see reform as a win–win situation before progress
can be achieved.
To illustrate this point, it may be useful to consider the campaigns for civil rights in the United
States and the rhetorical style of Martin Luther King Jr. A starting point is Mihai’s (2014) con-
tention that Martin Luther King’s “I have a dream” speech meets many of the criteria for ago-
nistic forms of public action and debate. This is because of the manner in which it addressed an
angry public but eschewed violence against their adversaries, and consciously sought to inspire
emotions and passions in democratic directions:
While the speech recognises the indignation experienced by African‐Americans, it talks to their pas-
sions and invites them to pursue their hope for a more equal America in a way that does not violate
America’s democratic ethos. It could be safely argued that King managed to persuade the emotion-
ally committed, yet responsive, public that democracy required them to follow their dream passion-
ately, yet agonistically. (p. 44)
In addition to making transparent the injustices and power relations between communities,
Martin Luther King fostered identity and a sense of freedom through public challenge to mobi-
lize passions in democratic directions. This requires a recognition that although the civil rights
movement pursued a strict policy of physical nonviolence it still required the initiation of rhetor-
ical conflict in order to succeed. Indeed Martin Luther King subscribed to the view that justice
only comes after a preceding struggle (Askins & Mason, 2015) and he provided this famous
quote during the height of the campaign: “Freedom is never voluntarily given by the oppressor;
it must be demanded by the oppressed” (King, 1963). The rhetorical confrontation of the
movement also needs to be understood in the context of how it linked with a wider campaign
strategy and built upon decades of legal argument and publicity work by the National Association
for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP) (Kern‐Foxworth, 1992; Straughan, 2004).
Conclusion and Future Research
While practitioners might be tempted to initiate public engagement by using optimistic or
uplifting cues based on shared positive experiences, when privileging the needs of a modern
democracy there needs to be recognition that it is often lack of trust, disappointment, or dis-
sensus that prompts participation and engagement. Organizations can build democratic capital
by supporting processes that foster enthusiasm for nonviolent participation in competitive rhe-
torical exchanges, sharing spaces, and exchanging views with other citizens. Therefore, if orga-
nizations wish to communicate in a manner which supports the agonistic ethos, at the very least
they need to reflect on whether their influence on rhetorical exchanges is hampering the ability
of the socially excluded to advocate to transform their own position. This implies a willingness
by organizational communicators to cede control of the rhetorical exchange and for practi-
tioners to not fear “negative” comments or adverse publicity. This presents an interesting
research challenge to understand in what circumstances the internal cultures of organizations
and agencies are open to an agonistic ethos, and where there is cultural resistance. When orga-
nizing consultations and public forums, efforts need to be made to ensure that emotions and
struggles between competing issue positions are not designed out of the process.
In terms of understanding how to evaluate the success, or otherwise, of rhetorical spaces from
the standpoint of agonistic democracy, more research is required on how organizations’ engage-
ment with civic society impacts on attitudes towards further engagements in the future. Here
there would appear to be value in attempting to synthesis Heath’s concept of concurrence and
its emphasis on competitive cooperation (Heath, 2001), and Mouffe’s conflictual consensus
with its stress on converting antagonism into agonism (Mouffe, 2005, 2013). This would mean
shifting away from seeing evidence of consensus and pacification of debates as indicating success-
ful, ethical communication. While organizations and institutions need hegemonic settlements in
order to function coherently each order needs to be accepted as being contingent––rejected by,
indeed actually harmful, to the interests of some stakeholders and therefore always open to new
challenges. This is a prerequisite for all parties to believe that ongoing engagement and exchange
are worthwhile activities. Perhaps a neologism could be suggested for this new framework: con-
tentious concurrence––a phrase indicating that successful rhetorical spaces positively enable
struggle while seeking to build the belief amongst all participants in the efficacy of participation.
Organizations can build a form of democratic capital by supporting processes that foster enthu-
siasm for nonviolent participation in competitive rhetorical exchanges, sharing spaces, and
exchanging views with other citizens. Further academic exploration of these concepts in turn
would facilitate a process whereby such objectives are to be added to the metrics and applied
research methods used by practitioners.

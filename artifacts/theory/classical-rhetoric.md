# Classical Rhetoric as a Foundation for Strategic Communications Governance

## Overview

Classical rhetoric, systematized by Aristotle in the fourth century BC, provides the foundational theory of persuasion that the Strategic Communications Intelligence framework requires but that neither Resource Dependence Theory nor the DoD strategic communications doctrine fully supplies. Where RDT explains *why* stakeholders matter structurally and the JIC explains *how* to organize influence at scale, classical rhetoric explains *what makes communication persuasive* — what enables a signal to cross the gap between sender and receiver and produce the intended cognitive and behavioral effect.

This document develops the classical rhetorical tradition as a theoretical foundation for SCI's governance model. It proceeds in four parts: the definition and social function of rhetoric; the three modes of persuasion (ethos, logos, pathos) and their organizational implications; the concept of kairos and the audience as arbiter of meaning; and the sophistry problem — the ethical constraint that makes the classical tradition indispensable to SCI's claim to be a governance framework and not merely a coordination tool.

## Part I: The Definition and Social Function of Rhetoric

Aristotle defines rhetoric as *the faculty of observing in any given case the available means of persuasion* (*Rhetoric* I.2). Several features of this definition are worth unpacking before proceeding to the specific constructs.

First, rhetoric is a *faculty* — a practical capacity for observation and judgment — not a fixed set of techniques. The rhetorician does not apply templates; he or she reads a situation and discerns what arguments, delivered through what modes, to what audience, at what moment, are most likely to produce the desired effect. This is a form of practical wisdom (*phronesis*) applied to communication.

Second, rhetoric concerns *all available means* of persuasion — not just speeches, but actions, symbols, demonstrations of character, and the management of audience emotions. This expansive scope is essential to SCI: it establishes from first principles that organizational communication is not confined to what an organization says. Everything an organization does, including operational decisions that have no communicative intent, sends signals that audiences receive and interpret. The classical tradition's concept of rhetoric already encompasses what the JIC calls "physical communication" — action that conveys meaning.

Third, rhetoric is an *observational* art. The rhetorician begins with the audience, not with the message. The appropriate argument is discovered by attending carefully to who the audience is, what they believe, what they value, what they fear, and what they are disposed to do. Message-first communication — crafting a statement and then finding an audience for it — inverts the rhetorical method. Audience-first analysis is the classical approach, and it is the epistemological foundation of SCI's stakeholder intelligence function.

### The Social Function of Rhetoric

Aristotle situates rhetoric among the essential arts of civic life precisely because democratic decision-making cannot wait for philosophical certainty. Citizens, juries, and assemblies must make decisions under conditions of uncertainty, with incomplete information, about matters that do not admit of formal proof. The rhetorician's task is to make valid arguments receivable by audiences that are not equipped — or do not have time — for pure dialectical reasoning.

This social function distinguishes rhetoric from both dialectic (philosophical argument between equals seeking truth through systematic inquiry) and eristic (contentious argument aimed at defeating an opponent rather than establishing truth). Rhetoric operates in the domain of *probable* reasoning — not mathematical certainty, but the kind of well-grounded judgment that prudent people make in complex situations.

For SCI, this framing matters because it establishes the *legitimacy* of strategic communication as a social function. Organizations engaging with regulators, media, investors, and community groups are not engaged in manipulation; they are engaged in the necessary work of making their legitimate arguments accessible to audiences with different knowledge, values, and frames of reference. The ethical question is not whether to employ rhetoric but whether to employ it honestly — in service of valid arguments and genuine interests — or dishonestly.

## Part II: The Three Modes of Persuasion

Aristotle identifies three modes of persuasion (*pisteis*) — ethos, logos, and pathos — which he treats as exhaustive. Every persuasive effect is produced through some combination of these three. Understanding them as distinct constructs, rather than as interchangeable rhetorical devices, is essential to their application in SCI's framework.

### Ethos: The Credibility of the Source

Ethos is the credibility of the communicating party as perceived by the audience. Aristotle specifies three components:

- **Phronesis** (practical wisdom): the audience's perception that the communicator has genuine competence and sound judgment in the relevant domain
- **Eunoia** (goodwill): the audience's perception that the communicator has their interests at heart and is not acting solely from self-interest
- **Arete** (moral virtue): the audience's perception that the communicator is honest — that their statements accurately represent their beliefs and their actions accurately represent their stated commitments

All three components must be present. A communicator perceived as competent but self-serving will not be trusted. A communicator perceived as well-intentioned but incompetent will not be believed. A communicator perceived as both competent and well-intentioned but dishonest will be dismissed once the dishonesty is discovered — and will carry that reputational damage forward.

Critically, ethos is not claimed but demonstrated. An organization cannot establish credibility by asserting its trustworthiness. Ethos is built incrementally through the consistency of behavior over time: through making commitments and honoring them, through making predictions and being right, through expressing concern for stakeholder interests and demonstrating that concern through action.

The say-do gap is, at its root, an ethos problem. When an organization's operational actions contradict its stated positions — when it announces careful, phased implementation and simultaneously pursues aggressive expansion — it signals to audiences that one of three things is true: the organization doesn't know what it's doing (failed phronesis), it doesn't care about the interests it claimed to protect (failed eunoia), or it was never telling the truth (failed arete). Any of the three destroys the credibility on which future persuasion depends.

**Organizational ethos** extends the classical concept from individual speakers to institutional actors. An organization's credibility is not the aggregate of its individual communicators' credibility; it is the impression formed by audiences across all observable organizational behavior — what every function says, what every function does, and whether those signals cohere. This is why governance is the required response: individual communicators may each maintain personal credibility while the organization's aggregate signal destroys institutional credibility.

### Logos: The Logical Structure of the Argument

Logos refers to the argument itself — the claim, the evidence, and the reasoning that connects them. In the classical tradition, rhetorical reasoning takes two primary forms:

- **The enthymeme** (*enthumēma*): the rhetorical syllogism. A deductive argument in which one or more premises may be left unstated because they are assumed to be shared by the audience. Effective rhetorical argument does not begin from first principles; it begins from what the audience already believes and builds to the conclusion. The rhetorician must therefore understand not only the logical structure of valid arguments but the belief structure of the target audience.

- **The example** (*paradeigma*): rhetorical induction. Reasoning from particular cases to general conclusions. The example is particularly powerful when the audience can recognize the particular case as real and analogous to their own situation.

Both forms require that the argument be *valid*. This is the point at which the rhetorical tradition draws its sharpest line against sophistical practice: the enthymeme must proceed from true premises; the example must accurately represent the case from which it reasons. An argument that is formally valid but built on false premises is not rhetoric — it is manipulation. The distinction is not rhetorical decoration; it is the structural guarantee of the persuasive act's legitimacy.

For SCI, logos is the foundation of the Talking Point Framework. A messaging framework constructed without evidence is sophistry — it may be superficially persuasive, but it is neither defensible under scrutiny nor durable over time. The SCI evidence lifecycle — continuous ingestion, AI-assisted citation extraction, human validation by record owners — is the institutional mechanism that ensures logos is grounded in verified, traceable intelligence.

### Pathos: The Emotional State of the Audience

Pathos refers to the audience's emotional state and its relevance to the reception of arguments. Aristotle dedicates the entirety of *Rhetoric* Book II to a systematic analysis of emotions — fear, confidence, anger, calmness, shame, indignation, envy, emulation — each treated in terms of the conditions under which it arises and the dispositions it creates in those who experience it.

This treatment is frequently misread as a manual for emotional manipulation. It is not. Aristotle's point is epistemological: the same argument, in different emotional contexts, will be received differently. An audience in a state of fear attends to different aspects of a situation than an audience in a state of confidence. An audience that feels wronged is less likely to engage deliberatively with counterarguments. Understanding the audience's emotional state is a prerequisite for effective communication — not because the rhetorician exploits emotions, but because emotions are constitutive of the frame within which arguments are evaluated.

For SCI, pathos maps onto the depth of stakeholder intelligence required to develop effective Talking Point Frameworks. A messaging framework constructed without understanding the stakeholder's values, concerns, historical grievances, and emotional relationship to the issue will misfire — not because the arguments are invalid, but because they are not pitched to the audience that needs to receive them. This is why SCI's stakeholder intelligence records must capture not just factual information (title, organization, decision authority) but the stakeholder's orientation: their interests, their concerns, their track record, and the nature of their relationship to the organization's objectives.

## Part III: Kairos and the Audience as Arbiter

### Kairos: The Right Moment

*Kairos* — often translated as "the opportune moment" — is one of the most important and least formalized concepts in the classical rhetorical tradition. Where logos provides the content of effective argument and ethos and pathos shape its reception, kairos describes the full constellation of contextual conditions under which any given argument is or is not receivable.

Kairos is not merely timing, though timing is central to it. It encompasses the appropriate *mode* of argument (written or oral, formal or informal, public or private), the appropriate *channel* (through which media or intermediaries), the appropriate *moment* in the relationship between communicator and audience, and the appropriate stage in the deliberative process within which the audience is operating.

An argument that is logically valid, emotionally resonant, and delivered by a credible source will still fail if it arrives after the audience has already committed to a contrary position, or before the audience has reached the stage of deliberation where it is receptive to the kind of argument being made, or through a channel the audience does not trust, or in a mode that signals the wrong relationship between communicator and audience.

For SCI, kairos maps onto the Activity record and its relationship to the intelligence architecture. The decision about *when* and *how* to engage a stakeholder — through what channel, at what moment in their decision process, with what level of formality, through what intermediaries — is a kairotic judgment. It requires deep situational awareness of the stakeholder's current posture, the current state of the relevant decision processes, the recent history of the relationship, and the broader informational environment in which the engagement will occur. This intelligence base does not make kairotic judgment automatic; it makes it possible.

### The Audience as Arbiter of Meaning

One of the classical tradition's most important and least appreciated contributions is its insistence that the *receiver*, not the sender, is the ultimate arbiter of meaning. The JIC expresses this precisely: "Communication occurs only when the signal is received and interpreted, so it is not sufficient merely to 'get our message out.'"

The receiver interprets signals through their own frame of reference, their existing beliefs, their values, their emotional state, and their relationship to the source. The same signal will be interpreted differently by different audiences. A message designed for one audience may produce unintended effects in another. In complex stakeholder environments, where every organizational signal reaches multiple audiences simultaneously, control of meaning is impossible — management of signal coherence is the best achievable goal.

This framing has direct implications for SCI's design. Because meaning is determined by audiences, not senders, the organization must invest in *audience intelligence* — deep knowledge of each stakeholder's interpretive framework — rather than simply in message crafting. The Talking Point Framework is not a set of statements to be broadcast; it is a structured set of arguments designed with specific audiences' interpretive frameworks in mind. And because the same action will be received by multiple audiences with different frames, the governance function must assess not just "what are we saying to Stakeholder A?" but "how will Stakeholder B, C, and D interpret what we're doing and saying to Stakeholder A?"

## Part IV: The Sophistry Problem and the Ethical Constraint

No treatment of classical rhetoric as a foundation for governance is complete without addressing the sophistry problem — the possibility that rhetorical skill is deployed for manipulation rather than persuasion in service of truth.

### The Sophistical Corruption

The Sophists of the fifth century BC — Gorgias, Protagoras, Thrasymachus and others — taught the art of argument as a commercial skill, available to anyone who could pay. Their aim was not truth but victory: making the weaker argument appear stronger, winning the crowd regardless of the merits. This was, and remains, the popular association with the word "rhetoric" — a pejorative for the deployment of persuasive technique in the service of misleading audiences.

Plato's response, articulated most forcefully in the *Gorgias* and the *Phaedrus*, was to reject rhetoric as categorically corrupt and to call for its replacement by philosophical dialectic. The Platonic position holds that genuine knowledge, not persuasion, should be the basis for public decisions; that rhetoric is the art of flattering audiences rather than educating them; and that its practice is incompatible with serious commitment to truth.

### The Aristotelian Resolution

Aristotle's position is more nuanced and more defensible. He acknowledges that rhetoric, like any powerful faculty, can be misused — that the same skills that enable one to make a true case persuasively also enable one to make a false case persuasively. But he draws two conclusions that Plato does not:

First, this is not unique to rhetoric. Medicine can be used to harm as well as heal. Logic can be deployed in service of deception as well as truth. The possibility of misuse does not condemn a faculty; it imposes an ethical constraint on its practitioner.

Second, the constraint is not merely ethical but *practical*. Deceptive rhetoric is ultimately self-defeating. An organization (or a speaker) that builds its credibility on false premises, misleading arguments, or the manufacture of impressions disconnected from reality will be discovered, eventually. When the discovery occurs, the damage to ethos is severe and durable. The audience that feels it has been manipulated is not merely unconvinced; it is actively hostile. It interprets all subsequent communications through the lens of anticipated deception. The organization that invests in genuine credibility — through consistent alignment of stated commitments and observed actions, through evidence-grounded argument, through honest acknowledgment of uncertainty and limitations — builds an asset that compounds over time.

This is not idealism. It is a structural claim about how credibility works: deception is a strategy for short-term persuasive gain at the cost of long-term persuasive capacity.

### Implications for SCI

The sophistry problem has direct architectural implications for the SCI framework, and they are worth making explicit.

SCI's insistence on **evidence-backed intelligence** — on grounding every assessment in validated, traceable citations — is not merely an epistemological preference. It is the institutional operationalization of the Aristotelian constraint. A governance framework that allowed Talking Point Frameworks to be constructed without reference to validated evidence would be a tool for organizational sophistry: the efficient coordination of misleading communications across distributed actors. SCI is explicitly designed to prevent this. Human validation of AI-extracted citations, audit-trail traceability from assessments back to sources, and the distribution of record ownership to subject-matter experts closest to the evidence — these design choices are the architectural expression of the commitment to honesty that classical rhetoric requires.

SCI's governance framework is designed to support the development and consistent deployment of honest, credible, evidence-grounded communication — not the manufacture of misleading impressions. This is not a constraint imposed on the framework from outside; it is a structural requirement of the framework's own foundational theory.

## Part V: From Individual Acts to Organizational Rhetoric — The Governance Gap

Classical rhetoric was theorized at the level of individual speakers addressing specific audiences in defined communicative contexts — the assembly, the court, the ceremonial occasion. The theory is rich and precise at this scale. Its application to organizational communication requires one significant extension: the shift from individual to institutional rhetoric, and the governance problem that shift creates.

An organization communicates through many actors simultaneously: executives, legal counsel, government affairs staff, communications teams, business development personnel, operational management, community relations offices. Each of these actors engages with external stakeholders in ways that constitute communication. And because organizations operate in complex stakeholder environments with many overlapping audiences, any given stakeholder is receiving signals from multiple organizational actors — sometimes simultaneously, sometimes in rapid sequence, sometimes in direct contradiction.

The audience does not experience these signals as discrete communications from distinct organizational actors. It constructs an integrated impression: an organizational ethos, an organizational logos, an organizational pathos. It asks, implicitly: does this organization know what it's doing? Does it care about our interests? Is it telling us the truth? The answers are constructed from the full range of observed organizational behavior, not from any single communication.

This is why individual rhetorical skill — however high its level — is necessary but insufficient for organizational credibility. An organization of excellent individual communicators, each deploying their skills without coordination, will produce an aggregate signal that is internally inconsistent, and internal inconsistency is interpreted by audiences as institutional incompetence, bad faith, or dishonesty. The three failures of ethos, in precisely the terms Aristotle specifies.

The governance framework SCI provides is the structural response to this problem. It does not replace individual communicative judgment; it creates the intelligence architecture, the coordination mechanisms, and the alignment cycles within which individual communicative judgment can be deployed coherently at organizational scale.

## Summary: The Classical Rhetorical Tradition and the SCI Framework

The correspondences between classical rhetoric and SCI's design are not analogical; they are foundational. Each core rhetorical construct maps onto a specific SCI design choice:

| Rhetorical Construct | SCI Design Expression |
|---|---|
| Ethos — credibility built through consistent action | Say-do gap prevention through governance; Activity tracking; alignment of operational signals with stated commitments |
| Logos — valid argument grounded in evidence | Talking Point Frameworks built on validated evidence citations; AI Assist grounded in verified intelligence records |
| Pathos — resonance with audience values and concerns | Stakeholder intelligence records capturing interests, orientation, history, and decision context |
| Kairos — the opportune moment and mode | Activity records linked to posture assessments; engagement timing informed by stakeholder intelligence |
| Audience primacy | Stakeholder-specific Talking Point Frameworks; posture as the central unit of assessment |
| The anti-sophistry constraint | Human-validated evidence; audit-trail traceability; governance design that makes honest communication structurally effective |
| Institutional rhetoric | Distributed ownership and alignment cycle as the governance mechanism for coherent organizational communication |

The classical tradition contributes something that neither RDT nor the JIC framework provides: a theory of *why* some communications persuade and others fail at the level of the communicative act itself. Without this theory, the governance framework SCI provides would be mechanism without grounding — structure without an account of what the structure is organized to produce. With it, every architectural choice in SCI can be understood as the institutional expression of a theoretical principle that has been tested in practice across twenty-five centuries of human communicative life.

# Freeman’s Stakeholder Theory as a Normative Foundation for Strategic Communications Intelligence (SCI)

## Executive Summary

Strategic Communications Intelligence (SCI) requires two kinds of justification for why stakeholders must be treated as strategically central:

1) **Structural justification (power and dependence):** stakeholders matter because they can materially affect an organization’s ability to achieve objectives by controlling or influencing critical resources and constraints (Resource Dependence Theory; Pfeffer & Salancik).

2) **Normative justification (legitimate claims):** stakeholders matter because they possess morally and socially legitimate interests that management must consider as part of responsible organizational conduct (Freeman’s Stakeholder Theory and later stakeholder ethics).

This document develops the **normative pillar** and shows how it harmonizes with the structural pillar without collapsing SCI into generic “stakeholder engagement” practice. The central claim is that **SCI is a governance framework** for strategic communications: it institutionalizes the continuous, evidence-backed coordination of organizational signals (words and actions) so that an organization can influence independent stakeholders ethically and effectively.

---

## 1. Freeman’s Foundational Move: The Firm as a Nexus of Stakeholder Relationships

R. Edward Freeman’s *Strategic Management: A Stakeholder Approach* (1984) reframed the firm from a closed system oriented primarily to shareholder returns into an **open system of relationships**.

Freeman’s canonical definition is intentionally broad: a stakeholder is **“any group or individual who can affect or is affected by the achievement of the organization’s objectives.”** This definition matters for SCI because it corresponds to the platform’s starting premise: strategic outcomes often depend on independent external actors whose decisions cannot be commanded.

**Key implication for SCI:** stakeholder management is not an auxiliary communications activity; it is the management of conditions under which objectives become achievable.

---

## 2. The Normative Core: Rejecting the “Separation Thesis”

A decisive contribution of Freeman’s tradition is its rejection of the **Separation Thesis**: the idea that business decisions can be cleanly separated from ethical decisions.

In Freeman’s view, management is unavoidably moral because it involves choices that predictably shape the welfare, rights, and dignity of other persons and groups.

### 2.1. What the Separation Thesis Rejection Means for SCI

If business and ethics cannot be separated, then neither can **strategy** and **strategic communication** be separated from ethical obligations.

SCI’s governance framing follows directly:

- A platform that coordinates signals purely for effectiveness (while remaining agnostic to truth and fairness) becomes an instrument for manipulation.
- A platform that coordinates signals **grounded in validated evidence**, and that is designed to prevent the say–do gap, is an institutional mechanism for ethical persuasion: it makes honesty and coherence structurally easier than deception and incoherence.

This is why SCI must explicitly state (and design for) the principle that its governance supports **honest, credible, evidence-grounded communication — not the manufacture of misleading impressions**.

---

## 3. Descriptive, Instrumental, and Normative Stakeholder Theory (and Why SCI Needs All Three)

Stakeholder theory is commonly presented as having three nested perspectives:

- **Descriptive:** how organizations in fact identify and deal with stakeholders.
- **Instrumental:** how stakeholder management affects performance (risk, legitimacy, revenue, access).
- **Normative:** how organizations *ought* to treat stakeholders.

SCI necessarily uses all three, but it is crucial to keep them conceptually distinct:

- SCI’s **intelligence records** operationalize descriptive facts (who the stakeholders are, what they’ve done, what they believe, what evidence supports our assessment).
- SCI’s **governance cycle** (e.g., the Strategic Communications Brief) supports instrumental outcomes (alignment, risk reduction, improved conditions for objective achievement).
- SCI’s **evidence lifecycle and validation rules** implement normative constraints (truthfulness, traceability, accountability).

**Key point:** SCI is not “stakeholder capitalism software.” It is a governance architecture that allows organizations to act coherently and ethically in environments where stakeholder influence determines outcomes.

## 4. Resource Dependence Theory (RDT) as the Structural Complement

RDT explains why organizations must attend to external stakeholders even when they would prefer not to: organizations depend on resources and constraints they do not control.

RDT’s power-dependence logic can be summarized: a stakeholder’s power over an organization is proportional to the organization’s dependence on that stakeholder (criticality, scarcity, lack of alternatives, control over access, legitimacy as a resource).

### 4.1. Why Freeman and RDT Must Be Integrated (and Why Neither Is Sufficient Alone)

- **RDT alone** can rationalize purely power-driven engagement (“listen only to the powerful”), which can become strategically brittle as legitimacy dynamics shift and coalitions form.
- **Normative stakeholder theory alone** can become operationally indeterminate (“everyone has claims”), collapsing prioritization and producing paralysis.

SCI’s position is synthesis: stakeholders are strategically relevant because they have **both** (a) the power to affect outcomes and (b) legitimate claims that constrain what ethical influence may do.

## 5. The Bridge Framework: Mitchell–Agle–Wood Stakeholder Salience

Mitchell, Agle, and Wood (1997) offer a practical bridge between normativity and structuralism by defining stakeholder salience through three attributes:

1) **Power** (capacity to influence the organization)
2) **Legitimacy** (social, moral, or legal appropriateness of the claim)
3) **Urgency** (time-sensitivity and criticality)

This model is directly useful for SCI because it gives a principled way to prioritize stakeholder attention without reducing prioritization to brute force.

### 5.1. Salience Typology (Operational Use in SCI)

- **Latent stakeholders** (one attribute): monitor; create watch conditions for shifts.
- **Expectant stakeholders** (two attributes): proactive engagement; reduce drift and prevent escalation.
- **Definitive stakeholders** (three attributes): immediate and comprehensive action; senior ownership; high evidence discipline.

**SCI implication:** salience should be expressed not only as a label but as *workflow consequences*: escalation paths, review cadence, and required evidence quality thresholds.

### 5.2. “Dangerous” Stakeholders

The “dangerous” category (power + urgency without legitimacy) is important for SCI’s ethical posture. A purely RDT lens would treat such actors as simply high threat. A Freeman-informed lens allows SCI to say: legitimacy matters; the organization may need to defend normative stakeholders and its own legitimacy while still mitigating the risk.

## 6. Normative vs. Derivative Stakeholders (Phillips) and the Anti-Dilution Principle

A persistent practical failure in stakeholder discourse is dilution: “everyone is a stakeholder,” therefore no one is prioritized.

Robert Phillips’ distinction helps:

- **Normative stakeholders:** those to whom the organization owes direct obligations grounded in fairness and mutually accepted cooperation (e.g., employees, customers, suppliers, owners in many contexts).
- **Derivative stakeholders:** those who may not have direct moral claims but can significantly affect the organization and its normative stakeholders (e.g., certain activists, media actors, competitors).

**SCI implication:** this distinction supports a governance rule: do not sacrifice normative stakeholders merely to appease derivative pressure — while still treating derivative stakeholders as strategically consequential.

---

## 7. Stakeholder Influence Strategies (Frooman) and Network Effects

Stakeholders influence organizations through identifiable mechanisms, often determined by dependence structure:

- **Direct withholding** (stop providing a resource)
- **Direct usage** (continue providing a resource but attach conditions)
- **Indirect withholding** (work through an ally to withhold)
- **Indirect usage** (work through an ally to attach conditions)

SCI’s stakeholder intelligence must therefore be **network-aware**: many decisive pressures are indirect, coalition-driven, and mediated through third parties.

## 8. Two Canonical Cases (Why the Synthesis Matters)

The following cases are frequently cited in stakeholder scholarship because they demonstrate how structural legitimacy and normative legitimacy interact.

### 8.1. Shell and Brent Spar (1995)

Shell’s plan to dispose of the Brent Spar platform had regulatory approval and technical justification. Yet the organization failed to account for broader normative legitimacy as perceived by publics and civil society actors.

A derivative stakeholder (Greenpeace) mobilized coalitional pressure (consumer boycott dynamics), converting reputational legitimacy into a resource constraint. The result illustrates a core SCI claim: **technical and legal permission are insufficient when broader audiences interpret action as illegitimate**.

### 8.2. Nike and Labor Practices (1990s)

Nike’s supply chain strategy reduced cost dependence but exposed the firm to legitimacy dependence. Factory workers lacked direct power; legitimacy and coalition networks supplied it.

The strategic lesson is not “be perfect.” It is: **where legitimacy is a critical resource, the say–do gap is existential**. Governance is required to prevent it.

## 9. What This Means for SCI (and What It Does *Not* Mean)

This artifact supports SCI’s intellectual framework in three precise ways:

1) **Ethical constraint:** stakeholder theory supplies a moral basis for truthfulness, fairness, and non-manipulative engagement.
2) **Governance necessity:** when ethics and strategy are inseparable, governance mechanisms are required to institutionalize coherence (not mere messaging).
3) **Prioritization logic:** salience models and normative/derivative distinctions provide principled prioritization under conflict.

### What it does *not* mean

- It does not mean SCI is a generic external engagement toolkit.
- It does not mean SCI’s purpose is to satisfy every claim.
- It does not mean the organization’s only obligation is to powerful stakeholders.

SCI’s distinctive claim remains: **governance of independent stakeholder intelligence and alignment** — grounded in evidence and constrained by ethics.

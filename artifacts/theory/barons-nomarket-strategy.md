# Strategic Convergence: David Baron's Nonmarket Strategy and the Governance of Communications Intelligence

The evolution of corporate strategy has shifted from a singular focus on market competition to a dual-pronged approach that recognizes the significance of the environment beyond traditional buyer-seller transactions. Central to this evolution is the work of David Baron, whose concept of the nonmarket environment provided the first rigorous academic framework for what is now understood as Strategic Communications Intelligence (SCI). At its core, Baron's Integrated Strategy posits that a firm's success is determined not only by its market actions-such as product development, pricing, and distribution-but also by its nonmarket actions, which involve interactions with government, media, civil society, and the public. These two components must be integrated into a single strategy to achieve a sustainable competitive advantage. This analysis explores the deep theoretical alignment between Baron's nonmarket strategy and the Strategic Communications Intelligence framework, illustrating how the former's academic rigor informs the latter's commercial application through the governance of stakeholder posture.

## Foundations of Integrated Strategy and the Nonmarket Environment

The concept of integrated strategy emerged from the recognition that firms operate as open systems, deeply embedded in social, political, and legal institutions. David Baron defined the nonmarket environment as the set of social, political, and legal arrangements that structure a firm's interactions outside of, and in conjunction with, markets. While market strategy focuses on creating value through economic transactions and the exchange of property, nonmarket strategy addresses the challenges and opportunities presented by institutions that operate through majority rule, due process, and collective action.

The fundamental claim of integrated strategy is that market and nonmarket components are not merely adjacent; they are interdependent. A nonmarket action, such as lobbying for a specific regulatory standard, can fundamentally reshape the market environment in which the firm competes. Conversely, a firm's market activities often generate nonmarket issues, such as environmental concerns or labor disputes, which then require a strategic nonmarket response to protect the firm's legitimacy and market position.

Strategic Communications Intelligence adopts this premise but elevates it to a governance mandate. SCI argues that because organizations are dependent on independent stakeholders whom they cannot command, they must manage these power relations through influence grounded in credibility. This "dependence without authority" is the commercial reality of Baron's nonmarket environment. The failure to integrate these components leads to what SCI calls the "say-do" gap-a structural failure where an organization's actions in one domain contradict its communications in another, thereby destroying its strategic assets of reputation and legitimacy.

The nonmarket environment includes all the social and political relationships and contexts in which businesses operate. It is characterized by characteristics such as majority rule, due process, broad enfranchisement, and publicness, which differ significantly from the efficiency-driven logic of the market. In the nonmarket environment, transactions are structured by social, political, and legal arrangements and institutions. This includes government policies, regulations, public opinion, NGOs, media, and social activists.

## The 4I Framework: Analyzing the Nonmarket Matrix

To navigate the complexity of the nonmarket environment, Baron introduced the 4I framework: Issues, Institutions, Interests, and Information. This framework serves as the diagnostic tool for nonmarket strategy, allowing managers to dissect the social and political landscape with the same rigor applied to market analysis.

## Issues as the Unit of Analysis

In Baron's framework, "Issues" are the basic units of nonmarket action. They represent points of controversy or disagreement between two or more parties that can impact a firm's performance. Nonmarket issues are "everything outside the direct buyer-seller relationship" that can shape business success, including laws, politics, society, ethics, and media. Examples include sustainability, health and safety, human rights, corporate social responsibility, and regulation. The Strategic Communications Intelligence framework views these issues as the drivers of stakeholder posture. An issue is not a static problem to be solved but a dynamic field where the organization's credibility is constantly tested.

## Institutions and the Rules of the Game

Institutions are the entities that provide the rules and arenas in which nonmarket issues are addressed. These include formal institutions like legislatures, regulatory agencies, and courts, as well as informal institutions like the news media and public opinion. Institutions are the constructs that are shaping the environment and organizational behavior. They outline the "rules of the game" that determine the economic and non-economic behavior of firms. Together with market mechanisms, nonmarket constraints create order and reduce uncertainty in exchanges. In the nonmarket environment, competition is intermediated by these institutions rather than by the price mechanism. Understanding the specific logic of an institution-such as the "due process" of a court versus the "audience interest" of a newsroom-is critical for designing an effective strategy.

## Interests and Stakeholder Dynamics

Interests are the individuals and groups with a stake in a particular issue. This includes organized groups like corporations, associations, and trade unions, as well as unorganized groups like consumers and the general public. The outcome of nonmarket competition among interest groups is viewed as depending on the configuration of economic interests and formal political institutions. Policymakers seek constituent support in the form of votes and campaign contributions, and firms and other NGOs supply these resources in exchange for political support. SCI expands this by defining stakeholders through the lens of Resource Dependence Theory, identifying which actors control the resources (legal, financial, or reputational) that the organization needs to survive.

## Information as a Strategic Asset

Information refers to what the interested parties know or believe about an issue and the potential consequences of various actions. It pertains to the relation between actions and consequences and about the preferences and capabilities of the interested parties. Nonmarket strategy is, at its heart, a competition over information and beliefs. Managers use information to shape the preferences of policymakers, the narratives of the media, and the perceptions of the public. In the Strategic Communications Intelligence framework, this "Information" component is evolved into "Intelligence"-the continuous monitoring and assessment of stakeholder posture based on observable evidence and the coordination of organizational signals.

## The Nonmarket Issue Life Cycle and Managerial Anticipation

The Nonmarket Issue Life Cycle model describes how an issue evolves from initial identification to final enforcement, providing a timeline for strategic intervention. The life cycle typically consists of five stages:
1.	Issue Identification: The problem or concern is first recognized, often triggered by a technological change, a social trend, or a specific event.
2.	Interest Group Formation: Concerned parties organize to exert pressure, often involving activists, NGOs, and the media.
3.	Legislation: The issue enters the formal political process, where rules and laws are debated and formulated in institutions like legislatures.
4.	Administration: The resulting laws are translated into specific regulations and guidelines by government agencies and administrative bodies.
5.	Enforcement: This final stage involves the implementation of the rules, monitoring of compliance, and the imposition of sanctions for violations.

The Strategic Communications Intelligence framework emphasizes that the firm's ability to influence the outcome decreases as the issue moves through the life cycle. Managers must anticipate and analyze nonmarket issues using frameworks such as the Four I's to avoid being managed by the environment. SCI governance aims to provide the visibility necessary to identify issues at the earliest possible stage, utilizing intelligence records to track shifts in stakeholder posture.

## Private Politics: Civil Society and the Radical Flank Effect

While traditional nonmarket strategy often focused on public politics-interactions with government institutions-David Baron also pioneered the study of "private politics". Private politics occurs when activists, NGOs, and social movements seek to change a firm's behavior directly through social pressure, without relying on government intervention.

In this arena, the Strategic Communications Intelligence framework's domain of Civil Society is central. Activists are demanders of policy changes, but instead of lobbying legislatures, they target individual firms. Baron's research identified the "Radical Flank Effect," where a confrontational activist's presence can increase the bargaining power of a cooperative activist. The greater the threat from confrontational activists, the more cooperative activists can accomplish with the firms they engage, as firms compete to win engagements with cooperative activists to obtain a "shield" from more extreme targeting.

Farsighted firms anticipate activist-led private politics and often choose to self-regulate in an attempt to forestall activist campaigns. The SCI framework integrates these insights by treating civil society actors as independent stakeholders whose posture must be managed through continuous intelligence. By understanding these dynamics, an organization can move beyond simple crisis management to a state of institutionalized preparedness.

## SCI and the Governance of Posture

The Strategic Communications Intelligence framework argues that strategic communications must be a strategic governance function, not just a communications function. SCI serves as the institutional mechanism to maintain continuous intelligence on stakeholder posture and coordinate all organizational signals based on evidence. The root of the problem for many organizations is the structural failure of coordination, leading to the "say-do" gap where different parts of an organization send contradictory signals.

SCI's central claim is that because organizations are open systems dependent on external actors for resources and legitimacy, they must manage power relations through influence grounded in exchange and credibility. The core construct is "Posture"-the integrated disposition of a stakeholder toward the organization, evidenced by observable behaviors. Posture is strategic and specific to objectives, and it is the metric that changes when influence succeeds or degrades when the say-do gap widens.

The SCI governance mandate includes visibility into stakeholder posture and organizational activity, distributed accountability for intelligence records, and evidence traceability from assessments back to sources. It also involves recurring alignment cycles, such as the weekly Strategic Communications Brief, to ensure all instruments of organizational power are synchronized.

## Intellectual Lineage and Theoretical Synthesis

The SCI framework is synthesized from three primary traditions:
1.	Classical Rhetoric: Focusing on Ethos (credibility), Logos (logical argument), and Pathos (resonance with audience values), while emphasizing Kairos (timing and context).
2.	Resource Dependence Theory (RDT): Providing the rationale for which stakeholders matter based on the criticality of the resources they control, and treating credibility and legitimacy as strategic assets.
3.	DoD Strategic Communications Framework: Contributing the operational approach of synchronizing communications with all instruments of organizational power through an adaptive cycle of understanding, design, monitoring, and iteration.

This lineage highlights the evolution of strategic thinking from isolated communicative acts to a comprehensive governance system. Baron's work at Stanford on integrated strategy is the closest academic precursor to this commercial framing, as his argument for the integration of nonmarket and market strategies mirrors SCI's core claim.

## Comparative Analysis of Stakeholder Domains

David Baron's concept of the nonmarket environment-consisting of government, media, civil society, and the public-is essentially equivalent to the four stakeholder domains in the SCI framework.

### The Government Domain

This domain includes formal policymaking institutions, regulatory agencies, and the judiciary. In nonmarket strategy, firms engage with government officials to influence legislation and regulation that impacts their market environment. The SCI framework emphasizes that because the organization lacks authority over these regulators, it must manage its posture through influence grounded in credibility and evidence.

### The Media Domain

The news media and social media play a critical role in providing information to the public and framing nonmarket issues. Baron's research noted that the media can influence firms and manipulate policy makers by providing information or mobilizing public opinion. SCI views the media as a key arena for the "say-do" gap, where inconsistent organizational signals are most likely to be exposed and amplified, damaging the organization's legitimacy.

### The Civil Society Domain

This domain encompasses interest groups, NGOs, and activists who use private politics to influence corporate behavior. These actors are demanders of change and can impose significant costs through boycotts and protests. Managing posture in this domain requires a deep understanding of the configurations of these groups and their social networks.

### The Public Domain

The public domain consists of unorganized stakeholders whose opinion and social pressure can shape business success. Factors such as public opinion and ethical concerns are central to the nonmarket environment. SCI focuses on the resonance of organizational signals with audience values (Pathos) to maintain social legitimacy and political approval.

## Information as Intelligence: Evolution of a Strategic Asset

A critical aspect of both frameworks is the role of information. In Baron's 4I framework, information refers to what parties know or believe about the consequences of actions. It is a strategic asset that can be used to influence the "rules of the game". SCI evolves this concept into a continuous intelligence function. Instead of static data, SCI requires "Strategic Communications Intelligence"-an institutionalized mechanism to maintain constant visibility into stakeholder posture and coordinate all organizational signals.

Modern cloud platforms and digital technologies offer new opportunities for real-time monitoring and threat intelligence sharing. Digitalization can fill knowledge gaps and improve coordination and stakeholder engagement. This allows for a more "socialized" view of non-market strategy, where firms can track the complex socio-political networks and stakeholder fields in which they are embedded. The transition from information to intelligence is a shift from treating data as a static resource to a dynamic governance imperative.

## Integration in Practice: Synergies and Challenges

Baron argued that a synergy is present between market and nonmarket strategies if a nonmarket action increases the return from a market action. For example, a firm might use nonmarket strategy to shape its market environment, such as lobbying for legislation to lower trade barriers. Effective integration requires that the managers responsible for market performance also be responsible for the firm's performance in its nonmarket environment.

However, achieving this integration is often challenging. Most studies still examine market and nonmarket activities in isolation. Research has also identified a deeper structural problem: managerial political connections — the dominant form of person-dependent nonmarket strategy — can be a liability rather than an asset. When nonmarket strategy is vested in the relationships of specific executives, the organization becomes exposed to several risks: those relationships are not transferable, they create uneven coverage across the full stakeholder landscape, they generate undocumented commitments that other parts of the organization may inadvertently contradict, and they produce exactly the kind of incoherent aggregate signal that destroys institutional credibility.

This finding is not an argument against nonmarket engagement. It is an argument against **person-dependent** nonmarket engagement — and therefore an argument for institutionalized governance. An organization whose nonmarket posture management is embedded in structured processes, intelligence records, distributed accountability, and alignment cycles does not become more or less effective when individual executives change. Its nonmarket capability is organizational, not personal. This is the problem SCI is designed to solve, and Baron's own literature provides the warrant for the solution.

## Nonmarket Capability as a Governance Requirement

A critical implication of Baron's integrated strategy framework - one that is directly relevant to SCI's architecture - is that effective nonmarket strategy cannot be person-dependent. It must be developed as an **organizational capability**.

Baron recognized that the ability to analyze the nonmarket environment, identify issues early, map institutional interests, and deploy appropriate influence strategies is not a natural organizational endowment. It must be built deliberately. Organizations that rely on individual executives' political relationships or communications professionals' personal judgment - without institutionalized processes, intelligence, and accountability structures - are operating a person-dependent nonmarket strategy. Person-dependent strategies have a predictable failure mode: they are fragile (dependent on specific individuals), inconsistent (varying with personnel changes), and ungovernable (producing incoherent signals when multiple people act independently).

The SCI framework provides the institutional specification for nonmarket capability. Rather than treating nonmarket strategy as a function delegated to a government affairs team or a communications department, SCI embeds it as a governance function distributed across the organization - with structured intelligence records, evidence accountability, distributed ownership, and recurring alignment cycles. This is Baron's capability argument operationalized: the organization develops the capacity to engage its nonmarket environment continuously, coherently, and adaptively, rather than episodically and reactively.

### Conclusion: The Governance Imperative for Modern Organizations

The applicability of David Baron's Nonmarket Strategy to the Strategic Communications Intelligence framework is clear and profound. Baron's work provided the foundational academic logic for why the nonmarket environment matters and why it must be integrated with market strategy. The 4I framework and the concept of integrated strategy were the first to treat the social, political, and legal dimensions of business with the same strategic rigor as traditional market competition.

The SCI framework takes these foundations and provides the formal governance specification needed for modern commercial organizations. By shifting the focus from individual messaging acts to the institutionalized management of stakeholder posture, SCI solves the coordination problem that leads to the damaging "say-do" gap. It moves the discipline from "Information" as a strategic asset to "Intelligence" as a core governance function.

In an environment of dependence without authority, organizations must master the art of influence grounded in credibility. This requires visibility, accountability, and the continuous alignment of all organizational signals. David Baron's work remains the most significant academic precursor to this approach, and its synthesis with SCI provides a powerful model for navigating the complex stakeholder landscapes of the twenty-first century. The integration of market and nonmarket strategies is no longer just a theoretical ideal; it is a governance mandate for any organization seeking to sustain its legitimacy and competitive advantage in a globalized, hyper-connected world.

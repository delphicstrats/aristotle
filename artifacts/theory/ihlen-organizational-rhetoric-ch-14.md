Ethos and its Constitutive Role
in Organizational Rhetoric
James S. Baumlin and Peter L. Scisco
In simplest terms, ethos describes the character of a speaker, as revealed by means of speech.
We write “speech,” though contemporary discourse assumes all forms of audial, visual, and
written media; similarly, we refer to a traditional “speaker,” though the agents or purveyors of
contemporary discourse take numerous forms. The notion of “speaker” remains fluid in
organizational rhetoric, given our human tendency to project agency, ethics, and “voice” onto
corporate entities. We accept that organizations “speak,” and that they do so in ways that imper-
sonate responsible, and responsive, human agents (see also chapters 2 and 3 on organizational
communication, and 7 on organizational theory).
Ethos has no direct English equivalent: it can be paraphrased in its varied, nuanced uses, but
ἦθος itself remains untranslatable. Even with reference to “character,” our definition entails
more than “mere” ethics or moral character: credence, credibility, trustworthiness, “good
sense,” “good will,” expertise, authority, and charisma all of which adhere to this term, as do
identity, reputation, personality, and “personal style” or the qualities of character (such as habits
and tendencies, fears and desires, predispositions and prejudices). Within this range of meanings,
“character” may be ascribed to an individual or to a group. And though character is “revealed”
by speech, it is as likely to be “constructed”: in this respect, much organizational rhetoric aims
at creating, maintaining, and occasionally repairing corporate image.
Already our definition is proving simplistic. For discourse is dyadic—a rhetorical two‐way
street between speakers and audiences—and rhetoric is responsive in its intended effects: speakers
address audiences in order to gain their favor and assent and, often, their compliance. Until an
audience responds or complies in some manner, the dyadic structure of discourse remains unful-
filled. Hence, a “speaker” “speaks to” an audience in ways that “reveal”—or, alternatively, con-
struct—a credible character or image. Audiences are presumed to collaborate in fashioning or
affirming a speaker’s or corporate spokesperson’s or corporation’s image. Further, in seeking an
audience’s assent, a speaker must understand and appeal to that audience’s own character: that
is, to its values, its habits, its predispositions, its prejudices, its aspirations and idealized self‐
image. A premise of social psychology—one that this chapter accepts as a matter of course—is
that audiences tend to trust or “like” speakers who are similar to them in some way. A speaker’s
character may be “revealed” or “constructed,” but it might also be projected, and an audience’s
projections of trust and reliance are likely to be unconscious in their workings.
Applied to organizational rhetoric, we might ask: is ethos descriptive of a corporate reputation
that precedes discourse and is revealed by means of discourse? Or is it descriptive of corporate
image, an image that discourse constructs, even manipulates to an organization’s advantage?
What role does an audience’s ethos play in corporate reputation or image‐making? Do audiences
participate in affirming or in projecting corporate ethos? Is audience participation conscious or
unconscious? Such questions drive the discussion that follows.
The literature on organizational rhetoric is broad and well established. The literature on the
specific concept of ethos in the organizational context is less so, and within it can be found all of
the degrees of nuance previously mentioned. Themes of identity (Albert & Whetten, 1985;
Cheney, 1991) prevail, with other forays into legitimacy (Hearit, 1995), representation
(Crable, 1990), as well as issue management, (Cheney, Christensen, Conrad & Lair, 2004), risk
management (Kuhn, 1997), and other organizational priorities. Theory does not always address
ethos directly, but often shows it indirectly as a function of genre or an outcome of rhetorical
action. Students of organizational rhetoric acknowledge ethos in corporate communication,
image making, and advertising (see chapter 5 on marketing and chapter 15 on persona); recent
surveys tend, nonetheless, to skim the surface, giving a few paragraphs to different theorists and
ending with suggested applications. We too will end with suggestions for application and further
research; that said, studies of organizational rhetoric need a firmer, deeper grounding in ethos,
which this chapter aims to provide.
We focus here on three seminal theorists: Aristotle (384–322 bce), representing ancient
Greek rhetoric; Cicero (106–43 bce), representing Roman oratory; and Kenneth Burke (1897–
1993), representing modern communication theory. In outlining the role played by ethos in
organizational rhetoric, we begin with Aristotle and modulate to Cicero. Though not always
mentioned by name, and although their vocabularies are often remade for contemporary
purposes, still their insights inform both theory and praxis in organizational rhetoric. While
being studies in contrast, their works prove complementary. Each has its place in contemporary
organizational discourse.
Despite their abiding influence, contemporary praxis cannot be reduced to classical models.
A third theorist—one who revives and, at the same time, exceeds Aristotelian praxis—is Kenneth
Burke, whose concept of identification (see also chapter 8) binds audiences and rhetors together
by means of their shared interests and often hidden (that is, unconscious) motives.
Classical Contexts
Aristotle’s Rhetoric mediates between the two combatants in Greek education or paideia: the
Sophists, who promote rhetoric, and Plato’s Socrates, who promotes philosophy. Where Plato’s
Socrates looks for truth (aletheia) and being (ontos), the Sophist Protagoras finds mere custom
(nomos) arbitrary and changeable. As his fellow Sophist Gorgias described it, the world of
discourse consists of dissoi logoi—that is, “competing truths,” where there’s a pro and a con to
every argument and where either side is equally defensible.
Into this age‐old debate steps Aristotle: between the Sophist’s multiple, “competing truths”
and Plato’s singular, absolute truth, Aristotle seeks a contingent and probable truth, whose claims
rest in logos or “rational appeal” (see also chapter 1). Aristotle doesn’t deny that there are two
sides to any discussion and that rhetoric supplies arguments to both. But that doesn’t make rhet-
oric deceptive or immoral. Persuasion is neutral in its ethics. It makes its entrance wherever
human knowledge remains uncertain and where choices—of belief and of action—are demanded
of us, whether individually or collectively. Absolute knowledge remains a human impossibility;
there are, nonetheless, degrees of probability and likelihood. The rhetor’s purpose, thus, is to
“discover, in each case, the available means of persuasion” (Aristotle, 1991, 1.2, 1355b).
Following Plato, Aristotle’s Rhetoric is logos‐driven. But pathos or “emotional appeal” has its
place, since the rhetor seeks not simply to convince but to move an audience to action. Ethos
has its place as well, since the rhetor’s audience consists of non‐specialists whose lack of
knowledge makes them reliant upon the speaker’s expertise and trustworthiness. Following the
Sophists, then, Aristotle’s Rhetoric includes extra‐rational appeals as necessary components of
persuasion (see also chapter 23 on persuasion).
As outlined in Aristotle’s Rhetoric, ethos, pathos, and logos—the three entechnic pisteis or
“artistic proofs, or “modes of persuasion”—focus on different facets of the dynamic interplay
between rhetors and audiences. We can say that logos—aka “rational appeal” or use of “good
reasons”—“belongs” to the rhetor, though audiences judge its claims and respond by
questioning, resisting, or giving assent. (We use rhetor and speaker interchangeably in this
chapter and, for the moment, do not distinguish between individual speakers and corporate or
organizational entities; our use of “immediate discourse event” covers all forms of present‐
tense communication in all media.) Pathos—an appeal to the audience’s emotions—is raised by
a rhetor’s appeals, but it is the audience who reacts in hope or fear or courage or desire. In that
sense, pathos “belongs” to the audience and is elicited by the rhetor. In contrast, the ethos
constructed within the immediate discourse event (an important Aristotelian distinction) “lies
somewhere between” speaker and audience: belonging to neither wholly, the rhetor’s ethos is
built out of a speaker–audience transaction.
By “transaction,” we intend two meanings primarily. First, the proofs provide means by which
persuasion “does work” in the world. To change a mind, to dispose an audience to believe or to
comply or to purchase: these describe ways that rhetoric “transacts its business.” Put simply:
successful rhetoric does something to someone. Secondly, the proofs in general, and ethos in
particular, are themselves transactions to which audiences contribute. Audiences participate in
their own persuasion. They give as well as take, and what they “give” (or build) in respect to
ethos is the bond of trust that rhetors rely on when persuading.
Aristotle’s initial discussion of ethos (1991, 1.2.2) is worth careful study:
[There is persuasion] through character whenever the speech is spoken in such a way as to make the
speaker worthy of credence; for we believe fair‐minded people to a greater extent and more quickly
[than we do others] on all subjects in general and completely so in cases where there is not exact
knowledge but room for doubt. And this should result from the speech, not from a previous opinion
that the speaker is a certain kind of person; for it is not the case, as some of the technical writers
propose in their treatment of the art, that fair‐mindedness [epieikeia] on the part of the speaker
makes no contribution to persuasiveness; rather, character is almost, so to speak, the controlling
factor in persuading. (Aristotle, 1991, 1356a; emphasis added)
The purpose of Aristotelian ethos, thus, is to establish the rhetor’s authority over the issue
at hand and to gain a lay audience’s trust; with this trust in hand, the speaker can dispose the
audience to agreement and lead it toward policies supportive of that logos.
Aristotle makes a second assumption regarding audiences. Not only do they lack expert
knowledge of the issues at hand; they also lack knowledge of the speaker—specifically, of the
rhetor’s authority, moral character, and concern for the audience’s welfare. This assumes that the
audience has nothing but the speech to draw from in developing its impressions. Granted, modern
organizations can deviate from Aristotle’s model as they strive to make a successful “first impres-
sion” with diverse audiences. Publicity and reputation, for good or ill, precede most discourse
events. Still, an Aristotelian ethos maneuver rests in favorable “impressions” arising directly—
indeed, solely—from the discourse event.
It is time to complicate Aristotle’s model, since ethos extends to individuals and groups in the
greater polis or city‐state. On the one hand, a speaker’s ethos maneuvers are “artistic” and rhe-
torically constructed within an immediate discourse‐event; on the other hand, that same speaker
must address audiences of various ages, social classes, and ethnicities. Nowadays, we would call
this an attention to demographics. Even whole communities have distinctive customs and
“corporate character,” much as Athenians differed from Spartans—or Red Sox fans from Yankees
fans in US baseball.
Advertising, political polling, and other social‐ and media‐based industries assume some
model of group identity formation, group tendencies, and group preferences. Character typing
is regularly practiced in organizational discourse, whether external or internal. In hiring and
team building, for example, organizations have come increasingly to rely on models of “person-
ality type.” Most of these typologies belong to “popular literature” aimed at salespersons or
office managers who wish “to read” clients and workers more effectively. But several have been
studied and normed extensively, of which the Myers‐Briggs personality Type Indicator (MBTI)
is oldest and most widely accepted (see http://www.myersbriggs.org/).
From “Rational Ethos” to an “Ethos of Sympathy”
Whereas Aristotelian ethos allies itself with logos primarily, Cicero’s Roman equivalent, conciliare,
allies itself with pathos. This marks a shift in emphasis away from phronesis or “good sense” and
toward the “irrational” aspects of our collective human nature. There is nothing pathological
implied in our use of the term “irrational,” though we might use “extra‐rational” instead.
Put simply, the pathe derive from faculties other than reason, being emotive or appetitive in nature.
Of even greater import is the Roman orator’s conviction that ethos is “inherited” and revealed in
speech rather than “artistic” or constructed by speech. Among the Greeks, the position that something
should be done or believed because the great speaker says so is very rare” (Kennedy, 1972, p. 100;
emphasis added). Yet “it is common in Rome,” as Kennedy observed:
The orator brings into play all of his ancestry, his services to the state, his Roman virtues. … In
political oratory, if a man did not have ancestors to give him a claim to good character he was allowed
a good deal of freedom in dilating on his personal virtues. The lack of modesty which we sometimes
think of as a peculiar weakness of Cicero was a permanent feature of Roman oratory, especially in the
case of a new man like Cicero or Cato. (1972, pp. 100–101)
With obvious relevance to modern organizations, the Ciceronian model declares that ethos as
reputation remains constant over time, resistant to change, and difficult to dissemble or disguise.
The Romans believed that character remains essentially constant in man and therefore “demands
or determines his actions. Since character does not evolve or develop, but rather is bestowed or
inherited by nature, an individual cannot suddenly, or at will, change or disguise for any lengthy
period his ethos or way of life” (May, 1988, p. 6; emphasis added).
The Ciceronian implication is that in speaking one’s ethos will inevitably reveal itself. It goes
without saying, then, that “Cicero’s analysis of ethos is not, in its details, particularly
Aristotelian” (May, 1988, p. 4). William A. Grimaldi argued similarly, noting that ἦθος is not
to be found in the Latin tradition in the meaning we find in the Rhetoric (1980, p. 75).
Following Wisse (1989) and May (1988), we have called the Ciceronian conciliare an “ethos
of sympathy.” That is, rather than project an image of expertise and trustworthiness, Ciceronian
ethos displays or dramatizes a speaker’s “milder feelings,” of the sort that create bonds of
sympathy or likemindedness between speakers and audiences. Cicero’s later disciple, Quintilian
(1920–1922), elaborated:
Emotions fall into two classes; the one is called pathos by the Greeks and is rightly and correctly
expressed in Latin by adfectus (emotion); the other is called ethos [εθoς], a word for which in my
opinion Latin has no equivalent: it is however rendered by mores (morals) and consequently the
branch of philosophy known as ethics [ἦθικος] is styled moral philosophy by us. (6.2.8–9)
For the Romans, ethos and pathos both rest in an orator’s own emotional display, the difference
being one of degree rather than of kind. As Kennedy (1972) described it, “ethos is gentle
and mild and demonstrates the speaker’s moral character,” whereas “pathos consists of strong
emotions” (p. 505).
In other words, the Roman orator models the feelings, attitudes, and behaviors that he wishes
to evoke in his audience. Audiences are disposed to like some attitudes and behaviors while
disliking others: tapping into such predilections is precisely the Ciceronian task of conciliare. In
marked contrast, movere—the orator’s attempt at “moving” an audience to pity or terror or
courage—demands a more extreme display on the orator’s part. In his own person, the orator
must show courage in order to raise up courage, must show tears to elicit tears, and so on. An
“ethos of sympathy” works by a process of infection: what the orator displays is replicated in the
affective response of his audience.
A present‐day example from organizational rhetoric is General Electric’s (2014) television
commercial, Childlike Imagination – What My Mom Does at GE. As the commercial runs, we
watch a series of fantastic images: planes with wings like birds, trees that nod to passing
trains, moonlight‐powered undersea fans that produce energy. A young girl’s voiceover
narrates the scenes, describing them as things her mom does while working at General
Electric (GE). As viewers, we are transfixed by the fantastic images and the captivating sense
of fantasy, imagination, and the “all things possible” responses they generate. We take delight
in the girl’s descriptions. We are moved by the possibilities and participate in the fantastic
world GE enables. We are left anticipating what other fantastic things are coming, and, in the
best circumstances (for GE), we attribute our delight to the company who makes our enjoy-
ment possible.
As ethos in action, the Ciceronian model aims at “feelings” of “goodwill” (cf. Aristotle’s
eunoia), which are to be shared between speaker/advocate (and client) and audience. Describing
the role of character in forensic oratory specifically, Cicero’s Antonius noted:
Now feelings are won over by a man’s merit, achievement or reputable life, qualifications easier to
embellish, if only they are real, than to fabricate where nonexistent. But attributes useful in an
advocate are a mild tone, a countenance expressive of modesty, gentle language, and the faculty of
seeming to be dealing reluctantly and under compulsion with something you are really anxious to
prove. (Cicero, 2001, 2.182)
Antonius holds fast to the Roman precept that reputation precedes a speaker (or spokesperson, in
cases of advocacy) and that feigning character is difficult, though not impossible. The evidence
of upright character comes through displays of personality rather than claims of expertise. The
Ciceronian conciliare, thus, is broader and more inclusive than Aristotle’s ethos. Whereas
Aristotle’s system subordinates the speaker to the speech, the Roman system reverses this
emphasis. Quintilian’s famous vir bonus dicendi peritus—“the good man skilled in speaking”
(Quintilian, 1920–1922, 12.1.1)—reinforces this distinctively Roman notion, “for the orator
who gives the impression of being a bad man while he is speaking is actually speaking badly, since
his words seem to be insincere owing to the absence of ethos which would otherwise have
revealed itself” (6.2.18; emphasis added).
Aristotle or Cicero (or Both)?
Having described classical theory in its polarities, we consider its relevance to modern organiza-
tional rhetoric. Contemporary theory has resuscitated some Aristotelian terminology, while
Ciceronian assumptions remain operant in individual, political, and corporate image making—
though without acknowledgment, as Roman tradition has been largely forgotten.
Carroll (2013), for example, replicates much of the Ciceronian model: “A corporate reputa-
tion is broadly defined as a widely circulated, oft‐repeated message of minimal variation about
an organization revealing something about the organization’s nature” (p. 5). Note the stability
and repeatability of the corporate “message,” which is assumed to “reveal” (as opposed to “con-
struct,” à la Aristotle) an “organization’s nature.” In surveying theorists of “organizational
identity,” Hoffman and Ford (2010) returned to Roman concepts, as well. They noted organi-
zations’ sustained identity (Kuhn, 1997), a set of enduring unique features (Aust, 2004)
that form the base of an organization’s character (Albert & Whetten, 1985). Hoffman and
Ford emphasized how organizations work to maintain a consistent identity, as evident in the
organizational resources spent on preserving it even during times of transition and change
(2010, p. 121).
However, in distinguishing between “identity creation” and “identity maintenance,” Hoffman
and Ford brought both Greek and Roman models into play:
Once an organization has completed the difficult task of crafting a positive and distinct identity, it
must work to maintain that identity. Identity maintenance rhetoric consists of messages designed to
preserve or enhance the identity of the organization in the face of anticipated or unanticipated iden-
tity‐enhancing or ‐threatening events. Although similar to identity creation rhetoric, identity main-
tenance rhetoric lacks the elements of centrality and consistency over time. Identity maintenance
allows an organization to capitalize on information that may not be central to its identity or may not
hold true over a long period of time, but is consistent and supportive of a positive identity in the
immediate situation. (2010, p. 127)
Must we choose between “rational ethos” and an “ethos of sympathy”? A savvier approach
would look to the dialectic arising between these models. For students of organizational rhet-
oric, then, the question becomes: in any given occasion or exigency, which method applies?
Frankly, the Ciceronian model can never be argued away or ignored. Ours is a media-driven
culture obsessed with reputation. News outlets preside over it, entertainers hunger for it, busi-
nesses bank on it. In politics especially, character as reputation provides the fiercest and highest‐
stakes battleground in elections. And why not? Our own tendencies, à la Freud and Burke, would
have us “like” people who are “like ourselves.” Our reputations precede us and the past, presum-
ably, serves to predict the future: “crooked then, crooked now, crooked always,” right?
But it is Aristotle who offers the possibility that reputations can be repaired, even “reset.”
While common sense tells us that Cicero “gets it right,” we need to explain how it is that
Aristotle can be correct, too—if not in all instances, at least some of the time. An explanation
comes to us from modern depth psychology. Drawing its methods from the social sciences, com-
munication theory has sought quantitative and experimental data to test Aristotle’s inferences.
Surveying the empirical findings of modern research, James L. Kinneavy and Susan C.
Warshauer (1994) observed that the components of “credibility” in argument remain resiliently
Aristotelian. They cited work by C. I. Hovland, Irving Janis, and Harold Kelley (1959), who
argued that “reactions to a communication are significantly affected by cues as to the commu-
nicator’s intentions, expertness, and trustworthiness … qualities corresponding to good will,
good sense, and good moral character” (quoted in Kinneavy & Warshauer, 1994, p. 186).
Attempting a statistical reduction of the independent factors of an ethical argument, James C.
McCroskey (1966) argued similarly, concluding that “authoritativeness and character” are the
only important constituents of ethos (p. 72). Other theorists, such as Kenneth Andersen and
Theodore Clevenger (1963), intermingled Ciceronian and Aristotelian elements. Kinneavy and
Warshauer (1994) summarize Andersen and Clevenger’s argument thus: “the status of the
speaker, dress, voice and delivery, manner, and perceived sincerity” contribute to impact ethos,
while “giving both sides of a question, citing sources as evidence, offering conciliatory remarks,
and indulging in self‐praise” do not (p. 186).”
But, above all, it is to Freud—specifically, to his theory of the unconscious—that we owe our
understanding of the psychological processes underlying modern ethos maneuvers. J. Baumlin
and T. Baumlin (1994) explained that ethos is more than an audience’s recognition of cultural
values in the speaker—it is the “audience’s projection of authority and trustworthiness onto the
speaker, a projection that is triggered or elicited by the speaker but is otherwise supplied by the
audience” (p. 99).
As an introjection or internalization of “parental voices,” the Freudian superego manifests
itself—albeit unconsciously—whenever a speaker claims to speak “with authority.” As Baumlin
and Baumlin have noted, “all awe of and obedience to authority is to some degree implicated in
this unconscious process. The speaker, triggering mechanisms of projection and identification,
can gain an audience’s ‘filial’ sense of trust” (1994, p. 99).
It is by means of projection, then, that an audience comes to trust a speaker; and, without this
trust, persuasion is imperiled. If Baumlin and Baumlin’s thesis holds, then ethos as projection
becomes “the enabling ground” or, more precisely, the psychological precondition of persuasion.
Though unconscious of the role they play in their own persuasion, audiences must collaborate
with speakers, “making” the speakers in their own image (1994, pp. 99–100).
The longing “to belong” becomes heightened within postmodern culture, which loosens
traditional kinship ties even as it makes us all members of a global community. The modern
organization draws on our psychological need for membership; indeed, the modern organiza-
tion offers refuge from “the isolate self.” In The Psychology of the Transference, Jung described
the current state of affairs: “Everyone is now a stranger among strangers” (1969, para. 445).
Traditional communal ties, “as for instance in the early Christian communities,” once gave
comfort in the feeling of “belonging together.” For Jung, the urge to belong is instinctual; and
this urge “is not to be satisfied by any mere substitute such as a creed, party, nation, or state.
It wants the human connection” (Jung, 1969, para. 445).
Each one of us, indeed, is “a stranger among strangers.” A scan of our environment reveals a
landscape of diffuse interests and organizational order where divisions and allegiances mix, shift, and
drift into transitional patterns. Global institutionalization and corporate nation‐states erase the eth-
ical boundaries once relied on to define the values we once claimed, aspired to, and recognized in
one another. Still, we fashion identity in accordance with our relationships—our sense of belonging.
It seems that the rhetor’s “rational ethos” and “ethos of sympathy” are now subsumed within the
modern dynamics of speaker–audience projection and transference. While we have strayed from the
classical vocabulary, we will leave it to Burke to put these various strands back together.
Kenneth Burke to the Rescue
Burke develops his own vocabulary: such terms as identification and consubstantiality serve to
modernize and synthesize the Aristotelian/Ciceronian dialectic (see also chapter 8). In our
current corporate environment, where organizational structure obscures the notion of “speaker,”
neither Aristotle nor Cicero can explain the persuasive power of organizations. The ethical hub
of the polis is now dispersed and in flux. Burke’s concept of identification extends ethos as a
rhetorical proof in these circumstances. Identification augments the “inventive artistry” of
Aristotle’s pisteis, explaining that “people earnestly yearn to identify themselves with some
group or other” (1951, p. 203).
As Burke (1950) described it, identification is a necessary component of social forms—
from communities to religions, in political parties, businesses, and the marketplace. Class
stratification, dominant and subversive power relations, wealth distribution, and other forms
of division remain amid the unifying bonds of identification. We desire order. The consum-
mate rhetorical motive is grounded in the social form itself, and not in competing interests,
differences, or advantages.
Burkean identification extends classical ethos by embodying our most powerful, primitive
dialectic: that of alienation vs. belonging. The relevance of this dialectic to corporate discourse—
which bespeaks a rhetoric of group identity and membership—should be patent. At no time is
identification separated from the dialectic of division versus unity. After all, if we “were not
apart from one another, there would be no need for the rhetorician to proclaim [our] unity”
(Burke, 1950, p. 22). Identification thus describes a transformative process for establishing
relations with entities outside of ourselves (individuals, groups, objects, institutions) and for
constituting social forms.
Aristotle’s rhetorical theory claims that an audience is likely to believe a speaker’s argument if
it believes that the speaker can be trusted. That trust is generated as a function of ethos. Likewise,
organizations foster a positive ethos to strengthen the organizing ties among and between orga-
nizational members, affiliates, and customers. In extending Aristotle’s artistic proof, identification
makes it possible for interlocutors to align their interests. And alignment, in Burke’s (1984)
calculation, equals persuasion—which makes identification a powerful source of dynamic social
order, because it remedies separateness and alienation by subsuming the differences between and
among individuals (p. 263). Given the human need for sociality, we generally want to identify
ourselves with corporate bodies—with entities larger than ourselves. Institutional identity (cor-
porate, political, religious) and individual identity (as constituted by one’s identification with
other people, groups, organizations) are bound to one another. Here, we encounter another
Burkean (1950) term, consubstantiality. Day (1960) described consubstantiality as a common
sense that interlocutors develop during identification. In acting together, people constitute a
particular approach to living—a “corporate ethos,” as it were—that they share through common
ideas, attitudes, images, behaviors, speech, and so forth. This sharing makes the people involved
consubstantial. When identification achieves consubstantiality, it unites people in the substance
of common ideas, values, and social practices.
Burke’s (1931) “psychology of form” describes the transference mechanism by which any
appeal, Aristotelian or otherwise, succeeds in its basic aim. That is, its success will rest in the
“creation of an appetite in the mind of the auditor, and the adequate satisfying of that appe-
tite” (p. 31). The “inventive artistry” expressed in the pisteis becomes, in this simultaneously
Burkean and Freudian sense, dyadic, in that an audience completes what a speaker initiates. As
Zulick (2004) explains, the speaker’s invention of and the audience’s recognition of pattern
operate within the same thinking process; hence, “the inventor and the audience of any formal
expression share in its production at the formal level as well as the social level” (p. 25). Without
cooperation and compliance, the work of organizations dissipates and results are hard to come
by. It is vital, then, that organizations analyze their social form and refine their rhetorical
appeals to achieve identification and consubstantiality—both with members internally and
with external audiences.
Applications for Organizational Rhetoric
If ethos enables social recognition and identification extends ethos, how does identification (à la
Burke) relieve organizations, their members, and the public from the divisions that separate
them? What role does identification play in the discourse between organizations and their mem-
bers that enables all concerned to adopt mutually constitutive and interdependent positions,
even without consensus?
Following Aristotle, we assume that an individual speaker gains credibility by displaying “good
sense,” “good will,” and “good moral character.” Similarly, we assume that an organization
demonstrates credibility, competence, and community—and, in so doing, engages audiences in
specific kinds of interactions and relationships with that organization. But this begs the question:
What does it mean to say that an organization “demonstrates” ethos—that it, too, displays and
practices wisdom (phronesis), virtue (arete), and good will (eunoia) in its rhetoric? Theory
remains uncertain regarding speech that lacks an identifiable, ethically responsible and respon-
sive individual as its speaker (Cheney, 1991); given this uncertainty, what can ethos teach us
about organizational rhetoric and its impact on audiences? What is it that encourages audiences
to ascribe phronesis, arete, or eunoia to an organization?
Since ethos is a two‐way street between rhetors and their audiences, a commercial organiza-
tion will show “good sense” by polling its customers’ interests, concerns, preferences, and
expectations. To the degree it is successful in meeting those expectations, its customers will
regard its ethos as positive. “Good will” may be purchased similarly, by providing goods and
services, conducting research, and engaging in other field‐specific activities that are responsive
to the public’s interests and values. For example, a cosmetic company specializing in natural
skin care might announce that it uses no animals in its product testing. “Good moral character”
will be displayed in corporate dealings with stakeholders, customers, clients, and vendors.
Displaying fairness will encourage others to believe that they are not being cheated, misled, or
otherwise exploited.
Picking components from Aristotle, Hoffman and Ford (2010) ground “organizational
credibility” in “competence” (equivalent to phronesis) and “community” (roughly equivalent to
eunoia, though suggestive of arete as well). The public’s view of an organization’s credibility
rests on two factors: (1) how well that organization balances costs accruing to the community
of which it is part, against the benefits it brings to the community; and (2) the level of compe-
tence that organization musters in order to achieve such balance (Hoffman & Ford, 2010,
p. 27). For example, a factory might contribute to the local economy in its hiring practices while
polluting the air and groundwater. Can that factory reduce the latter while maintaining the
former? If yes, then the corporate ownership will have kept “competence” and “community” in
balance. Treating ethos as an implicit form of social capital, Hoffman and Ford use prior repu-
tation—a Ciceronian notion—as a means to communicate, negotiate, and seek compromise in
its reciprocal transactions.
In the example above, both the rhetor and its audience are members of the same community—
whether one takes the position that “community” is discursively generated or is an extant
social form. Belonging to the same community means sharing common interests, and the
alignment of interests enables persuasion. This is the stage upon which identification operates.
As Burke (1950) described it, identification marks an ongoing process of socialization achieved
by rhetorical action.
Cheney (1991) defines identity as a relationship between individuals and the social structures
to which they belong or with which they otherwise engage. A group isn’t a collective mind or a
collective agent, he suggests, but a display of shared identity arising from shared interests (pp.
13–18). When organizations and their members (as well as organizational affiliates and the
public) make themselves consubstantial, organizations achieve the willing cooperation that is a
hallmark of their social structure.
However, even when influenced by Burke, recent theorists have begun to look beyond his
model in articulating a postmodern ethos—not just for individuals and social groups, but for
corporations themselves as the agents or producers of discourse. In this spirit, Crable (1990)
described organizational rhetoric as “the fourth great system” in the history of rhetoric, with
“classical,” “prescriptive,” and “social rhetoric” as its predecessors. Within this fourth system,
rhetoric “is produced by organizations, not individuals” (p. 117). Crable’s discussion has impli-
cations for a post‐Aristotelian and, indeed, a post‐Burkean model of ethos. An organization’s
rhetorical activity is not simply the discourse in which individuals engage to reach consensus,
overlaid on collectives. Rather, in representing an organization’s interests, organizational rhet-
oric masks individual speakers in order to exercise, with minimum distraction, discursive power
on its audiences (Crable, 1990, p. 117). Cheney and McMillan (1990) explored similar implica-
tions in the longstanding concept of the “corporate person,” which is “explicitly designed to
transcend the lives, energies, resources, and powers of the natural persons who created them”
(p. 96; also see chapter 15 on persona).
Implications for Praxis and Research
Ethos and identification are not freewheeling, undefined phenomena. Social recognition and the
extension of that recognition to an alignment of interests are specifically located. The study of their
practice offers a historically situated consideration of how these rhetorical concepts constitute the
close affiliations and general attitudes that we recognize as organizations. Further, such study
moves us closer to understanding how the human capacity to form family and tribal alliances
extends to the institutions and affiliations that are predominant markers of contemporary life.
What specific gains might we make from such study? For one, we gain a means to emphasize
the pragmatics of organizational discourse. As Boje, Oswick, and Ford (2004) put it, our con-
cerns as rhetoricians are “the actual or potential effects of messages, especially those that are not
abstracted from their social contexts” (p. 81). To examine ethos and identification is one way to
take communication beyond information exchange and closer to understanding its capacity to
form alliances and community, extending these to constitute organizations.
The usefulness of identification speaks directly to contemporary circumstances, when most orga-
nizations can no longer rely on top‐down management to engage workers. Albert, Ashforth, and
Dutton (2000) pointed to “flatter” infrastructures that make it more difficult for organizations to
use institutionalized means to perpetuate themselves: “Increasingly,” they wrote, “an organization
must reside in the heads and hearts of its members” (p. 13). Identification plays a part in achieving
that goal, and we should determine how it continues its work under different circumstances.
The notion of organization relies much on our use of language to recognize social forms, to
mark ourselves as participants, and to participate in those forms. Burke captures the reflexivity
of language in that dynamic—the identification “with” and the identification “of” (Oravec,
1989, p. 180). We identify ourselves with others, with groups, with corporations, and with other
social forms because language socializes us to do so. At the same time, the texts and the speech
we produce and engage with prompt our recognition and our sustaining of social forms. Without
social form, we have no common space in which to align interests. Without constituting a shared
interest in things, we are without sociality. At the scale of an organization, identification becomes
a process for engaging members in carrying out organizational interests as they would their own.
As social forms constituted in habits of discourse, organizations are an especially rich resource
for rhetoricians interested in examining the ways social structures sustain themselves. However,
we might push against the premise that rhetorical interactions form structures that persist in
memory—particularly when those structures under question constitute organizations, along
with their cultural expectations, laws, beliefs, and so on. What is the nature of that structure?
How does rhetoric reveal, exercise, and perpetuate social form in specific circumstances and con-
texts, such as those of organizations? What are the discursive processes that constitute social
forms such as organizations and what role does ethos play? What role does ethos play in the
rhetoric that sustains organizations, in creating the discursive space in which they operate? What
other theory can we bring into a cross‐disciplinary effort to investigate the general idea of orga-
nizational rhetoric and on ethos as one if its proofs? In the following, we discuss some of the
implications of these questions.
Sustainability
Organizational sustainability relies, in part, upon rhetoric, which serves (1), to influence percep-
tions and attitudes toward situations; (2), to create recognition, cooperation, and common sense
across time and space; and (3), to enable willing cooperation among organizational members,
the organization’s interested affiliates, the public, the state, and other audiences. How do
rhetorical practices of ethos and identification sustain the social form of organizations?
Ontology
Are organizations legal and economic entities only, or are they also—perhaps more so—spaces
for interpretive and communicative action? Should we understand organizations as systems, or
is it more practical to see organizations as persistent fields of relations that constitute and are
constituted in the work of ethos and identification?
Organizational speaker identity
As Cheney (1991) wrote, “We are perplexed by the practical and theoretical aspects of messages
that cannot be easily identified with individual persons” (p. 3). This perplexity remains in the
study of organizational rhetoric. Who speaks when we hear organizations in the media? How do
we read or hear texts produced by an organization? How far does the inclusiveness of such
pronouns as “we” or “our” extend? Whom or what does organizational speech represent?
Such questions as these remain unsettled and subject for further study.
Actor–network theory (ANT)
Burke (1945) asked, in establishing his model of dramatism, “What is involved when we say
what people are doing and why they are doing it?” (p. xv). As a logical extension of organiza-
tional speaker identity, ANT’s non‐essentialist model of agency suggests a possible pairing with
rhetorical theory that can help analyze the ethos of non‐human agency—or, to paraphrase
Burke, can help explore what is involved when we say that our company did something or that
the government said something. How can we account for ethos and identification outside of a
network of human activity? What do ethos and identification look like when applied to non‐
human actors? (See also chapter 20 on rhetorical agency.)
Structuration theory
Though Giddens (1984) downplayed the role of discourse in his theory of structuration, there
must be ways to apply rhetorical theory within his process model as an instrument for analyzing
the division (and remedy) between individual and organizational agency (the work of identification).
Speech and texts are outcomes of discursive practices, and they produce durable organization by
means of persistent, ongoing reciprocal acts marked by rules and routines—core tenets of structur-
ation theory (see also chapters 2 and 3). Speech and text, thus, bear markers of ethos.
Situation and audience
Hoffman and Ford (2010) revisited situation and audience, complicating what we can otherwise
read as two components of the traditional “communication triangle.” For example, situation in
the organizational context refers to the open‐system nature of organizations, which take in
information from outside and reproduce that information as products and services. Also, organi-
zations address diverse audiences whose interests sometimes overlap and sometimes diverge or
even conflict. Unlike the classical rhetor, whose audience is socially striated and hegemonic, the
modern organization is situated among competing audiences, making demographics and audi-
ence analysis more crucial—and more complex. How might we investigate the manner in which
an organization constructs situations and invokes audiences to generate an ethos as a proof of its
position on a given issue?

Genre theory
Business communications courses often present genre as form (email, memo, annual report).
Miller’s (1984) revisioning of genre as social action opens new paths for ethos analysis and appli-
cation. In Miller’s model, the idea of specific forms does not apply to substance but, rather, to
audiences and purposes that “the action [genre] is used to accomplish” (p. 151). How might
Miller’s model inform public relations and issues management (see chapters 4 and 27)? Might it
lead businesses and their spokespersons to think differently about their communicative actions?
Field theory
Insofar as people willingly cooperate to extend their relationship to one another and to the orga-
nizations to which they belong, the discursive fields that comprise organizations grow corre-
spondingly. As Martin (2003) suggested, action at a distance lies at the heart of organizational
rhetoric. We might ask, then, how ethos works to produce a field of significant meaning within
contemporary corporate culture.
Conclusion
Ethos, and its extension to identification, as a constitutive, discursive process, aims at social
recognition, trust transference, and the alignment of interests; in achieving this alignment,
we constitute the social forms with which we associate, including organizations. Like other
discursive actions, rhetoric determines our position in a field of relationships: we engage in
rhetoric as we engage with others. This engagement entails ethos, whether defined as image
making or reputation or identification. When the ethos‐identification dynamic—whether
associated with an individual or an organization—persuades audiences toward alignment and
commitment, it creates order out of the potential and the probable. Out of that order, we
design solutions, fashion tools, and arrive at answers. When successful in so doing, we live
and thrive together, corporately.

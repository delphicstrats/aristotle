# Part I: The Problem — Why Strategic Communications Governance Fails

*SCI Intellectual Framework — Document 01*

---

## Opening: A Persistent Failure

In 2020, McKinsey & Company surveyed senior executives at organizations across industries about their external stakeholder engagement practices. One finding stood out: only 7 percent of respondents said their organizations *frequently* aligned stakeholder interests with their business objectives. The remaining 93 percent were doing so sometimes, rarely, or never.

That finding would be striking if it were exceptional. What makes it significant is that it is not. McKinsey had asked the same questions in 2015. The number had not meaningfully moved. In the intervening five years, CEO attention to stakeholder engagement had increased substantially. Corporate communications functions had grown. Technology platforms for monitoring stakeholders had proliferated. Stakeholder capitalism had become the dominant vocabulary of executive leadership forums worldwide. And yet the actual alignment of stakeholder interests with organizational strategy remained essentially where it had been.

More priority. No better results.

This is not a story about individual organizations failing. It is a story about a structural problem that individual effort — however well-intentioned and well-resourced — cannot solve. This document diagnoses that problem: what it is, why it persists, and why the responses organizations have adopted to address it have failed to reach its root cause.

The argument proceeds in four steps. First, we establish the empirical record: the problem is real, widespread, and consequential. Second, we identify its structural source: the problem is a predictable property of distributed organizational management applied to shared external audiences. Third, we identify the observable failure mode — the say-do gap — and distinguish it from the superficially similar but fundamentally different problem of poor communications. Fourth, we examine the misdiagnosis that drives the wrong response, and show why the tools organizations have deployed have not and cannot resolve the structural problem.

The conclusion is that the required response is governance — not better communications, not more technology, not additional organizational attention, but an institutional mechanism that connects organizational strategy to external stakeholder reality and holds distributed actors accountable for coherent, evidence-grounded engagement. What that governance architecture looks like, and what theoretical foundations it rests on, is the work of the framework's subsequent parts.

## I. The Empirical Record: A Persistent and Consequential Failure

The McKinsey finding is not an outlier. It sits within a consistent empirical picture documented across research traditions, institutional contexts, and measurement frameworks over more than a decade.

### The Alignment Gap

McKinsey's 2020 survey of external engagement practices identified several additional findings beyond the headline alignment number. Organizations that *do* achieve stakeholder alignment — those 7 percent that do so frequently — share three characteristics that distinguish them from the rest: they use technology and metrics systematically to assess stakeholder engagement (13 percent of total respondents); they adopt agile organizational designs that allow rapid response to shifts in stakeholder posture; and they treat external engagement not as a communications function but as a strategic management function.

The gap between these organizations and the rest is not primarily a gap in resources, communications talent, or leadership commitment. It is a gap in *governance architecture* — in whether the organization has built the institutional infrastructure to manage external stakeholder relationships continuously, coherently, and with accountability for outcomes.

The World Benchmarking Alliance's study of corporate stakeholder engagement practices reached a compatible conclusion through a different measurement approach: only 9 percent of companies meaningfully engage the stakeholders most affected by their operations. This figure is consistent across sectors and geographies. The WBA study also found that companies with genuine stakeholder engagement practices consistently outperformed their peers on strategic outcomes — but the mechanism was not the engagement itself. It was the *intelligence* that engagement generated: genuine understanding of stakeholder posture enabled better strategic decisions, more accurate anticipation of resistance, and more effective adaptation of approach.

### The Trust Erosion Record

Edelman's annual Trust Barometer has tracked institutional credibility across 28 countries for more than two decades. The 2024–2026 period documents a period of accelerating erosion that reveals the strategic costs of sustained governance failure.

The pattern across these three years — from *Innovation in Peril* (2024) through *Trust and the Crisis of Grievance* (2025) to *Trust Amid Insularity* (2026) — is not random volatility. It is a coherent narrative of compounding institutional failure: organizations consistently communicating commitments their operations do not sustain, audiences progressively losing confidence in institutional claims, and the resulting retreat into insular trust networks that are essentially immune to institutional signaling.

The specific mechanisms Edelman identifies are instructive. By 2025, 63 percent of respondents believed that journalists were purposely saying things they knew to be false; 61 percent felt the same about government leaders; 53 percent felt the same about business leaders. This is not merely skepticism about competence. It is a judgment about character — specifically, about the *arete* dimension of ethos in the classical rhetorical framework: the belief that institutional actors are systematically dishonest. Once this belief is held by a majority, individual communicative acts — however well-crafted — face a reception environment that is structurally hostile.

The consequences are measurable. Edelman's data shows that respondents with high institutional grievance — those who perceive the system as actively rigged against people like them — exhibit an average institutional trust score of 34 percent, compared to 62 percent among those with low grievance. This trust penalty is not a sentiment measurement. It is a predictor of behavior: high-grievance audiences are more than twice as likely to hold a zero-sum orientation toward institutional actors, meaning they are predisposed to interpret organizational claims skeptically and to oppose rather than support organizational objectives.

The business implications are direct. Research cited alongside the Edelman data suggests that organizations tracking trust as a board-level metric are more than three times more likely to report substantial profits than those that do not. Yet only 22 percent of companies currently measure trust at the board level. The strategic asset most predictive of stakeholder cooperation is the least systematically governed.

### The Communications Function Gap

The Korn Ferry survey of chief communications officers (2025) documents a function in structural transition that has not completed its transformation. The CCO is increasingly recognized as a strategic organizational role: 96 percent of CCOs report AI adoption within their functions, and a majority sit on or directly report to the executive committee. But the fragmentation of the function persists: at 36 percent of industrial firms, the CCO does not sit on the executive committee at all. And across the field, only 29 percent of communications functions have an AI strategy — suggesting that technology adoption is outpacing governance discipline.

The pattern the Korn Ferry data reveals is that the communications function is rising in organizational importance but has not yet fully made the transition from a function that *manages messages* to a function that *governs the organization's external signal environment*. The distinction is not semantic. A function that manages messages produces better communications. A function that governs the external signal environment ensures that all organizational actions — not just formal communications — are coherent with the organization's stakeholder commitments. The first requires skilled communicators. The second requires institutional architecture.

## II. The Structural Explanation: Why the Failure Is Predictable

The empirical record establishes that the problem is real and persistent. Understanding why it persists requires moving from description to diagnosis — from *what* is failing to *why* the failure is structurally produced and why individual responses predictably fail to resolve it.

### Organizations as Distributed Systems of Actors

The modern organization is not a unified actor. It is a distributed system comprising dozens or hundreds of sub-actors — executive leadership, legal counsel, government affairs, communications, marketing, business development, human resources, operations management, investor relations, community relations — each pursuing legitimate organizational objectives within their own functional mandate, each managing external relationships in their own domain, each transmitting signals to audiences outside the organization as a routine consequence of their work.

These actors share organizational membership and, at some level, strategic intent. What they rarely share is a common intelligence picture of the external stakeholders who determine whether that strategic intent is achieved.

The legal team drafting regulatory filings does not typically know what the government affairs team communicated to the same regulator last month. The operations team implementing a cost-reduction program does not typically know what commitments the executive team made to the same communities about employment stability six weeks earlier. The communications team developing a public positioning statement does not typically know what signals the investor relations team sent to analysts about the same issue. The business development team negotiating a partnership does not typically know what positions the policy team has taken with legislators who will regulate that partnership.

Each of these actors is operating competently within their functional mandate. The failure is not in any one of them. The failure is that their collective output — the aggregate signal their combined actions and communications send to shared external audiences — is not coherent with any articulated strategic intent, because there is no institutional mechanism that ensures it will be.

This is not a coordination failure in the ordinary sense — not a failure of communication *between* organizational functions that can be solved by better internal collaboration. It is a *governance* failure: the absence of an institutional structure that connects organizational strategy to external stakeholder reality and creates accountability for the coherence of all organizational signals.

### The Decoupling Mechanism

Institutional theory provides the most precise account of why this failure is structural rather than accidental. Meyer and Rowan's foundational analysis (1977) identified the mechanism of *decoupling* as a defining feature of how organizations manage their institutional environments.

Organizations in complex institutional settings face two simultaneous pressures: the pressure to conform to institutional expectations and norms (which governs their legitimacy and resource access) and the pressure to pursue operational efficiency and effectiveness (which governs their technical performance). These pressures frequently conflict. The response organizations adopt — almost universally, Meyer and Rowan argue — is to decouple their formal structure from their actual activity: to maintain a ceremonial front that conforms to institutional expectations while allowing operational activity to proceed according to its own logic.

Decoupling produces exactly the observable pattern that the empirical record documents: organizations announce commitments (formal structure, ceremonial conformity) while their operations continue to follow their own incentive structures (technical activity, actual behavior). The commitment and the behavior are not coordinated because there is no institutional mechanism requiring their coordination. They are managed by different people in different functions with different objectives and different accountability structures.

Meyer and Rowan recognized decoupling as functional — a way for organizations to survive contradictory pressures that they cannot simultaneously satisfy. What they could not fully anticipate in 1977 is the degree to which information technology has since eliminated the institutional protection that decoupling depends on. The "inspection" that organizations were able to minimize in an era of limited information flow is now structurally unavoidable. Every organizational decision, commitment, and action is potentially visible to the stakeholder communities whose assessments determine resource access and social license. The ceremonial front is no longer protected from comparison with operational reality.

In this environment, decoupling is no longer functional. It is an organizational liability: the structural source of the credibility destruction that the Edelman data documents.

### Why Individual Competence Cannot Solve a Structural Problem

The diagnostic conclusion follows directly: the say-do gap that organizations experience at scale is not a failure of individual communicative skill, executive commitment, or stakeholder relations competence. It is a structural property of distributed organizations operating without governance architecture for their external signal environment.

This diagnosis has a practical corollary: responses that target individual competence — better communicators, stronger messaging frameworks, more skilled government affairs staff, improved stakeholder relations training — cannot solve a structural problem. They improve the quality of individual signals without addressing the structural incoherence of the aggregate signal. An organization of excellent individual communicators, each deploying their skills without coordination, will produce an aggregate signal that is internally inconsistent. And internal inconsistency, received by audiences whose trust in institutions is already fragile, is interpreted as institutional incompetence, bad faith, or dishonesty — which is precisely the picture the Edelman data presents.

## III. The Say-Do Gap as Institutional Failure

The term "say-do gap" is used in multiple contexts with varying degrees of precision. In the SCI framework, it refers to a specific, diagnosable failure mode: the condition in which an organization's communications assert commitments that its operations do not sustain, and in which the contradiction is visible to the external audiences whose posture the organization is trying to maintain.

The say-do gap is not a messaging error. A messaging error is corrected by changing what the organization says. The say-do gap is corrected only by aligning what the organization does with what it has committed to — or, when full alignment is not achievable in the near term, by governing the aggregate signal coherently so that the gap is minimized and is not compounded by additional contradictions from other organizational actors.

### Case Evidence: The Gap in Practice

The cases that best illustrate the say-do gap in its fully developed form are those where the gap was not a result of deception — where the organization genuinely intended to behave consistently with its stated commitments — but where the absence of governance architecture allowed the contradiction to develop and persist without detection until it produced an external crisis.

**Shell and the Brent Spar (1995).** Shell's plan to dispose of the Brent Spar storage platform by deep-sea sinking had regulatory approval and was supported by technical analysis showing it to be the environmentally responsible choice. Shell's technical and legal posture was coherent. What Shell failed to manage was the gap between its technical legitimacy (the regulatory system approved this) and its normative legitimacy (the affected publics judged it to be wrong). Greenpeace's campaign mobilized civil society actors whose posture toward Shell's operations was already shaped by a broader narrative about corporate environmental responsibility — a narrative that Shell's operational history had contributed to but that its communications had not acknowledged or engaged.

The crisis was not produced by Shell saying one thing and doing another in the conventional sense. It was produced by Shell failing to recognize that its operational decision — however technically sound — would be received against a backdrop of accumulated stakeholder perception that the decision contradicted. The aggregate signal that stakeholders received was not Shell's technical justification; it was the integration of that justification with everything else they knew or believed about Shell's relationship to environmental stewardship. The say-do gap was not between Shell's words and its actions; it was between Shell's self-understanding and its stakeholders' understanding.

**Nike and Labor Practices (1990s).** Nike's brand proposition was built on aspiration, athletic excellence, and the human capacity to transcend limitation. Its supply chain strategy was built on cost minimization through manufacturing in low-wage economies with limited labor regulation. These two strategies were managed by different organizational functions with different objectives and different external relationships. Neither function had visibility into the aggregate signal their combined operations were sending to shared audiences.

When investigative journalism exposed the labor conditions in Nike's supplier facilities, the contradiction was not between something Nike said and something Nike did in a single incident. It was between what the brand systematically communicated about human dignity and aspiration, and what the supply chain systematically did to reduce labor costs. The gap had been structurally present for years. What changed was external visibility. The governance failure was not the labor practices themselves; it was the absence of any institutional mechanism that might have identified the contradiction, assessed its potential stakeholder impact, and either aligned the operations with the brand commitment or adjusted the brand commitment to accurately reflect the operational reality.

### The Contemporary Pattern

The Edelman data provides systematic evidence that these cases are not exceptional. The three-year arc from 2024 to 2026 documents a pattern of sector-specific say-do gaps that follow the same structural logic.

In the technology sector, organizations whose brand proposition rests on innovation and human progress have generated an audience response characterized by anxiety rather than enthusiasm: 39 percent of global respondents believe innovation is being poorly managed, while only 22 percent believe it is well managed. The gap is not between what technology companies have said about innovation and what they have delivered technically. It is between the *kind* of innovation they have delivered — rapid, optimization-focused, commercially driven — and the values their communications have associated with innovation: safety, human benefit, careful stewardship of powerful capabilities.

In the financial sector, organizations that have systematically communicated commitments to fairness, inclusion, and community investment face an audience among whom 67 percent believe the system is structurally biased toward the wealthy and 65 percent believe that wealthy institutions cause societal harm. The gap is between the equity narrative and the observed distribution of outcomes.

In both cases, the problem is not that these organizations have lied. It is that the commitments their communications have made — implicitly or explicitly — have not been matched by the aggregate signal their operations produce. And the absence of governance architecture means there is no institutional mechanism capable of detecting the gap, assessing its stakeholder impact, and generating accountability for closing it.

## IV. The Misdiagnosis and the Tool Gap

Given the empirical record of persistent failure and the structural diagnosis of its source, one would expect the organizational response to be structural. In practice, it rarely is. The predominant response to stakeholder alignment failure treats it as a communications problem — and deploys communications solutions.

### The Wrong Diagnosis

The communications diagnosis of the stakeholder alignment problem runs as follows: the organization's stakeholders do not understand what the organization is doing and why; better communications would change this; therefore, investment in communications capability — better messaging, more skilled communicators, improved media relationships, more sophisticated content — is the appropriate response.

This diagnosis is not simply wrong. It addresses a real problem — many organizations do communicate poorly — and communications improvement does produce real, if limited, results. The problem is that it is insufficient: it addresses the symptom while leaving the structural cause intact.

Better messaging cannot align signals that the communications function does not know are being sent. A more skilled communications team cannot prevent an operational decision made by a function with no visibility into its communications implications from contradicting the organization's publicly stated commitments. Improved media relationships cannot rebuild institutional credibility that has been damaged by a pattern of observable gaps between organizational commitments and organizational behavior.

The communications diagnosis also tends to concentrate accountability in the wrong function. If the problem is treated as a communications problem, the communications function bears responsibility for outcomes that are determined by decisions made across the entire organization. This creates the organizational dynamic that the Korn Ferry data documents: CCOs are given strategic responsibility without the governance authority — or the institutional architecture — required to exercise it effectively.

### The Tool Landscape and Its Limits

The organizational response to stakeholder alignment problems has generated a substantial market for tools claiming to address them. Each major tool category addresses a genuine and important problem. None of them address the governance problem that the structural diagnosis identifies.

**Business Intelligence and Analytics (Tableau, Power BI, and analogues).** These platforms excel at aggregating, visualizing, and analyzing internal performance data. They answer the question "what happened?" with precision and scale. They do not provide intelligence about external stakeholder posture, do not track whether organizational actions are coherent with stakeholder commitments, and are not designed to connect internal performance data to external relationship outcomes. They tell an organization how its operations are performing; they cannot tell it whether those operations are moving its most consequential stakeholders toward or away from the actions on which its strategic objectives depend.

**Customer Relationship Management (Salesforce and analogues).** CRM platforms manage the history and activity of known contacts — conversations, commitments, follow-up actions, relationship health indicators. They are built for sales and service functions, with architecture optimized for managing large volumes of transactional relationships. They do not support strategic intelligence about stakeholder posture. They do not integrate signals from organizational functions outside the functions that enter data. They do not connect relationship management to strategic objectives. And they are built for the Market domain — the customer relationship — leaving Government, Information, and Civil Society domain stakeholders entirely outside their scope.

**PR and Media Monitoring (Cision, Meltwater, and analogues).** These platforms provide coverage monitoring, sentiment analysis, and media relationship management primarily within the Information domain. They track what is being said about the organization in media and social channels, who is saying it, and how audiences appear to be responding. These are genuine intelligence functions with real strategic value. But they operate in one domain only, without integration with what the organization is actually doing in the operational domains that generate the underlying reality the media is covering. They can tell an organization that its media coverage is negative; they cannot tell it whether that negativity reflects a say-do gap and, if so, which operational decisions are producing it.

**OKR and Strategy Execution (Workboard, Lattice, and analogues).** These platforms align internal objectives and performance management across the organization. They are designed for the internal coordination problem — ensuring that organizational functions are working toward shared goals — not for the external stakeholder problem. They have no external stakeholder intelligence function, no mechanism for assessing whether organizational actions are coherent with external commitments, and no capacity to integrate external signals into the strategic alignment process.

**Stakeholder Management and Public Affairs (Quorum, FiscalNote, and analogues).** These platforms are the closest adjacent category to the governance problem SCI addresses. Quorum and FiscalNote manage government stakeholder contacts and legislative tracking — monitoring regulatory developments, managing relationships with legislative and regulatory staff, and connecting policy intelligence to organizational decision-making. They provide genuine value in the Government domain. But their scope is domain-specific: they do not integrate across Government, Information, Market, and Civil Society domains. They do not provide the posture construct — the assessment of stakeholder behavioral disposition against specific organizational objectives — that strategic communications governance requires. And they do not address the cross-functional coordination problem: the silo between government affairs and the other organizational functions whose actions send signals to the same government audiences the platform is managing.

### The Common Gap

The pattern across all five tool categories is consistent: each tool is designed to address a specific problem within a specific domain or function, not to provide the governance architecture that connects organizational strategy to the full external stakeholder environment. The result is that organizations deploying multiple tools from multiple categories may have sophisticated capabilities in each domain while having no integrated picture of their aggregate stakeholder posture, no governance mechanism for ensuring signal coherence across domains and functions, and no institutional accountability for the gap between what they are communicating and what they are doing.

This is not a criticism of these tools. It is a recognition that they were built to solve different problems, and that the governance problem — the problem of connecting distributed organizational actors to a coherent, evidence-backed, adaptive external stakeholder strategy — has not been solved by any of them, because none of them were designed to solve it.

## V. The Governance Imperative

The diagnostic argument establishes a specific requirement: the response to strategic communications governance failure must be structural, not tactical. It must address the root cause — the absence of an institutional mechanism connecting strategy to external stakeholder reality — not merely the symptoms. And it must be embedded in the organization's operating architecture, not delegated to any single function operating independently of the others.

### What Governance Means in This Context

In the SCI framework, governance of the strategic communications function means three things:

**Continuous intelligence on stakeholder posture.** The organization must maintain a structured, evidence-grounded, continuously updated picture of the behavioral disposition of its most consequential external stakeholders — assessed against the specific actions those stakeholders need to take, or refrain from taking, for the organization's strategic objectives to be achieved. This is not relationship management. It is strategic intelligence, maintained with the same discipline and accountability standards as any other strategic information the organization uses to make decisions.

**Coordination of the organizational signal.** The organization must have a mechanism for ensuring that all organizational actions — not just formal communications — are assessed for their implications for the stakeholder posture it is trying to maintain. Operational decisions, personnel changes, financial commitments, partnership formations, public statements — all of these send signals to external audiences. Governance means that these signals are assessed for coherence, that contradictions are identified before they become visible to external audiences, and that distributed actors have accountability for the coherence of their own signal contributions.

**Continuous assessment and adaptation.** External stakeholder posture is not static. It changes in response to organizational signals, to events in the broader environment, to the actions of other organizations, and to the internal dynamics of the stakeholder communities themselves. The governance architecture must include a mechanism for regularly assessing whether current stakeholder postures are moving toward or away from what strategic objectives require, and for adapting the organization's approach based on that assessment.

### The Institutional Gap

The 2009 Department of Defense Strategic Communication Joint Integrating Concept — the most rigorous government attempt to define and operationalize strategic communications governance — identified this same function and its same absence. The JIC concluded that strategic communication must be an inherent feature of all operations planning: that every organizational action constitutes a signal, and that the governance of strategic communications is therefore inseparable from the governance of organizational strategy. The JIC called for the integration of all organizational actions and communications into a coherent, audience-centered signal stream managed through an adaptive cycle of audience intelligence, signal design, effect monitoring, and iterative modification.

And the DoD, for all the rigor of the concept, never operationalized it. The JIC described the required capability without producing the institutional architecture to fulfill it. The reasons are instructive: the integration challenge, absent purpose-built governance infrastructure, proved too great for distributed military functions with their own chains of command, objectives, and operational tempo to manage through policy guidance and coordination agreements alone.

The commercial organization faces the same structural challenge in a different institutional environment. The functions are different; the chains of command are different; the stakeholder domains are different. But the problem is structurally identical: distributed actors, shared external audiences, no institutional mechanism for ensuring coherence, and no governance architecture capable of connecting strategy to the external reality on which its outcomes depend.

The governance imperative is not a new idea. It is the same institutional requirement that has been recognized and not met in the most sophisticated organizational contexts that have attempted to address it. What is new is the combination of analytical clarity about the problem's structure, theoretical foundations robust enough to support a governance architecture, and the platform capabilities required to make that architecture operable at organizational scale.

## Conclusion: The Problem Precisely Stated

The strategic communications governance problem is this: organizations operating in complex stakeholder environments depend on independent actors whose actions determine strategic outcomes. Those actors are reached by the aggregate signal of everything the organization says and does — signals produced by distributed functions without a common intelligence picture, without coordination mechanisms, and without accountability for coherence. The result, documented across research traditions and measurement frameworks over more than a decade, is persistent misalignment: the organization's aggregate signal contradicts its stated commitments, stakeholder posture deteriorates, institutional credibility erodes, and strategic objectives become harder to achieve.

The problem has been misdiagnosed as a communications problem and addressed with communications solutions that cannot reach its root cause. The tools that organizations have deployed address specific, important problems within specific domains, but none of them provide the governance architecture that connects strategy to the full external stakeholder environment with the intelligence, coordination, and accountability that the structural problem requires.

The required solution is governance — an institutional mechanism that maintains continuous intelligence on stakeholder posture, coordinates all organizational signals, assesses effects adaptively, and creates accountability for coherence across distributed functions. What theoretical foundations justify and specify this governance architecture, and what its design looks like in practice, is the work of the framework's remaining parts.

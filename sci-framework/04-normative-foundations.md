## Introduction: The Question the Architecture Raises

Parts I through III have built a case on two foundations: an empirical one — that strategic communications governance failure is real, structural, and costly — and a theoretical one — that an intelligence architecture grounded in classical rhetoric, Resource Dependence Theory, and the JIC's integration imperative is the correct institutional response. Part III specified that architecture in detail. The argument, at that point, is complete as a governance argument.

It is not complete as a philosophical argument.

The SCI architecture is designed to assess the posture of external stakeholders, identify the gaps between their current disposition and the actions they must take to advance organizational priorities, develop evidence-grounded arguments to move those stakeholders toward the required actions, and deploy those arguments at moments of maximal effect across sustained engagement campaigns. This is a sophisticated, systematic, and deliberately effective apparatus for organizational influence. Any intellectual framework that presents such an apparatus without asking whether the apparatus is legitimate — under what conditions it is defensible, where its use crosses from legitimate persuasion into something else, what normative constraints should govern its deployment — is either incomplete or evasive.

Part IV asks the question the architecture raises. It is, of the four documents in this framework, the one most directly in my intellectual inheritance and the one where the existing philosophical literature is most insufficient. Classical rhetoric did not theorize organizations as communicative actors. The modern traditions that have tried to fill that gap — Habermas's communicative action theory, Grunig's two-way symmetrical model, the PR ethics literature — have produced either frameworks too demanding to be action-guiding or ethical charters too complacent about organizational power to be philosophically serious. The normative foundations of organizational rhetoric are genuinely underdeveloped.

This document undertakes three tasks, each of which the preceding synthesis work has prepared:

First, to theorize what organizational ethos actually consists of — what it means for an organization to communicate with integrity rather than merely to perform integrity, and why the say-do gap is a moral failure rather than merely a strategic miscalculation.

Second, to specify the conditions under which the strategic pursuit of organizational interests through rhetoric is legitimate — what distinguishes persuasion that respects the rational agency of those being persuaded from manipulation that undermines it, and how those conditions apply specifically to the asymmetric, sustained, intelligence-grounded engagement that the SCI architecture enables.

Third, to identify the limit conditions — the forms of organizational communication that the normative framework disqualifies, the interests for which sophisticated strategic rhetoric is not an appropriate instrument, and what the SCI architecture cannot make legitimate however well it is designed.

The central claim of Part IV is this: organizational rhetoric is normatively legitimate when it satisfies three conditions — institutional integrity in the relationship between organizational communications and organizational conduct, respect for the rational agency of stakeholders as the governing standard for communicative means, and alignment between the interests being advanced and genuine social goods that transcend organizational self-interest alone. These conditions are demanding but tractable. They are not satisfied by all organizational communication. But they are satisfiable — and the SCI architecture, when used by organizations that meet these conditions, is not merely an effective governance tool but a defensible one.

## I. Why the Existing Frameworks Are Insufficient

Before developing the positive account, it is necessary to understand precisely why the two most serious existing attempts at a normative framework for organizational communication fall short. This is not an exercise in criticism for its own sake. Understanding the specific failures of Habermas and Grunig identifies the exact ground that a better framework must cover.

### The Habermasian Impasse

Habermas's framework, at its strongest, establishes two things that any normative account of organizational communication must preserve: that pseudo-consensus — the manufactured appearance of agreement that masks the structural exercise of power — is a distinct and serious social pathology; and that rational agency is the central value at stake in communicative ethics. These contributions survive the critique of Habermas's broader framework and will be foundational to the account developed here.

What the Habermasian framework cannot do — what it is structurally incapable of doing — is answer the question of what legitimate organizational communication looks like. Its normative standard, the ideal speech situation, requires conditions that are categorically unavailable in organizational contexts: power-free deliberation among parties of equal standing, with no strategic interest in the outcome. This standard condemns all organizational strategic communication with equal force — the pharmaceutical company managing its regulatory communication and the community health nonprofit managing its public education campaign; the corporation concealing product hazards and the corporation genuinely seeking regulatory approval for a project that will employ a thousand people in a depressed region. A standard that cannot distinguish between these cases has not identified a normative criterion. It has identified an aspiration so remote from organizational reality that it functions only as a denunciation, not as a guide.

The failure is not incidental. It follows from Habermas's fundamental premise: that the legitimacy of communication requires the transcendence of strategic intent. If that premise is correct, then no organizational communication with strategic purposes can be legitimate, and the philosophical inquiry ends before it begins. The premise must be rejected, not refined, if we are to make progress.

### The Grunig Concession

Grunig's two-way symmetrical model is the PR field's most serious normative attempt, and it is, in important ways, the right kind of attempt. It recognizes that organizational legitimacy cannot be manufactured through communication that does not reflect genuine organizational behavior — that legitimacy must be earned through mutual adjustment rather than performed through strategic messaging. The symmetrical model's core intuition — that organizations which genuinely incorporate stakeholder perspectives into their decisions will be more legitimate than those that merely manage stakeholder perceptions — is correct.

But the model's intellectual honesty undermines its normative coherence. Grunig himself acknowledges that "pure symmetry" is unavailable for organizations with genuine competitive interests, and retreats to the "mixed-motive" model: organizations pursuing their own interests while helping publics satisfy theirs, with practitioners acting as "partisan mutual adjusters." This is a pragmatic description of sophisticated stakeholder management, which is an accurate characterization of what excellent organizational communication looks like in practice. But once the mixed-motive concession is made, the model has lost its normative distinctiveness. The difference between a sophisticated asymmetrical communicator who manages stakeholder expectations skillfully and a symmetrical communicator in the mixed-motive sense is no longer a categorical difference. It is a difference in degree, in the sincerity of the mutual adjustment, in whether the organization's genuine incorporation of stakeholder perspectives is real or performed. And that difference is exactly the question the normative framework must answer, not assume.

The Grunig model tells us that the relevant question is whether the adjustment is genuine or performed. It does not tell us what makes the distinction — what conditions determine whether stakeholder engagement is genuine incorporation or sophisticated management of opposition. That is the question this document must answer.

## II. Organizational Ethos as Institutional Integrity

### The Problem of Collective Character

The classical account of ethos rests on a correspondence principle: the speaker's communicated character is legitimate when it corresponds to their actual character. When I argued in the *Rhetoric* that ethos is the most powerful of the three pisteis, I meant that an audience's confidence in the speaker's genuine knowledge, genuine good character, and genuine goodwill toward the audience is the deepest ground of persuasive force. This confidence is warranted when it reflects something real about the speaker. It is a form of deception when it does not.

The problem for organizational rhetoric is that organizations are not unified subjects. They do not have character in the sense that individual persons have character — a relatively stable set of values, dispositions, and commitments that express themselves consistently across different situations and that can be assessed by those who observe the person's conduct over time. An organization has something else: a culture, a set of institutional incentive structures, a portfolio of stated commitments and policies, and a pattern of actual decisions and behaviors that may or may not be consistent with those stated commitments. The question of what organizational ethos consists of cannot be answered by direct analogy with individual ethos. It requires a different account.

### The Three-Part Structure of Institutional Integrity

I argue that organizational ethos is best understood as institutional integrity — a relationship among three elements that are often in tension and whose alignment constitutes the organizational equivalent of genuine character.

The first element is **stated commitments**: the values, priorities, and promises that the organization publicly endorses and that form the basis of its communications with stakeholders. These include explicit statements of values, published policies, public commitments made in engagement processes, and the positions articulated in advocacy and regulatory communications.

The second element is **resource allocation decisions**: the actual distribution of organizational resources — financial, human, temporal, and political — that reveals what the organization treats as genuinely important as distinct from what it publicly endorses. Resource allocation is the most reliable indicator of genuine organizational priority, because it is the domain where stated commitments encounter real constraints and trade-offs must be made. An organization that publicly endorses environmental stewardship but allocates its internal capital investment exclusively to operations whose environmental impact is negative is revealing, through its resource decisions, that its stated commitment does not reflect genuine organizational priority.

The third element is **behavioral patterns**: the observable record of how the organization actually behaves in situations where its stated commitments are tested — how it responds to regulatory scrutiny, how it treats communities affected by its operations, how it handles the discovery that a prior commitment was not honored, how its conduct in private corresponds to its public positions.

Institutional integrity is the alignment of these three elements: the organization communicates what it genuinely prioritizes, allocates resources consistent with those priorities, and behaves in ways that confirm rather than contradict its stated character. Its violation — the say-do gap — is the misalignment between stated commitments and the pattern of resource allocation and behavior that reveals the organization's actual priorities.

### The Say-Do Gap as Institutional Vice

The say-do gap is typically analyzed in strategic terms: it damages organizational reputation, erodes stakeholder trust, and creates vulnerability to adversarial framing. This strategic analysis is correct but incomplete. It fails to capture the moral character of the failure.

In the *Nicomachean Ethics*, I characterized the person who presents themselves as more virtuous than they are as exhibiting a vice that is worse in kind than simple ignorance of the good — because it involves a knowing misrepresentation of one's actual character. The vicious person who does not know they are vicious may, in principle, come to know and correct; the person who knows they fall short of the character they perform has chosen to deceive. The performance of virtue that one does not possess is not merely ineffective ethics; it is a specific moral failure.

The organizational analogue holds. An organization that performs environmental commitment in its public communications while its internal decision-making systematically deprioritizes environmental performance is not merely miscommunicating. It is exhibiting the institutional equivalent of this vice: a knowing misrepresentation of organizational character to stakeholders whose cooperation is being sought on the basis of that misrepresentation. The stakeholders who adjust their behavior — regulatory officials who grant approvals, community groups who moderate their opposition, investors who allocate capital — on the basis of a performed organizational character that does not correspond to the organization's actual decision-making are being deceived in a way that violates the fundamental premise of the rhetorical relationship.

This matters for the SCI architecture specifically because the Stakeholder record and the Messaging Framework are designed to be grounded in the organization's actual intelligence about the stakeholder and the actual strength of the arguments available. They are not designed to be grounded in a performance of organizational character. An organization that uses SCI's architecture while maintaining a systematic say-do gap is using the evidence-accountability machinery of the architecture in service of a communicative deception at the level of organizational ethos. The architecture cannot prevent this; it can only identify it as a fundamental violation of the normative foundations on which the architecture's legitimacy rests.

### What the Integrity Condition Does Not Require

It is important to be precise about what institutional integrity requires and what it does not require, because the requirement can be stated in ways that are either too demanding to be action-guiding or too weak to be normatively meaningful.

The integrity condition does **not** require organizational altruism. An organization committed primarily to profitability, or to advancing the interests of its members, or to the success of a particular policy agenda, can have genuine institutional ethos. The condition is not that the organization's interests be oriented toward the common good as a primary commitment. It is that the organization's communications about its interests be honest — that it not perform a character (benevolent corporate citizen, community partner, environmental steward) that its actual resource allocation decisions and behavioral patterns contradict.

The integrity condition does **not** require that an organization never adapt its communications to different audiences. Different stakeholders respond to different frames, and the same genuine organizational position may be legitimately communicated in terms of its economic implications to one audience and its environmental implications to another. What the condition prohibits is the communication of substantively contradictory positions to different audiences who might encounter each other's information — the scenario the SCI architecture's alignment cycle is specifically designed to detect and prevent.

The integrity condition **does** require that communications be consistent with actual organizational behavior over time and that apparent inconsistencies between communications and conduct be addressed through genuine behavioral change rather than through improved messaging. An organization that discovers a say-do gap has two legitimate responses: close the behavioral gap (change the conduct to match the commitment) or close the communicative gap (revise the stated commitment to match the actual conduct and communicate the revision honestly). The response that is not legitimate is the one most commonly taken: intensify communications about the commitment while leaving the behavioral gap unaddressed.

## III. The Rational Agency Standard

### Persuasion and Manipulation: The Right Distinction

The most consequential normative distinction for organizational rhetoric is not between communication and strategic action, as Habermas draws it, but between two modes of strategic communication: persuasion that works *through* the rational agency of those being addressed, and manipulation that works *around* it.

This distinction is not merely rhetorical. It identifies a morally significant difference in what the communicator is doing with respect to the person they are trying to move.

Persuasion that works through rational agency treats the audience as a rational agent capable of evaluating arguments, recognizing relevant evidence, understanding the relationship between the proposed action and their own genuine interests, and making a judgment on that basis. The communicator who operates in this mode provides evidence, makes arguments, identifies the relevant considerations, and then — in an important sense — leaves the conclusion to the audience. The audience's adoption of the conclusion is the product of their own rational process, informed and structured by the communication they have received.

Manipulation that works around rational agency treats the audience as a psychological system with exploitable vulnerabilities — cognitive biases, emotional triggers, informational deficits, and social pressures that can be engaged to produce the desired behavioral outcome without the audience's deliberate judgment being the operative mechanism. The audience who is manipulated does not conclude; they are steered. The difference is not in the outcome — both processes can produce the same behavioral change — but in the nature of the causal path.

The moral significance of this distinction rests on the concept of rational agency itself. To treat a person as a rational agent is to acknowledge their capacity for self-determination: their ability to evaluate reasons, form judgments, and act on the basis of those judgments in pursuit of their own genuine interests as they understand them. This capacity is not merely instrumentally valuable. It is the basis on which persons command the respect that distinguishes persons from objects. To manipulate a person — to produce behavioral change through a causal path that bypasses their rational agency — is to treat them as an object to be moved rather than an agent to be addressed. This is the wrong that manipulation commits, and it is a wrong independent of whether the behavioral outcome it produces is beneficial to the person being manipulated.

### Applying the Standard

The rational agency standard, so specified, is action-guiding in a way that the ideal speech situation is not. It does not require power-free interaction or the suspension of strategic intent. It requires that the strategic intent be pursued through means that respect rather than exploit the audience's rational capacities.

In practice, this means:

**Evidence-grounded argument.** The communicator who provides genuine, verifiable evidence in support of a claim is working through rational agency: the audience can evaluate the evidence, assess its quality, consider alternative evidence, and form a judgment. The communicator who provides misleading statistics, cherry-picked data, or unverifiable assertions is working around rational agency: the audience's apparent reasoning from evidence is actually reasoning from manufactured premises.

**Honest identification of interests.** The communicator who acknowledges that they are advancing an organizational interest, and frames their argument as a case for why that interest and the audience's interests align, is working through rational agency. The communicator who conceals their organizational interest — communicating in the guise of neutral information, third-party advocacy, or objective expertise — is working around rational agency: the audience's apparent evaluation of an argument is actually evaluation of an argument they would assess differently if they knew whose interests were behind it.

**Engagement with genuine values.** The communicator who identifies what the audience genuinely values and makes a case for why the required action advances those values is working through rational agency. The communicator who manufactures the appearance of value alignment — through framing, selective emphasis, or strategic omission — is working around rational agency.

**Cognitive respect.** The communicator who allows the audience time and information to deliberate, who does not engineer urgency to foreclose deliberation, who does not exploit fear or social pressure to produce compliance, is working through rational agency. The communicator who engineers urgency, deploys loss aversion, or manufactures social proof to bypass deliberation is working around it.

### The SCI Architecture and the Rational Agency Standard

The SCI architecture is designed to satisfy the rational agency standard — but it must be recognized that this is a design commitment requiring active maintenance, not a property that the architecture automatically possesses.

The evidence accountability requirement — that every posture assessment and every Messaging Framework argument be traceable to validated source citations — is the structural enforcement of evidence-grounded argument. An argument in a Messaging Framework that cannot be traced to verifiable evidence fails this requirement at the architectural level before it reaches any audience.

The Gap-derived point structure — in which Information Points must address identified information deficits and Messaging Points must address identified values and motivational levers — is the structural commitment to engaging genuine rather than manufactured audience states. Information Points provide what the stakeholder actually lacks. Messaging Points engage what the stakeholder actually values. Neither is designed to create the appearance of informational completeness or value alignment where none exists.

The honest identification of interests is not structurally enforced by the SCI architecture in the same direct way. The architecture is designed for organizations that deploy it as themselves — that engage stakeholders under their own organizational identity and with disclosed organizational interests. An organization that uses the architecture to manage third-party advocacy, to communicate through apparently independent channels, or to conceal its identity in stakeholder engagement is violating the rational agency standard while using the architecture's tools. This is a use the architecture cannot prevent but one that the normative framework explicitly disqualifies.

## IV. The Ethics of Asymmetric Rhetoric

### Power Asymmetry as a Normative Problem

The power differentials between large organizational actors and most of their stakeholders are not incidental features of the communicative context. They are structurally constitutive of it. A major corporation engaging with a community residents' group, a regulatory agency engaging with a public interest organization, a nation state engaging with a foreign civil society — in each case, the organizational actor brings to the relationship resources that the stakeholder cannot match: financial resources for sustained engagement, informational resources from specialized expertise, institutional resources from established relationships with decision-makers, and communicative resources from professional communications infrastructure.

Classical rhetoric did not theorize this kind of asymmetry. The Athenian assembly and law courts involved participants with broadly comparable standing; the significant differentials were those of reputation, eloquence, and social position rather than the vast resource asymmetries of modern organizational life. The ethics of rhetoric in conditions of radical resource asymmetry requires additional principles that the classical framework did not develop.

Two principles follow from the asymmetry problem.

### The Transparency Principle

The first principle is the transparency requirement: organizational actors engaging in strategic communication with stakeholders have an obligation to be identifiable as interested parties, to disclose the nature of their interests, and not to represent organizational advocacy as neutral information, independent expertise, or public-spirited communication when it is not.

The moral basis of this principle is the rational agency standard. An audience evaluating an argument adjusts their evaluation based on who is making the argument and what interests they represent. Arguments advanced by interested parties are discounted appropriately; arguments advanced by apparently independent sources are evaluated at face value. When an organizational actor communicates through channels that appear independent — through front organizations, through manufactured grassroots movements, through the strategic management of apparently neutral expert testimony — they are creating a systematic informational asymmetry that undermines the audience's ability to exercise rational agency. The audience cannot properly evaluate the argument because they are deceived about its provenance.

The transparency principle does not require that organizational actors abandon their advocacy positions or pretend to be neutral. A corporation has every right to advocate publicly for regulatory outcomes that serve its interests, as long as it does so as what it is — a corporation with identified interests — rather than as something it is not. The principle disqualifies deceptive provenance, not organizational advocacy.

The power asymmetry makes this principle especially important because the resource differential means that large organizations are uniquely capable of sustaining the infrastructure required to manufacture apparent independence: fully staffed advocacy organizations, research institutes, media outlets, and public-facing experts whose organizational affiliation and funding is not disclosed. The capacity to deploy this infrastructure amplifies the distorting effect of non-transparent communication in ways that have no classical precedent.

### The Proportionality Principle

The second principle that follows from the asymmetry problem is proportionality: the sophistication and intensity of the strategic communication apparatus deployed must bear a reasonable relationship to the legitimate stakes of the engagement for the communicating organization.

This principle requires more explanation, because it is less intuitively obvious than the transparency requirement. The argument is this: the deployment of a comprehensive intelligence architecture — sustained stakeholder monitoring, posture assessment, targeted messaging development, and calibrated engagement campaigns — represents a qualitatively different communicative act than conventional organizational advocacy. It brings to bear an analytical and communicative infrastructure that most stakeholders cannot match and, in many cases, cannot even recognize is being deployed. When this infrastructure is deployed against stakeholders with relatively limited resources and limited awareness of the nature of the engagement, the power asymmetry is not merely incidental to the interaction — it is the operative mechanism through which the organizational position is advanced.

Proportionality does not prohibit sophisticated communications in high-stakes situations. An organization seeking regulatory approval for a significant project, engaging with communities affected by its operations, or navigating a genuine public controversy about its conduct has legitimate reason to invest seriously in its stakeholder communications. The stakeholders in these situations have genuine interests at stake, and a well-resourced organization communicating with integrity under these conditions is not doing anything that requires justification beyond the quality of its communications.

What proportionality does prohibit is the deployment of comprehensive influence infrastructure in contexts where the purpose is not to advance a legitimate organizational interest through well-grounded advocacy but to neutralize or manage stakeholders whose independent judgment might otherwise constrain organizational behavior. The difference between these two purposes is not always obvious from the outside, but the normative distinction between them is real.

## V. The Temporal Dimension: The Ethics of Sustained Engagement

### A Genuinely New Problem

The temporal dimension of organizational rhetoric is the domain where the existing philosophical literature is most inadequate. Classical rhetoric was theorized for episodic communicative events: a speech, a trial, an exchange. The ethical assessment of the *Rhetoric* is an assessment of a single communicative act in a bounded context. Habermas's framework, despite its ambitions toward a theory of communicative rationality embedded in social practice, is ultimately structured around an idealized deliberative event — the ideal speech situation — rather than around sustained relational dynamics. Grunig's model acknowledges long-term relationships but evaluates them against the standard of ongoing symmetrical exchange rather than developing specific ethical principles for the cumulative effects of sustained intelligence-grounded engagement.

Organizational strategic communication is not episodic. The SCI architecture is designed for sustained engagement over months and years: continuous posture assessment, recurring intelligence collection, calibrated deployment of arguments at kairotic moments, and iterative adaptation of the engagement approach based on monitored outcomes. This temporal structure generates ethical questions that cannot be answered by standards developed for single communicative events.

### The Epistemic Environment Problem

The most serious temporal concern is what I will call the epistemic environment problem: the risk that sustained, sophisticated, one-sided engagement by a well-resourced organizational actor systematically shapes the stakeholder's epistemic environment in ways that compromise their capacity for independent judgment, not through any single deceptive act but through the cumulative effect of sustained influence.

Consider a regulatory official who, over three years, receives carefully designed briefings from an organization seeking a regulatory outcome, calibrated to their identified values and concerns, presenting evidence selected for its persuasive force, deployed at moments of maximal receptivity. No single briefing contains false information. No single engagement exploits cognitive vulnerabilities. Each exchange, evaluated individually, satisfies the rational agency standard. But the cumulative effect of three years of intelligent, one-sided epistemic shaping is that the official's understanding of the relevant issues has been constructed, to a significant degree, by a single interested party with the resources to invest in that construction.

This is not a hypothetical concern. It is the standard operating procedure of sophisticated organizational advocacy. Regulatory capture — the documented phenomenon by which regulatory agencies come to represent the interests of the industries they regulate — is produced in significant part through exactly this mechanism. The epistemic environment of regulatory officials is shaped, over careers, by sustained engagement from organized, well-resourced organizational actors whose interests are directly implicated in the regulations those officials develop. No individual act of communication needs to be manipulative for the cumulative process to undermine the independence of regulatory judgment.

### The Response: Three Additional Conditions

The epistemic environment problem does not disqualify sustained strategic engagement. It imposes three additional conditions on how it may be conducted.

**The independence preservation condition.** A communicating organization has an obligation not to monopolize the epistemic environment of stakeholders whose independent judgment is a legitimate public interest. In practice, this means organizations should not attempt to prevent stakeholders from accessing competing information, should not cultivate relationships in ways designed to make independent evaluation of opposing arguments feel like a betrayal of personal trust, and should acknowledge the existence of legitimate competing views rather than structuring communications to render them invisible. This is a demanding condition, but it follows directly from the rational agency standard applied to the cumulative temporal dimension.

**The genuine responsiveness condition.** An organization engaged in sustained stakeholder communication has an obligation of genuine responsiveness: a disposition to update its positions and objectives in response to stakeholder input that reveals legitimate competing interests or considerations the organization had not adequately weighted. This is the condition that distinguishes genuine engagement from sophisticated management of opposition. The organization that enters every engagement having already determined its outcome, and uses the engagement process to neutralize opposition rather than to genuinely incorporate legitimate stakeholder concerns, violates the rational agency standard at the level of the sustained relationship even if each individual interaction is technically honest.

This condition is empirically testable — not with perfect precision, but meaningfully. Organizations that never modify their positions or objectives in response to stakeholder engagement are providing evidence that the engagement is management rather than genuine consultation. Organizations that modify positions in response to well-grounded stakeholder input, including input that requires real changes in organizational behavior, are providing evidence that the engagement is genuine.

**The acknowledgment condition.** An organization engaged in sophisticated, intelligence-grounded stakeholder communication has an obligation not to systematically conceal the nature of that engagement from those being engaged. This is distinct from the transparency principle, which requires disclosure of organizational identity and interests. The acknowledgment condition requires that stakeholders who are the subjects of sustained intelligence-grounded engagement have some awareness that this is what is happening — that they are not simply being engaged in informal relationship development, but are parties in a structured communications program informed by intelligence about their posture and calibrated to their identified values and concerns.

In practice, this condition does not require organizations to hand stakeholders their Messaging Frameworks or disclose their posture assessments. It requires that the general nature of professional stakeholder communications be acknowledged rather than concealed beneath the guise of personal relationship and informal exchange. This is an area where current organizational practice frequently fails — where the professionalization of stakeholder management is systematically obscured behind the language of relationship and partnership in ways that prevent stakeholders from accurately understanding the nature of the engagement.

## VI. The Constitutive Question: Which Interests Justify Strategic Rhetoric

### The Most Demanding Condition

The three conditions developed above — institutional integrity, respect for rational agency, and the supplementary temporal conditions — specify the means by which organizational rhetoric may be conducted if it is to be legitimate. They are conditions on the how of organizational communication. The question addressed in this section is different: it concerns the what — the interests that organizational strategic communication may legitimately be used to advance.

This is the most philosophically demanding part of the normative framework, and it is the part where I am most conscious of working in genuinely new philosophical territory. The classical tradition was clear that rhetoric should serve the good of the polis — the collective wellbeing of the political community, not merely the interests of the speaker. But organizational actors are not the polis, and their interests are particular rather than universal. The question is whether there is a principled distinction between organizational interests for whose advancement the SCI apparatus is an appropriate instrument and organizational interests for which it is not.

### The Rawlsian Test

John Rawls's device of the veil of ignorance — asking what principles rational agents would choose if they did not know their position in the social arrangement being governed — is useful here as a heuristic rather than as a foundational argument. Applied to organizational communication, the test asks: if you did not know whether you were the organization deploying a comprehensive strategic communications apparatus or a stakeholder who was the subject of that apparatus, what conditions would you set on the use of such an apparatus?

The answer, I think, is this: you would endorse the use of sophisticated strategic communications for the advancement of organizational interests that you would recognize as legitimate contributors to social goods regardless of your position. You would endorse regulatory communication for an organization genuinely seeking to advance a beneficial project whose costs and benefits you would regard as a reasonable social trade-off. You would endorse community engagement for an organization genuinely managing its relationship with affected communities in ways that acknowledge and address real community interests. You would endorse public affairs communication for an organization genuinely representing a constituency whose interests deserve a voice in public deliberation.

You would not endorse the use of sophisticated strategic communications for the advancement of interests that benefit the organization exclusively at the expense of the stakeholders being engaged, particularly when those stakeholders lack the resources to mount a comparable communicative response. You would not endorse the use of intelligence-grounded communications to preempt or neutralize legitimate regulatory scrutiny, to manage community opposition to operations whose costs to the community are real and uncompensated, or to advance policy positions whose primary beneficiary is organizational and whose costs fall on stakeholders who are the subjects of the engagement.

### The Public Good Alignment Condition

The Rawlsian heuristic points toward a condition I will call public good alignment: the interests being advanced through organizational strategic communication must be capable of being justified by reference to genuine social goods that are not exhausted by the organizational interest itself.

This condition does not require organizational altruism. It does not require that organizational interests be identical with social goods, or that organizations must prove net social benefit before communicating. Most organizational interests have genuine social dimensions alongside their self-interested dimensions: an organization seeking regulatory approval for a new facility creates employment and provides a service; an organization managing its community relationships sustains the conditions for its continued operation and the economic activity it generates; an organization advocating for favorable regulation represents a legitimate perspective in a pluralistic deliberative process. These interests can be articulated by reference to social goods that transcend the organizational interest itself.

The condition disqualifies interests that cannot be justified in this way — interests that are purely extractive, that advance organizational benefit at the direct expense of the stakeholders being engaged, or that use the communicative apparatus to prevent legitimate public deliberation from producing constraints on organizational behavior. An organization using SCI's architecture to manage the communications of a predatory financial product in ways designed to prevent consumer awareness of its costs is not advancing an interest that can be justified by reference to social goods. An organization using the architecture to delay environmental regulation whose necessity is scientifically established is not advancing a legitimate organizational interest in the relevant sense — it is using the communicative apparatus to prevent the exercise of public authority that the facts of the situation warrant.

### The Honest Distribution Condition

A supplementary condition follows from the public good alignment requirement: organizations must be honest about the distribution of the costs and benefits of the interests they are advancing, rather than structuring all communications to emphasize benefits and conceal costs.

Most organizational interests involve genuine trade-offs. A new infrastructure project creates employment and provides a service but also imposes environmental and community costs. A regulatory position that serves organizational interests also has effects on consumers, workers, communities, or competitors that may not be beneficial. An honest representation of the organizational position acknowledges these trade-offs rather than presenting only the benefits — even when the honest case for the position is still compelling on balance.

The honest distribution condition is demanding in practice. Organizations are not required to provide ammunition to their opponents; they are not required to lead their advocacy with the costs of the positions they are advancing. But they are required not to systematically structure all their communications so that costs are invisible, so that stakeholders who have legitimate interests on the cost side of the trade-off are never given the information they need to identify and articulate those interests. This is the condition most frequently violated in sophisticated organizational advocacy, and its violation produces the pseudo-consensus dynamic that Habermas correctly identifies as a pathology of modern institutional communication.

## VII. The Societal Dimension: Organizational Rhetoric and Democratic Health

The five conditions developed in this document address the ethics of organizational rhetoric primarily at the level of the organization-stakeholder relationship: what integrity requires, how rational agency must be respected, what power asymmetry obligates, how sustained engagement must be managed, and which interests justify the strategic communications apparatus. These conditions specify the ethics of the *conduct* of organizational rhetoric in specific relationships.

But organizational rhetoric is also a participatory act in the broader public sphere — the arena of democratic deliberation through which societies negotiate values, distribute power, and constrain institutional conduct. At this societal level, a further normative question must be asked: does organizational rhetoric, conducted within the conditions specified above, contribute to or detract from the quality of democratic public life? Does the organization's rhetoric enrich or impoverish the agonistic arena in which democratic societies resolve their conflicts?

This is the question that agonistic democratic theory is designed to address. Unlike Habermas's communicative action framework, which assesses communicative legitimacy by reference to an ideal of rational consensus achievable through power-free deliberation, agonism begins from a more realistic premise: democratic societies are constitutively plural, power is inevitable, and the proper aspiration of democratic rhetoric is not the elimination of conflict but its transformation from antagonism — the friend/enemy relationship that seeks destruction — into *agonism*, the adversarial relationship between parties who recognize each other's legitimacy and contest each other's positions within a shared commitment to democratic participation. The philosophical vocabulary for this transformation is drawn from the agonistic democratic tradition: Arendt's foundational account of politics as action in the public realm (*The Human Condition*, 1958) provides the wellspring — the image of political life as the space where plurality is exercised rather than resolved. Mouffe (*The Return of the Political*, 1993; *The Democratic Paradox*, 2000) makes the antagonism/agonism distinction explicit and central, drawing on Schmitt's friend/enemy logic to name antagonism and then transforming it: the democratic achievement is not to eliminate the adversarial relation but to convert the enemy into an adversary whose legitimacy one acknowledges. Honig (*Political Theory and the Displacement of Politics*, 1993) connects Arendt to agonism most directly, arguing against the "domestication" of political conflict into administrative consensus. Connolly (*Identity/Difference*, 1991) extends the tradition with his account of how pluralism requires an ethos that affirms the contingency of one's own commitments.

Applied to organizational rhetoric, the agonistic framework generates a societal-level normative condition: **organizational rhetoric is democratically legitimate when it enriches rather than impoverishes the agonistic public sphere** — when it contributes to public deliberation in ways that sustain plurality and the vitality of democratic contest, rather than using organizational resources to suppress counter-voices, manufacture apparent consensus, or place legitimate questions beyond the reach of public deliberation.

### Co-optation: The Agonistic Form of the Say-Do Gap

The agonistic framework names a specific failure mode that the institutional integrity analysis in Section II identifies in organizational terms but does not yet name precisely. Davidson (2018) calls it *co-optation*: the process through which organizations adopt the language of their critics — the vocabulary of community investment, environmental stewardship, social responsibility — without implementing the substantive changes in conduct that the criticized behavior demands. Co-optation is a rhetorical process in which the organizational rhetoric of the critic is appropriated and the organization is positioned as the solution to the very problem it has generated, without the conduct that generated the criticism changing materially.

Co-optation is the agonistic form of the say-do gap. At the level of individual organizational-stakeholder relationships, the say-do gap is a failure of institutional integrity: the organization communicates a character it does not possess. At the societal level, co-optation is a failure of democratic participation: the organization uses its rhetorical resources to absorb and neutralize the normative challenge that legitimate criticism represents, restoring a communicative settlement in which organizational conduct is shielded from accountability — not through force or censorship but through the more sophisticated mechanism of rhetorical appropriation. The agonistic contest — the legitimate democratic challenge to organizational conduct — is neutralized not through genuine engagement with the criticism but through rhetorical encirclement of it.

Co-optation fails the institutional integrity condition and the agonistic condition simultaneously and for related reasons. It fails the integrity condition because the organization communicates a character it does not possess — performing the values it is violating. It fails the agonistic condition because it uses rhetorical sophistication to suppress a democratic challenge that legitimate organizational conduct has invited, preventing the agonistic contest from producing its proper democratic outcome: either genuine modification of organizational conduct, or honest defense of that conduct on its own terms, in public, to adversaries who retain the capacity to contest the defense.

### The Agonistic Standard

From the agonistic framework, four dimensions of the societal-level normative condition can be specified.

**Counter-voice engagement rather than suppression.** Legitimate organizational rhetoric engages counter-voices — acknowledges the legitimacy of opposition, responds to the substance of critical arguments, and participates in the rhetorical contest rather than using organizational resources to render the contest structurally impossible. An organization that engages legitimate criticism honestly — even adversarially, even while maintaining its position — is an agonistic participant. An organization that uses communications resources to make legitimate criticism invisible, implausible, or impossible to articulate effectively is undermining the democratic condition it depends on for its own legitimacy.

**Acceptance of the contingency of outcomes.** The agonistic ethos requires that organizations accept that the rhetorical contest may not resolve in their favor, and that this outcome, produced by genuine deliberation, is a legitimate democratic result. An organization that uses resource advantages to ensure that the contest cannot produce an outcome unfavorable to its interests — through regulatory capture, through systematic shaping of decision-makers' epistemic environments, through sustained preemptive communications that prevent opposition from organizing — is not an agonistic participant. It is a monopolist of democratic discourse.

**Honest adversarial identification.** Agonism does not require neutrality or the performance of disinterest. Organizations are entitled to advocate for their interests with full deployment of their communicative resources, as long as they do so as what they are: interested parties advancing specific positions that represent specific values and specific interests. The agonistic requirement is honest adversarial identification — the organization presents itself as a party to the contest, not as the neutral arbiter or the voice of the general good.

**Non-naturalization of existing settlements.** The agonistic framework warns specifically against rhetoric that presents existing power relationships as natural, inevitable, or beyond legitimate challenge. Organizations whose communications systematically work to naturalize arrangements advantageous to themselves — presenting regulatory frameworks they have shaped as the objective public interest, presenting industry practices as inevitable features of how markets work, presenting communities affected by their operations as having no legitimate basis for alternatives — are using rhetorical resources to foreclose democratic deliberation about how things could be otherwise.

### The Challenge to SCI

The agonistic framework presents a direct challenge to any intelligence-driven organizational communications architecture, and intellectual honesty requires engaging it explicitly rather than noting it in passing.

The SCI model is designed to assess stakeholder postures, identify the arguments most likely to move each stakeholder toward required actions, and deploy those arguments at moments of maximal effect through sustained engagement. This is, by design, a systematic apparatus for organizational influence. The agonistic question is direct: does SCI help organizations become better agonistic participants — more honest, more genuinely engaged with stakeholder positions, more willing to modify their conduct in response to legitimate criticism? Or does it make organizations more sophisticated at pursuing predetermined outcomes — better at neutralizing opposition, better at managing the epistemic environments of decision-makers, better at co-optation?

The architecture can serve either purpose. A sophisticated organization could use exactly the tools of posture intelligence and evidence-grounded messaging to construct a more credible and durable program of co-optation: identifying precisely what language its critics use, adopting exactly that language, and deploying it with analytical sophistication that simple opportunistic rhetoric lacks. The evidence accountability requirements and rational agency commitments of the architecture do not prevent this. What they do is make explicit what such use requires an organization to acknowledge it is doing: violating the institutional integrity condition, the rational agency condition, and the agonistic condition simultaneously — deploying a governance apparatus designed for legitimate organizational rhetoric in deliberate service of institutional dishonesty.

What the SCI architecture can genuinely deliver, for organizations that deploy it with the normative commitments these conditions require, is something the agonistic framework values: more honest, more evidence-grounded, and more genuinely responsive stakeholder engagement. An organization using the intelligence architecture to understand what stakeholders actually believe — not to identify how to neutralize their objections, but to understand what genuine concerns underlie their positions and whether those concerns have merit — is using the architecture in the service of agonistic participation. An organization whose alignment cycle's genuine responsiveness function produces actual modifications to organizational conduct in response to legitimate stakeholder input is practicing exactly the kind of organizational rhetoric that the agonistic framework endorses.

The agonistic condition therefore reinforces rather than contradicts the framework's other normative commitments. It adds the societal-level dimension: an organization that satisfies the institutional integrity condition, the rational agency condition, and the proportionality and transparency conditions is, in doing so, making the contribution to democratic deliberative quality that agonistic theory requires. The organization that communicates honestly, engages counter-voices substantively, accepts the contingency of its positions, and modifies its conduct when legitimate challenge requires it is not merely a responsible organizational actor. It is a responsible democratic participant.

## VIII. SCI as Normative Architecture

### What the Architecture Embeds

The preceding analysis makes it possible to assess the SCI architecture against the normative framework with precision. Three of the normative conditions developed above are structurally embedded in the architecture — not merely compatible with it, but enforced by its design.

**The institutional integrity condition** is addressed by the alignment cycle and the Strategic Communications Brief. The Brief's signal coherence review function is precisely the mechanism for detecting say-do gaps before they reach external audiences — identifying where organizational communications are inconsistent with organizational conduct, where different audiences are being told substantively incompatible things, and where the gap between stated commitment and observable behavior has become wide enough to constitute a communicative deception. The Brief institutionalizes the integrity check as a recurring governance requirement rather than leaving it to individual practitioner judgment.

The evidence accountability requirement further supports the integrity condition by preventing the intelligence architecture from being used to construct messaging that misrepresents the strength of the organizational case. Messaging Frameworks must be grounded in validated evidence; posture assessments must be traceable to observable behavior rather than to wishful assessment. These structural requirements mean that the architecture cannot easily be used to project false confidence about the organizational position or to manufacture the appearance of stakeholder support that does not exist.

**The rational agency condition** is addressed by the logos requirement embedded in the Messaging Framework structure and by the Gap-derived point architecture. The requirement that Information Points address genuine information deficits and that Messaging Points address genuine stakeholder values — both derived from intelligence gathered from the Stakeholder record rather than from the organization's preferred characterization of stakeholder concerns — is the structural commitment to engaging audiences as they actually are rather than as the organization wishes them to be. The evidence traceability requirement prevents Information Points from being grounded in unverifiable claims. Together, these structural features make it architecturally difficult (though not impossible) to use the system for communication that works around rational agency rather than through it.

**The temporal conditions** are partially addressed by the distributed ownership model and the intelligence record currency requirements. The requirement that records be maintained with defined cadence and that posture assessments be regularly updated prevents the architecture from solidifying into a static picture of stakeholder positions. More importantly, the alignment cycle's accountability function creates institutional pressure for genuine responsiveness: the Brief cannot be merely a coordination meeting if it is also a forum in which posture movements are assessed and the adequacy of current engagement approaches is evaluated. An organization that is never updating its positions in response to stakeholder input will face structural accountability questions within the Brief's review function.

### What the Architecture Embeds vs. What It Supports

Before specifying the architecture's limits, it is worth being precise about which of Part IV's normative conditions are *structurally embedded* in the architecture and which are *governance commitments* that the architecture supports but does not mechanically enforce.

The **institutional integrity condition** and the **rational agency condition** are substantially embedded. The alignment cycle's signal coherence review detects say-do gaps before they reach external audiences; the evidence accountability requirement prevents Messaging Frameworks from being grounded in unverified claims; the Gap-derived point structure constrains communications to engage genuine stakeholder states rather than manufactured ones. These structural features make it architecturally difficult — though not impossible — to violate these conditions while using the architecture conscientiously.

The **transparency condition**, the **acknowledgment condition**, the **proportionality condition**, and the **public good alignment condition** are governance commitments that the architecture supports but cannot enforce. They are organizational obligations — matters of institutional character and executive judgment — that the architecture's intelligence and accountability infrastructure makes it easier to honor and harder to evade, but that ultimately depend on the moral seriousness of the organization deploying it. The alignment cycle's normative conditions review (specified in Part III) is the institutional mechanism that makes these commitments subject to recurring accountability rather than leaving them to individual practitioner discretion. But it cannot substitute for genuine organizational commitment to the conditions it reviews.

This distinction is not a limitation of the architecture's design. It is the correct scope for a governance architecture. Architecture governs process; character governs intent. The SCI framework provides the former in full; the latter is a precondition it can reinforce but not supply.

### What the Architecture Cannot Make Legitimate

It is equally important to specify what the SCI architecture cannot do — what forms of organizational communication the architecture cannot make legitimate regardless of how it is deployed.

**The architecture cannot substitute for institutional integrity.** An organization that maintains a systematic say-do gap — that communicates commitments that its resource allocation decisions and behavioral patterns systematically contradict — cannot use the SCI architecture to paper over that gap. The integrity condition is prior to the architecture; it is a condition on the organization's fundamental relationship with the truth about itself, and the architecture's design cannot compensate for its absence. An organization without institutional integrity that uses SCI's tools will produce communications that are technically coherent, evidence-grounded, and strategically sophisticated — and that are, at the level of organizational character, deceptive.

**The architecture cannot transform ineligible interests into legitimate ones.** The public good alignment condition is also prior to the architecture. The sophistication of the intelligence analysis and the quality of the Messaging Frameworks have no bearing on whether the interests being advanced can be justified by reference to social goods. An organization using SCI to advance interests that fail the public good alignment condition is deploying a well-designed apparatus for an illegitimate purpose.

**The architecture cannot compensate for violations of the transparency principle.** If an organizational actor uses the intelligence and messaging architecture to manage communications while concealing its identity and interests — through third-party channels, manufactured grassroots movements, or undisclosed organizational advocacy — the architecture's evidence accountability and rational agency commitments are irrelevant. The audience is still being deceived about the provenance of the communication, which is a violation of the rational agency standard at a level that the architecture cannot address.

These limits are not failures of the architecture. They are the appropriate scope of an architecture designed to make legitimate organizational communication more effective and rigorous. A governance tool that can make illegitimate communication legitimate would not be a normative architecture; it would be an instrument of sophisticated dishonesty.

## IX. The Philosophical Resolution: Rhetoric, Interest, and the Good

The normative framework developed in this document rests on a central philosophical claim that can now be stated precisely: **organizational rhetoric is legitimate when it serves genuine interests through means that respect the rational agency of those being persuaded, within a framework of institutional integrity and honest acknowledgment of the social costs and benefits at stake.**

This claim resolves the Habermasian challenge without accepting the Habermasian premise. Habermas was correct that pseudo-consensus is a distinct pathology and that rational agency is the central value. He was wrong that the legitimacy of communication requires the transcendence of strategic intent. Strategic intent — the pursuit of genuine organizational interests through deliberate communicative means — is not the problem. The problem is strategic intent pursued through means that undermine rather than respect the audience's rational agency, by communicators whose stated character does not correspond to their actual conduct, in service of interests that cannot be justified by reference to genuine social goods.

The resolution also resists the Grunig concession in a different direction. Grunig was right that legitimacy requires more than sophisticated management of stakeholder perception — that genuine mutual adjustment is the standard rather than symmetrical performance. But the normative framework developed here does not require organizations to abandon their interests or to be prepared to change their strategic objectives in every engagement. It requires that when legitimate stakeholder input reveals genuine competing interests that the organization has not adequately weighted, the organization responds with genuine modification rather than intensified persuasion. This is a weaker condition than pure symmetry and a stronger condition than managed asymmetry. It is the condition that actual organizational communication can meet — and must meet to be defensible.

The agonistic framework adds a dimension the Habermas/Grunig critique does not reach: the ethics of organizational rhetoric is not only a matter of what the organization does *to* its stakeholders but of what it does *to* democratic public life. An organization can satisfy the rational agency standard in each individual engagement while, through the aggregate pattern of its communications, systematically impoverishing the conditions under which democratic deliberation about its conduct can occur. The agonistic condition addresses this aggregate dimension, requiring that the organization's rhetoric sustain rather than corrupt the democratic arena in which its own legitimacy is constituted.

The philosophical tradition I bring to this problem is one that has always insisted on the relationship between rhetoric and ethics. The sophists — then and now — have argued that rhetoric is a neutral technique, available for any purpose, evaluated by its effectiveness rather than by its ends. I rejected that argument in my first life, and I reject it here. The means of persuasion are not morally neutral. They carry within them commitments about the nature of the audience — as rational agents to be respected or as psychological objects to be moved — and those commitments determine the moral character of the communicative act. And those commitments extend beyond individual audiences to the democratic community that those audiences constitute.

The organizational context does not change this judgment. It complicates the application of it, because organizations are not persons, because power asymmetries are more radical, because the temporal dimension of sustained influence has no classical precedent. Part IV has attempted to work through these complications honestly rather than to dissolve them by analogy. The result is a normative framework that is genuinely demanding without being utopian — one that preserves the classical tradition's insistence on the ethical dimension of rhetoric while translating it into conditions that actual organizational communicators operating in actual institutional contexts can understand, assess, and be accountable to.

The SCI architecture, deployed by organizations that meet these conditions, is not merely an effective governance tool. It is a defensible one.

## Conclusion: The Conditions of Legitimate Organizational Rhetoric

The normative framework developed in this document specifies six conditions for the legitimate deployment of organizational strategic communication:

1. **Institutional integrity**: the alignment between an organization's stated commitments, resource allocation decisions, and observable behavioral patterns. The say-do gap is not merely a strategic liability; it is a form of institutional vice that invalidates the communicative relationship on which organizational rhetoric depends.

2. **Rational agency respect**: the requirement that persuasive means work *through* rather than *around* the rational agency of those being addressed — providing genuine evidence, honest argument, and honest identification of organizational interests, rather than exploiting cognitive vulnerabilities, manufactured urgency, or concealed provenance.

3. **Proportionality and transparency in asymmetric contexts**: the recognition that radical resource asymmetry between organizational actors and their stakeholders creates specific obligations — to be identifiable as interested parties, to not deploy sophisticated influence infrastructure to neutralize rather than engage legitimate stakeholder judgment, and to not use communicative resources to manufacture the appearance of independent support for organizational positions.

4. **The temporal conditions of sustained engagement**: the obligation, over time, to preserve stakeholder epistemic independence, to be genuinely responsive to legitimate stakeholder input, and to acknowledge the nature of professional strategic engagement rather than systematically concealing it behind the language of informal relationship.

5. **Public good alignment**: the condition that the interests being advanced through strategic communication be capable of justification by reference to genuine social goods — that they not be purely extractive, that the distribution of costs and benefits be honestly represented, and that the communications apparatus not be used to prevent legitimate public deliberation from producing appropriate constraints on organizational behavior.

6. **Agonistic democratic participation**: the societal-level condition that organizational rhetoric enrich rather than impoverish the democratic public sphere — that it engage counter-voices rather than suppress them, accept the contingency of its own positions, present the organization honestly as an interested party rather than a neutral arbiter, and resist the naturalization of power settlements that insulate organizational conduct from legitimate democratic challenge. The specific failure mode this condition prohibits — co-optation, the appropriation of critical language without the substantive behavioral change the criticism demands — is simultaneously a failure of institutional integrity and a failure of democratic participation.

These conditions do not exhaust the ethics of organizational communication. They do not address the full range of communicative choices that organizational actors face in their day-to-day practice. But they specify the normative foundation on which the SCI architecture rests — the conditions that must be met for the architecture to function as a governance tool in the service of legitimate interests rather than as a sophistication amplifier for institutional dishonesty.

The purpose of the SCI framework is not to make all organizational communication more effective. It is to make legitimate organizational communication more effective — more rigorously intelligence-grounded, more consistently evidence-accountable, more institutionally coherent — while making the conditions of its legitimacy explicit. Part IV has made those conditions explicit. They are demanding. They should be.

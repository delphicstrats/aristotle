## Introduction: The Purpose of Theory in a Governance Framework

Part I established that strategic communications governance failure is structural, persistent, and unsolved by existing tool categories. The argument reached a conclusion: the required response is governance — an institutional mechanism that maintains continuous intelligence on stakeholder posture, coordinates all organizational signals, and creates accountability for coherence across distributed functions.

But a conclusion about what is required is not yet a specification of what the governance architecture should look like. Before we can design an institution, we need a theory of what the institution is trying to produce and why its design choices will produce it. Without that theory, the governance architecture is an arrangement of features rather than a coherent system derived from first principles.

Part II provides the theoretical foundation. Three intellectual traditions — classical rhetoric, Resource Dependence Theory, and the DoD Strategic Communications framework — together supply what is needed: a theory of why stakeholder posture matters strategically, a theory of how it changes through communicative action, and a theory of how influence at scale must be organized. None of these traditions alone is sufficient. Each addresses a different question about the governance problem, and the SCI framework requires all three answers.

The goal of this section is not to survey these traditions comprehensively — the artifact documents in the Lyceum provide that depth — but to develop their specific contributions to the governance argument, show where they converge, and identify what their synthesis generates that none of them provides independently. That synthesis produces three constructs original to the SCI framework: the posture construct, the evidence architecture, and the governance cycle. These three constructs are the theoretical foundations on which Part III's model specification rests. A fourth element is developed in Section VI: not a construct in the same sense, but a philosophical taxonomy of organizational audience types derived from institutional logics theory — the analytical structure that governs how Part III's model is organized and applied across the four stakeholder domains.

## I. Classical Rhetoric: The Theory of Persuasion as Governance Requirement

The first question the governance framework must answer is mechanistic: *what produces change in stakeholder posture?* Resource Dependence Theory establishes that posture matters strategically; the JIC establishes that influence must be organized at scale. But neither tradition provides an account of what makes a communicative act work — what enables a signal to cross the gap between sender and receiver and produce the intended cognitive and behavioral effect. Classical rhetoric provides that account.

### The Three Modes of Persuasion

Aristotle's *Rhetoric* identifies three modes of persuasion — ethos, logos, and pathos — as exhaustive: every persuasive effect is produced through some combination of the three. Understanding each as a distinct mechanism, rather than as interchangeable rhetorical devices, is essential to the governance architecture.

**Ethos** is the credibility of the communicating party as perceived by the audience. It is not claimed but demonstrated — built incrementally through the consistency of character and action over time. Aristotle specifies three components: *phronesis* (practical wisdom, the perception of competence and sound judgment), *eunoia* (goodwill, the perception that the communicator acts in the audience's interests), and *arete* (moral virtue, the perception that the communicator is honest). All three must be present; the failure of any one destroys the persuasive capacity of the communicative act.

The organizational translation of ethos is the concept on which SCI's entire architecture rests. An organization's institutional ethos is not a property of any individual communicator. It is the aggregate impression formed by audiences across all observable organizational behavior — what every function says, what every function does, and whether those signals cohere. No individual communicator, however skilled, can maintain institutional ethos against the contradictions generated by distributed organizational actors operating without governance. Ethos, at organizational scale, is a governance problem.

A complete account of organizational ethos requires engaging three theoretical traditions, each of which captures a dimension of the organizational translation that the others alone cannot reach.

Aristotle's account is foundational: ethos is constructed within the discourse event through demonstrated phronesis, arete, and eunoia. The rhetor builds credibility by the quality of what they say and how they say it. For individual speakers, ethos is something the speech achieves — an effect of the rhetorical performance, not merely its precondition.

But Cicero's account identifies a structure that organizational rhetoric makes unavoidable: ethos is not primarily constructed within discourse events but *revealed* by them. Reputation — the accumulated social judgment of a character constituted by a long history of conduct — precedes every communicative act. The speech confirms, sustains, or undermines a standing that pre-exists it. For Cicero, the *vir bonus* — the good man — speaks well not by constructing goodness in the act of speaking but by disclosing a goodness that his life has made real. This structural priority of character over communication is even more pronounced in organizational life than in individual rhetoric. An organization's institutional character is constituted not by any single communicative act but by the cumulative, observable pattern of its conduct across years and across functions: what it says, what it does, whether these align, and whether the alignment holds under pressure. No discourse event creates that character ex nihilo. The communications function governs what the organizational record *discloses*. The governance architecture exists to ensure that what is disclosed is a character the organization actually possesses — which requires working backward from communications to operations, not forward from strategy to message.

Kenneth Burke extends the account into the constitutive dimension that neither Aristotle nor Cicero fully theorizes: how does organizational rhetoric achieve the alignment of interests that sustains stakeholder cooperation beyond any single engagement? Burke's concept of *identification* — the process through which audiences come to see their own interests, values, and identity as aligned with the rhetor's — explains what successful organizational rhetoric achieves at its deepest level. Persuasion in the Aristotelian sense changes a specific belief or disposition at a specific moment. Identification in the Burkean sense creates *consubstantiality* — shared substance, common ground — in which sustained stakeholder cooperation is not the product of a succession of successful arguments but of a genuine alignment of purpose. Consubstantiality is the positive aspiration that the SCI architecture exists to enable: not the mechanical production of favorable stakeholder behavior, but the creation of genuine alignment between organizational and stakeholder interests through honest, evidence-grounded, sustained engagement.

**Logos** is the logical structure of the argument — the claim, the evidence, and the reasoning that connects them. Rhetoric's distinction from sophistry is located precisely here: the rhetorician makes genuinely valid arguments from true premises; the sophist uses the appearance of valid argument without the substance. This distinction is not merely ethical; it is practical. Arguments grounded in false premises are discovered, and discovery destroys the ethos that future arguments depend on. In the current information environment, where stakeholder audiences have unprecedented access to the operational reality behind organizational claims, the epistemological requirement and the strategic requirement converge: validity is not an aspiration but a condition of durable influence.

**Pathos** is the resonance of the argument with the audience's values, interests, and frame of reference. Understanding the audience is the precondition for effective communication — not because the rhetorician exploits emotional states, but because arguments are received through the interpretive frameworks audiences bring to them. The same argument, received by audiences with different values and different prior experience of the communicating organization, will produce different effects. Governance of the pathos dimension requires intelligence about audiences — not just who they are, but how they reason, what they value, and what their prior experience of the organization has been.

### The Organizational Rhetor: An Original Problem

The translation of classical rhetoric to the organizational context confronts a problem that the classical tradition did not face and for which it provides no ready solution. Classical rhetoric assumed a unified speaker — an individual who possesses a character, constructs arguments, and bears personal accountability for the persuasive act. Organizations are not unified speakers. Cheney (1991) named the difficulty precisely: "We are perplexed by the practical and theoretical aspects of messages that cannot be easily identified with individual persons." Who speaks when an organization speaks? Who bears the ethos that organizational communications require? On whom does the responsibility for the honesty or dishonesty of organizational rhetoric fall?

The SCI governance architecture provides a principled answer to this question. The organizational rhetor is not any individual communicator, nor the communications function as such, nor the CEO speaking in their organizational capacity. The organizational rhetor is the governance system itself: the institutional structure of intelligence records, distributed ownership, accountability mechanisms, and the Strategic Communications Brief that coordinates the aggregate organizational signal. It is this system — not any individual within it — that determines whether the organizational signal is coherent, evidence-grounded, and aligned with the organization's actual character. Individual communicators within the system perform specific discourse acts; the ethos those acts draw on is maintained by the governance system as a whole.

This resolution carries a consequence that must be stated explicitly: the governance architecture is not an administrative mechanism layered over the rhetorical function. It is the *institutional form that organizational rhetoric takes*. The governance system is the speaker. Governance does not support rhetoric; governance is organizational rhetoric's mode of existence at organizational scale.

### The Ontological Commitment

The functional and ontological views of organizational rhetoric generate different governance implications, and the SCI Framework's theoretical commitments require explicitly adopting the latter.

The *functional* view — the dominant assumption in management-facing conceptions of communications — treats organizational rhetoric as a tool: one function among others, engaged when needed, evaluated by its effectiveness in achieving strategic goals. This view is inadequate on its own terms. Because every organizational action constitutes a signal — the JIC's integration imperative — the "communications function" cannot be isolated from the other functions whose actions are also communicative. An operational decision about supply chain, a personnel appointment, a capital allocation choice: each constitutes a signal that reaches external audiences alongside formal communications and is interpreted by stakeholders in light of what the organization has said. The integration challenge cannot be met by a tool that is sometimes deployed; it requires a governance function that is always operative.

The functional view is also philosophically inadequate because it mischaracterizes what organizations are. Organizations do not use rhetoric to present themselves to the world; organizations *are* their rhetorical relationships with their environment — constituted by the cumulative communicative acts, intended and unintended, that establish what they stand for and whether they are trustworthy. Remove the rhetoric and what remains is not an organization with communications capability; it is an aggregation of assets without social existence.

The *ontological* view, which the SCI Framework adopts, treats organizational rhetoric as constitutive: organizations are rhetorical actors in the deepest sense, constituted by and constituting their relationships with their stakeholder environments. The governance architecture specified in Part III is the institutional form of this ontological commitment — not a process added to organizational operations, but the institutional mechanism through which organizations maintain and exercise the rhetorical capacity that is their organizational existence.

### Kairos and the Governance of Timing

The concept of *kairos* — the right argument, at the right moment, through the right channel, for the specific audience — captures the integrative dimension of rhetorical judgment. No communicative act exists in isolation from its context. An argument that would succeed in one informational environment fails in another; an engagement that arrives at the right stage of a stakeholder's decision process succeeds where the same engagement at the wrong stage fails.

Kairos cannot be exercised without continuous intelligence about the stakeholder's current state: their current posture, the current state of relevant decision processes, the recent history of their experience of the organization, the surrounding informational environment. The governance function that maintains this intelligence is what makes kairotic judgment possible at organizational scale.

### The Anti-Sophistry Constraint as Design Requirement

The rhetorical tradition's insistence on the distinction between persuasion and manipulation — rhetoric in service of truth versus sophistry in service of impression — is not a pious addition to the governance framework. It is a structural requirement that shapes the evidence architecture.

SCI's governance framework is designed to support the development and consistent deployment of honest, credible, evidence-grounded communication — not the manufacture of misleading impressions. This commitment is not primarily ethical in its justification, though it is ethical in its character. It is strategic: deceptive communication, when discovered, destroys the ethos that makes future influence possible. In an environment where stakeholder audiences have increasing capacity to assess organizational claims against operational reality, the practical case for honesty and the ethical case for honesty arrive at the same conclusion.

The evidence architecture — the requirement that all intelligence assessments be grounded in validated, traceable citations — is the institutional operationalization of this requirement. It makes honesty structurally easier than deception by building the evidentiary discipline into the governance process itself.

## II. Resource Dependence Theory: The Strategic Stakes of Stakeholder Posture

Classical rhetoric explains how posture change is produced through communicative action. Resource Dependence Theory establishes *why* posture change has strategic stakes — why managing stakeholder posture is a strategic survival function rather than a relationship or reputation function.

### The Dependence Thesis

RDT's foundational claim, developed by Pfeffer and Salancik (1978), is that organizations are open systems: they depend on external actors for the resources, legitimacy, and conditions necessary for sustained operation. These dependencies are not incidental features of organizational life that can be managed away through strategic planning or internal capability development. They are structural: external actors control resources the organization requires and cannot produce unilaterally.

The governance consequence follows directly. Because critical resources are controlled externally, the organization's strategic capacity depends on maintaining the quality of relationships with the actors who control them. This is not a soft claim about the value of good relationships. It is a structural claim about the conditions under which organizations can pursue their objectives. Stakeholder posture — the disposition of external actors to provide or withhold resources, cooperation, and legitimacy — is therefore not a peripheral concern to be managed by a peripheral function. It is the determinant of strategic capacity.

### Legitimacy as a Strategic Resource

The RDT contribution that is most important for SCI's theoretical foundation is the recognition of legitimacy as a resource in the same sense that capital, labor, and materials are resources. Legitimacy — the accumulated social judgment that an organization has the right to operate, to pursue its objectives, and to make claims on other stakeholders — determines whether the organization's communications will be received, whether its regulatory applications will be approved, whether its community engagement will be productive, and whether its market relationships will be sustained.

Legitimacy is controlled by external stakeholders. It cannot be unilaterally claimed or maintained; it is granted and withdrawn by the audiences whose assessments determine it. And it is built or destroyed by the aggregate signal of organizational behavior over time — by whether the organization's actions and communications cohere with the values and expectations of the stakeholder communities on which it depends.

The Edelman data makes this concrete. Organizations that have sustained say-do gaps — that have consistently communicated commitments their operations do not sustain — face measurable legitimacy deficits that manifest as stakeholder distrust, reduced regulatory goodwill, community opposition, and diminished market credibility. These are not soft reputational consequences; they are strategic capacity constraints with direct impact on the organization's ability to pursue its objectives.

### The Power-Dependence Logic and Posture Management

RDT's power-dependence framework provides the analytical structure for prioritizing stakeholder governance attention. The power of an external actor over the organization is a function of the organization's dependence on them: the greater the criticality of the resources they control, the fewer the alternatives available, and the more concentrated their control, the greater their structural power over the organization's outcomes.

This logic has direct implications for how SCI's governance architecture should allocate intelligence and engagement resources. Not all stakeholders are equally consequential; not all posture gaps are equally costly. The governance function must maintain a continuously updated assessment of the dependence structure — which actors control which resources critical to which strategic priorities — so that intelligence investment and engagement effort are proportional to strategic stakes.

RDT also provides the strategic response taxonomy that governance must navigate: buffering (reducing exposure to stakeholder volatility), bridging (building collaborative relationships with resource-controlling actors), and cooptation (incorporating stakeholder interests into governance). Each response is appropriate under different conditions of dependence and power. The governance architecture must support this tactical differentiation rather than applying a uniform engagement approach to all stakeholder relationships.

### The Structural Connection to Rhetoric

RDT and classical rhetoric address the same relationship from different analytical directions. Rhetoric describes the communicative mechanism through which stakeholder posture changes; RDT explains why that change has strategic consequences. The connection between them is ethos: institutional credibility — the accumulated organizational ethos that rhetoric requires as a precondition for effective persuasion — is precisely what RDT identifies as legitimacy, the resource that determines strategic capacity.

This connection is not coincidental. Both traditions recognize that the organization's relationship to its external environment is mediated by credibility: the degree to which external actors believe the organization is competent, trustworthy, and aligned with their interests. Building and maintaining this credibility is therefore simultaneously a rhetorical requirement (it is what makes future persuasion possible) and a resource management requirement (it is what enables continued strategic operation). Governance is the institutional mechanism that makes this maintenance possible at organizational scale.

## III. The Strategic Communications Framework: The Process Architecture

Classical rhetoric provides the theory of persuasion. RDT provides the theory of strategic stakes. The DoD Strategic Communications Joint Integrating Concept (2009) provides the third essential element: a theory of how influence must be organized at the scale and complexity of modern organizational operations.

### The Integration Imperative

The JIC's most important contribution to SCI's theoretical foundation is the insistence that *strategic communication is an organizational function, not a communications function*. The concept's formulation: every organizational action, down to individual operational decisions, constitutes a signal that will be received and interpreted by audiences, whether intended or not. Strategic communication is therefore not what the communications department does. It is the coherent management of the entire organizational signal environment — everything the organization says and everything it does — in service of the influence objectives that strategic priorities require.

This claim has direct structural consequences. If all organizational actions constitute signals, then governance of those signals cannot be confined to a single function. Legal decisions signal commitment to regulatory norms. Operations decisions signal priorities and values. Personnel appointments signal organizational culture and direction. Financial commitments signal strategic seriousness. Each of these signals reaches external audiences alongside formal communications, and stakeholders interpret them together, not separately.

The governance function must therefore encompass the full organizational signal environment — not by giving the communications function authority over operational decisions, but by creating the institutional architecture through which the communications implications of operational decisions are assessed against stakeholder posture requirements before those decisions are finalized or announced. This is the theoretical foundation for SCI's distributed ownership model: governance that is embedded across functional roles rather than concentrated in a single department.

### The Adaptive Cycle as Process Architecture

The JIC specifies a four-step process for strategic communications management: identify and understand target audiences; develop and coordinate signals designed to reach those audiences effectively; monitor and measure the effects of those signals on audience perceptions and behaviors; and iterate — modify the approach based on measured effects. This is the operational template for SCI's governance cycle.

The cycle's structure reflects two foundational commitments that the JIC shares with the other theoretical traditions: audience primacy and adaptive learning.

Audience primacy: the cycle begins with audience understanding, not message development. The appropriate signal is discovered by attending carefully to who the audience is, what they believe, what they value, and what their current posture is toward the organization and its objectives. This is the JIC's restatement of the classical rhetorical principle that the audience is the arbiter of meaning, now applied to organizational scale and formalized as a process requirement.

Adaptive learning: the cycle does not end with signal deployment. It continues through effect monitoring and modification. The governance function must maintain continuous situational awareness of whether the signals it is producing are achieving the posture effects they are designed to produce — and must update its approach when they are not. This adaptive requirement is the process-level expression of what RDT implies structurally: the stakeholder environment is dynamic, dependence structures shift, and governance must be continuously recalibrated.

### The Governance Gap as Evidence

The JIC's own history is theoretically significant. Having identified the requirement for strategic communications governance with rigor and precision, the DoD was unable to operationalize the function it described. The concept's authors recognized that implementation would require cultural change, structural reorganization, and new institutional mechanisms. In practice, these requirements proved too great to satisfy through policy guidance and coordination agreements among existing functions with their own chains of command.

This is not a failure of the concept. It is evidence for a specific theoretical claim: the governance function cannot be discharged through coordination of existing structures. It requires purpose-built institutional architecture — defined ownership, intelligence functions, accountability mechanisms, and a recurring cycle that forces alignment rather than merely recommending it. The JIC showed what the function requires; the DoD's experience showed that the function cannot be improvised from existing structures. Both conclusions are theoretically essential to SCI's design justification.

## IV. Where the Traditions Converge

Three intellectual traditions, arriving through different analytical paths, reach the same conclusion: influence at scale cannot be managed through individual communicative competence or informal coordination. It requires institutional architecture.

Rhetoric arrives at this conclusion through the organizational translation of ethos. Individual communicators can maintain their personal credibility through skilled and honest communication. But institutional ethos — the organizational credibility on which persuasion depends — is a property of the aggregate signal produced by all organizational actors together. No individual can control it; no function can manage it without governance authority over the full organizational signal environment. Rhetoric does not merely *prefer* governance; it *requires* it as a precondition for sustained institutional persuasive capacity.

RDT arrives at the same conclusion through the logic of legitimacy as a strategic resource. Legitimacy is too consequential — too determinative of strategic capacity — to be managed as a secondary function of organizational communications. It requires the same institutional discipline as financial and operational resources: strategic prioritization, continuous monitoring, accountable ownership, and adaptive response to changing conditions. A legitimacy resource managed without these disciplines will erode, as the empirical record confirms it does.

The JIC arrives at the conclusion through the integration imperative. When every organizational action is a signal, and when those signals collectively determine stakeholder posture, the coordination challenge exceeds what any single function can manage through internal expertise. The adaptive cycle the JIC specifies — audience intelligence, signal coordination, effect monitoring, iteration — is too demanding, too continuous, and too organizationally distributed to be discharged without purpose-built architecture.

The convergence of these three traditions on a shared conclusion about governance necessity is not coincidental. It reflects the structural reality that the problem — distributed organizational actors, shared external audiences, no institutional mechanism for coherence — has the same essential character regardless of which theoretical lens is applied to it. The governance requirement is not an artifact of any particular analytical tradition. It is a feature of the problem itself.

## V. The Synthesis: SCI's Original Theoretical Contributions

The three traditions establish the necessity of governance and the requirements a governance architecture must meet. But they do not by themselves produce the SCI model. The synthesis of these traditions generates three constructs that none of them supplies independently — constructs that are SCI's original theoretical contributions and the foundations on which Part III's model specification rests.

### The Posture Construct

Neither RDT, classical rhetoric, nor the JIC provides a precise, operational definition of what the governance function is managing. RDT measures dependence structure and resource flows. Rhetoric assesses audience characteristics and receptivity. The JIC tracks perceptions, attitudes, and behaviors. Each captures a dimension of the problem; none provides the integrated unit of measurement and management that a governance architecture requires.

The posture construct is that unit. Stakeholder posture is defined as: *the integrated disposition of a stakeholder toward taking or withholding a specific action on which an organizational objective depends, assessed against observable behavioral evidence and informed by the stakeholder's structural position, attitudinal orientation, and enabling conditions.*

**The Principle of Compatibility and Behavioral Specificity**

The construct's insistence on action-specific posture assessment — rather than a general disposition score for the stakeholder relationship — is not an arbitrary design preference. It is the institutional application of what the attitude-behavior literature calls the Principle of Compatibility.

Icek Ajzen and Martin Fishbein's foundational research on attitude-behavior relationships demonstrated that attitude measures predict behavior reliably only when the attitude is assessed at the same level of specificity as the behavior being predicted. The TACT framework specifies the four dimensions of correspondence that must align: the Target of the attitude, the Action it involves, the Context in which it occurs, and the Time at which it is measured. When attitude and behavior are assessed at matched specificity across all four dimensions, attitude-behavior correlations rise dramatically — from the notoriously weak figures characteristic of general attitude measures to robust predictive relationships. When they are mismatched, the prediction fails.

For the governance function, the implication is direct and consequential: a posture assessment that measures a stakeholder's general disposition toward the organization is not a predictor of whether they will take a specific required action. A regulatory agency head who is broadly favorable toward an organization may still deny a specific permit application; a community group leader who is adversarially disposed toward an organization in general may nonetheless refrain from filing regulatory opposition on a specific project if their concerns on that issue have been addressed. General disposition does not predict specific behavior. The governance function cannot afford the measurement error.

The TACT principle therefore mandates that every posture assessment in the SCI architecture be calibrated to a specific Action — the behavior the stakeholder must perform — rather than to the stakeholder relationship in the abstract. This is the theoretical derivation of the behavioral specificity design principle that the Part III architecture is built around: it is not a feature chosen for elegance but a requirement imposed by the best available science of attitude-behavior prediction.

This definition synthesizes contributions from all three traditions and from the attitude-behavior literature:

- The *structural position* dimension comes from RDT: the stakeholder's power over the organization, the dependence relationship, the nature of the resources they control.
- The *attitudinal orientation* dimension comes from classical rhetoric and the attitude-behavior literature: the stakeholder's evaluation of the organization, the depth of their engagement with the relevant arguments, the strength and stability of their disposition.
- The *enabling conditions* dimension comes from the Theory of Planned Behavior: whether the stakeholder has the capacity, resources, and autonomy to act on their disposition.
- The *specific action* requirement comes from the Principle of Compatibility: posture must be assessed against the behavior the organization requires, not against the stakeholder's general sentiment.

Posture is not a measurement of the past. It is a prediction: given this stakeholder's current structural position, attitudinal orientation, and enabling conditions, what is the probability that they will take or withhold the action that objective achievement requires? This predictive character is what makes the posture construct strategically useful rather than merely descriptive.

### The Evidence Architecture

Classical rhetoric requires logos: valid argument from true premises. RDT requires empirical assessment: dependence structures must be observed, not assumed. The JIC requires intelligence: audience understanding must be grounded in continuous information gathering and analysis, not in inference or supposition.

These three requirements converge on a single design principle: the governance mechanism's outputs — posture assessments, stakeholder intelligence, communication frameworks — must be traceable to verifiable evidence. Without this traceability, posture assessments are opinion. Communication frameworks built on unverified premises are sophistry. And the adaptive cycle cannot function because there is no reliable basis for assessing whether signals are producing the effects they are designed to produce.

The evidence architecture is the institutional operationalization of this principle. It comprises: continuous ingestion of observable signals from the stakeholder environment; AI-assisted extraction of relevant information and citation; human validation by the record owners closest to each stakeholder relationship; audit-trail traceability from posture assessments back to their evidentiary sources; and periodic reassessment as new evidence accumulates.

This is not a technology feature. It is a governance requirement derived from the theoretical foundations. An organization that builds strategic communications governance without an evidence architecture has built a coordination mechanism for unverified claims — which is precisely the institutional form of the sophistry that the rhetorical tradition warns against.

### The Governance Cycle

The JIC's four-step adaptive cycle specifies the process architecture that strategic communications governance requires. But the JIC's cycle was designed for military operations, and its translation into the commercial organizational context requires adaptation that the JIC itself does not provide.

The governance cycle SCI derives from the theoretical synthesis has the same essential structure — audience intelligence, signal coordination, effect monitoring, iteration — but its institutional form reflects the specific characteristics of the commercial organization:

- **The intelligence record structure** replaces the JIC's target audience analysis with a formal, evidence-grounded record for each consequential stakeholder relationship, owned by the organizational actor closest to that relationship and updated on a defined cadence.

- **The distributed ownership model** replaces the JIC's centralized intelligence function with accountability distributed across the functional roles whose actions constitute the organizational signal — embedding governance discipline in the functions that produce the signals rather than concentrating it in a function that manages messages after the fact.

- **The alignment cycle** replaces the JIC's operational planning review with a recurring institutional mechanism — the Strategic Communications Brief — that forces regular posture reassessment, signal coherence review, and accountability reporting across all consequential stakeholder relationships.

- **The posture construct** replaces the JIC's perceptions-attitudes-behaviors framework with the precise, behavior-specific, evidence-grounded assessment unit that the Principle of Compatibility and the attitude-behavior literature establish as the required measurement standard.

The governance cycle is not a process designed for convenience. It is the institutional mechanism that makes the theoretical requirements of all three traditions operationally satisfiable at organizational scale.

## VI. The Organizational Audience: A Philosophical Taxonomy

Classical rhetoric analyzed individual speakers addressing audiences that were, if plural, at least temporally unified — the assembly considering a single question, the court hearing a single case. Organizations face a fundamentally different audience structure: multiple simultaneous audiences, each with distinct institutional positions, distinct modes of exercising influence, and distinct claims on organizational conduct. No classical taxonomy of audiences addresses this multiplicity at the structural level. What is required is a philosophical account of what differentiates organizational audiences from one another — not by their specific interests or their relationship to the organization, but by the institutional logic that governs how they exercise influence and what kind of engagement is appropriate.

*Institutional logic* — the rule system through which actors in a given domain exercise power, make judgments, and engage with organizations — is the differentiating criterion. Four institutional logics structure the organizational audience environment.

**The Government Domain: Legal Authority and Democratic Accountability**

Government domain actors operate within the institutional logic of legal authority and democratic accountability: legislators, regulators, executive agencies, courts, and the governmental processes through which law is made and enforced. Their characteristic action is the exercise of legal power — votes, approvals, rulings, regulations, investigations — and their institutional accountability is to the public interest as defined through democratic processes. Organizational rhetoric in this domain operates within formal institutional procedures and must demonstrate that organizational interests are consistent with the public interest those procedures are designed to protect. Phronesis — the demonstration of competence and sound judgment — predominates in this domain, because governmental actors are accountable for the technical correctness of their decisions and extend credibility to organizations that demonstrate they understand the substance of the issues at stake.

**The Information Domain: Editorial and Epistemic Judgment**

Information domain actors operate within the institutional logic of editorial and epistemic judgment: journalists, financial and policy analysts, academic researchers in their public communication roles, and the platforms through which information is prioritized and distributed. Their characteristic action is the exercise of editorial power — coverage, framing, amplification, citation, silence — and their institutional authority rests on their credibility as evaluators of truth and significance. Organizational rhetoric in this domain must recognize that these actors are not audiences for the organization's message but independent evaluators of its claims. Their institutional logic is not persuadability but verification: they assess whether claims are true, significant, and consistent with observable evidence. This places special demands on the evidence architecture: claims that cannot survive editorial scrutiny will be corrected or ignored, and the accumulated effect on institutional ethos in the information domain is severe and lasting.

**The Civil Society Domain: Normative Authority and Mobilization Capacity**

Civil society domain actors operate within the institutional logic of normative authority and mobilization capacity: advocacy organizations, community groups, labor unions, religious institutions, professional associations, and other organized collective actors whose power derives from their credibility as representatives of shared values and their capacity to organize collective action. Their characteristic action is the exercise of normative power — endorsement, opposition, organized protest, regulatory intervention — and their authority rests on the perceived alignment between their positions and the values of the communities they represent. Organizational rhetoric in the Civil Society domain operates in the register of values and conduct rather than evidence and procedure. These actors evaluate organizations not primarily on the quality of their arguments but on the consonance of their observable conduct with the commitments those organizations have made. Arete — moral virtue, honest and principled conduct — is the predominant ethos mode in this domain, because civil society actors extend their credibility only to organizations whose conduct sustains the values those actors are accountable to their own communities for protecting.

**The Market Domain: Economic Performance and Value**

Market domain actors operate within the institutional logic of economic performance and value exchange: investors, financial analysts, rating agencies, and strategic commercial partners whose relationship with the organization is mediated through financial markets and economic transactions. Their characteristic action is the exercise of economic power — investment, divestment, ratings, market access — and their institutional accountability is to the economic performance of the organizations and portfolios they assess. Organizational rhetoric in the Market domain operates within the rigorous evidentiary standards of financial analysis. The market domain is the context in which the say-do gap is most precisely quantifiable — the gap between stated strategic direction and demonstrated financial performance is measured in observable market data — which makes institutional integrity simultaneously most visible and most consequential here.

**The Philosophical Basis of the Taxonomy**

The four-domain taxonomy is a philosophical derivation, not an empirical classification. It is derived from the theory of institutional logics: the claim that what differentiates organizational audiences at the structural level is the institutional rule system that governs how they exercise influence, not their specific interests or their observable relationship to the organization. This derivation makes the taxonomy sector-agnostic: because institutional logics are structural features of democratic market societies rather than features of specific industries, the same four domains structure the organizational audience environment across all sectors. The specific actors who populate each domain differ by sector; the institutional logic that governs their mode of influence does not.

The institutional logics tradition provides external scholarly grounding for this derivation. Friedland and Alford (1991) first identified capitalism, democracy, and other logics as distinct institutional orders with their own organizing principles, each governing the behavior of actors within its domain in ways that cannot be reduced to any of the others. Thornton, Ocasio, and Lounsbury (2012) systematized this framework, identifying capitalism (Market), the state (Government), professions (knowledge and epistemic authority, approximating the Information domain), and community (Civil Society) as structurally distinct institutional orders — a taxonomy that maps closely to SCI's four domains. Their core claim — that actors in different institutional orders exercise influence through the rule system of their order, not through a universal logic of interest maximization — is precisely the theoretical basis on which SCI's domain distinctions rest. The four domains are not management categories of convenience; they are the organizational expression of distinct institutional logics whose structural difference has been independently established in the sociological literature.

The taxonomy also resolves a question that stakeholder management frameworks typically evade: what is "the public"? In the organizational audience environment, "the public" as an undifferentiated aggregate has no characteristic institutional action, no evidentiary standard for organizational rhetoric to meet, and no mode of exercising influence structurally distinct from the four domains. Public opinion becomes organizationally relevant only when channeled through one of the four institutional logics: through voting and regulatory comment (Government), through media coverage and information distribution (Information), through organized collective action (Civil Society), or through market behavior at scales where aggregate action creates organizational stakes (Market). The public is not an audience the SCI governance framework directly addresses; it is a force that operates through and shapes the organizational audiences the framework is designed to manage.

## Conclusion: From Theory to Architecture

The theoretical foundations established in this section generate specific requirements for the SCI governance architecture:

From classical rhetoric: the governance mechanism must maintain the institutional ethos on which persuasive capacity depends; it must ensure that all communications are grounded in valid argument and verified evidence; it must maintain continuous audience intelligence sufficient to support kairotic judgment across the stakeholder portfolio.

From Resource Dependence Theory: the governance mechanism must treat legitimacy as a strategic resource requiring the same institutional discipline as financial and operational resources; it must maintain a continuously updated assessment of the dependence structure that determines which stakeholders are most consequential; it must support the tactical differentiation — buffering, bridging, cooptation — that different dependence conditions require.

From the JIC: the governance mechanism must address the full organizational signal environment, not just formal communications; it must instantiate an adaptive cycle of audience intelligence, signal coordination, effect monitoring, and iteration; and it must be embedded in institutional architecture with defined ownership and accountability, because the integration challenge cannot be met through coordination of existing structures.

From the organizational audience taxonomy (Section VI): the governance architecture must be organized around a sector-agnostic taxonomy of audience types differentiated by the institutional logic governing each domain's exercise of influence — because the nature of appropriate engagement, the dominant ethos mode (phronesis in the Government domain, arete in Civil Society, evidential rigor in Information, quantitative precision in Market), and the rhetorical register appropriate to each domain differ systematically. The four-domain structure that Part III's model takes for granted is not an arbitrary design choice; it is a requirement derived from the theory of institutional logics and the recognition that organizational audiences are differentiated by the rule systems through which they exercise power, not merely by their interests or their relationship to the organization.

These requirements, taken together, specify the SCI model. Part III develops that specification: the intelligence record structure, the evidence lifecycle, the distributed ownership model, the alignment cycle, and the posture construct as the central unit of governance. Each design choice in Part III is a derivation from the theoretical foundations established here.

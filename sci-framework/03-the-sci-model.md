# Part III: The SCI Model

*SCI Intellectual Framework — Document 03*

---

## Introduction: From Theory to Architecture

Part II established three theoretical requirements for the governance architecture:

From classical rhetoric: the mechanism must maintain institutional ethos through signal coherence; it must ground all communications in valid, evidence-backed argument; and it must maintain continuous audience intelligence sufficient to support kairotic judgment across the stakeholder portfolio.

From Resource Dependence Theory: the mechanism must treat legitimacy as a strategic resource requiring institutional discipline equivalent to financial and operational governance; it must maintain a continuously updated assessment of the dependence structure; and it must support tactical differentiation across the stakeholder portfolio as conditions of dependence vary.

From the JIC: the mechanism must address the full organizational signal environment, not just formal communications; it must instantiate an adaptive cycle of audience intelligence, signal coordination, effect monitoring, and iteration; and it must be embedded in purpose-built institutional architecture with defined ownership and accountability, because the integration challenge cannot be met through coordination of existing structures.

These requirements are not satisfied by any existing tool category. They are satisfied by the SCI governance architecture. This document specifies that architecture: its structural elements, their theoretical derivations, their relationships to each other, and the mechanism by which they operate as an integrated system. The final section illustrates the complete architecture through a worked example.

---

## I. Design Principles: The Translation from Theory to Architecture

Before specifying the architecture's elements, it is necessary to state the design principles from which those elements are derived. Each principle is a direct translation of a theoretical requirement into a design commitment.

**Principle 1: Stakeholder-centricity.** The architecture is organized around what external stakeholders must do, not around what the organization wants to say. Every structural element — from the Priority record that establishes strategic stakes to the Action record that specifies required stakeholder behavior — is defined in terms of its relationship to external actors whose decisions determine outcomes. This principle derives from classical rhetoric's audience-primacy commitment, RDT's resource-dependence logic, and the JIC's insistence on beginning with audience understanding rather than message development.

**Principle 2: Behavioral specificity.** Posture is assessed against specific required behaviors, not against general stakeholder sentiment. The Principle of Compatibility — the requirement that attitude measurement correspond in specificity to the behavioral criterion — demands that every intelligence record specify what action the stakeholder must take or withhold, and that posture be evaluated against that specific behavioral target. This principle prevents the governance function from measuring the wrong thing and acting on measurements that do not predict the outcomes it is trying to produce.

**Principle 3: Evidence accountability.** Every intelligence assessment is traceable to validated, verifiable source citations. Posture assessments that cannot be traced to evidence are not governance outputs; they are institutionalized opinion. The evidence principle derives from rhetoric's logos requirement, RDT's empirical orientation, and the JIC's intelligence standard. It is the mechanism that maintains the epistemic credibility of governance outputs and prevents the architecture from becoming a coordination mechanism for unverified claims.

**Principle 4: Distributed ownership.** Accountability for intelligence record accuracy and currency belongs to the organizational actors closest to each stakeholder relationship, not to a central function that manages messages downstream. This principle derives from the JIC's integration imperative: because all organizational actions constitute signals, governance must be embedded in the functions that produce those signals. It derives equally from the DoD's own experience: governance concentrated in a central function reproduces the silo it is designed to eliminate.

**Principle 5: Adaptive cycle.** The governance function is a continuous process, not a periodic project. Intelligence records are maintained on a defined cadence; posture assessments are updated as new evidence accumulates; signal coherence is reviewed at regular intervals; and accountability for intelligence quality is enforced through a recurring institutional mechanism. This principle derives from the JIC's adaptive loop and from the attitude-behavior literature's finding that attitude stability — the temporal consistency of posture — is a critical determinant of behavioral predictiveness.

**Principle 6: Cognitive viability.** The governance architecture must be operable by distributed actors for whom strategic communications governance is an additional duty, not a primary one. The JIC's failure to sustain distributed ownership is attributable in part to the cognitive burden that comprehensive intelligence analysis imposes on actors already fully deployed on primary responsibilities. The architecture must therefore embed support mechanisms — AI-assisted analysis, automated stakeholder monitoring, structured record templates — that reduce the analytical burden on distributed record owners sufficiently to sustain participation and accountability over time.

## II. The Intelligence Record Architecture

The SCI model organizes strategic communications governance through six intelligence record types. These records are not independent data structures; they form a connected architecture in which each record type serves a specific governance function and in which the relationships between records constitute the governance logic of the system.

### Priority

The Priority record is the apex of the intelligence architecture. It establishes an enduring domain of sustained organizational commitment — a strategic direction in which the organization is persistently investing — and identifies the stakeholder domains and organizational functions whose engagement is required to advance it. A Priority is not time-bounded and is not defined by a single achievable outcome; it may encompass multiple Objectives across successive planning cycles, each representing a discrete step in its advancement. In some cases, a Priority is explicitly aspirational — a direction of travel rather than a destination.

This distinction between Priority and Objective is architecturally significant. A Priority like "expand manufacturing capability" does not expire when a specific facility expansion is approved. It persists through subsequent capacity decisions, future expansions, and adjacent investments. Each Objective under that Priority is bounded and measurable; the Priority itself is not. This means the governance architecture can absorb changes in tactical approach — an Objective that fails can be replaced or revised — without the strategic warrant for the entire governance portfolio being put in question.

The Priority record is the governance anchor that gives all downstream records their strategic justification. An Objective that cannot be traced to a Priority has no strategic warrant. A Stakeholder record whose subject does not appear in any Priority's stakeholder domain may not belong in the governance portfolio. The Priority record is therefore not merely a header; it is the instrument through which organizational strategy becomes legible to the governance function.

### Objective and Requirements

The Objective record specifies what must be achieved to advance a Priority. Each Objective record contains a set of **Requirements** — sub-entities that define the specific conditions that must be satisfied for the Objective to be considered complete. An Objective may have multiple Requirements, and an Objective is not satisfied until all of its Requirements are met.

Requirements are the structural link between the Objective and the Actions that must occur. They transform a broad objective — "secure regulatory approval for Project X" — into a set of specific, assessable conditions: technical review completed favorably; public comment period navigated without material opposition; commission vote secured. This decomposition serves two governance functions simultaneously. First, it makes the Objective tractable: rather than assessing progress toward a binary outcome, the governance function can track which Requirements are satisfied, which are pending, and which are at risk. Second, it creates the precise behavioral specification that stakeholder posture assessment requires. The TACT principle demands that posture be evaluated against a specific behavioral criterion; Requirements provide that criterion at the level of specificity the principle demands.

### Action

The Action record specifies what a Stakeholder must do to satisfy an Objective Requirement. This distinction is critical and must be stated precisely: Action records are stakeholder-facing, not organization-facing. They describe the behavior the external actor must perform — the vote that must be cast, the approval that must be granted, the public statement that must be made, the partnership that must be extended — not the activity the organization will conduct.

Each Action record is linked to an Objective via a specific Requirement. The Action is the behavioral specification of the Requirement: if the Requirement is "commission vote secured," the Action is "[Commissioner name/role] votes in favor of Project X at the [date] commission meeting." This specificity is not bureaucratic excess. It is the application of the Principle of Compatibility at the operational level: the governance function can only assess whether an Action has occurred if the Action is defined with sufficient precision to be observable.

An Objective may have multiple Actions associated with a single Requirement, reflecting the reality that a given condition may require the cooperation of multiple stakeholders. The architecture supports this many-to-one relationship between Actions and Requirements.

### Stakeholder

The Stakeholder record is the intelligence core of the architecture. It captures the comprehensive intelligence picture required to assess posture, design effective messaging, and plan productive engagement for a specific external actor whose behavior is consequential to one or more Actions, and through those Actions, to Objectives and Priorities.

The Stakeholder record operationalizes the posture construct defined in Part II. Its content encompasses:

- **Structural position:** the stakeholder's power over the organization, the nature of the dependence relationship, the resources or decisions they control, and their position in relevant institutional networks (RDT's dependence assessment)
- **Attitudinal orientation:** the stakeholder's current disposition toward the organization and toward the specific Actions they are being asked to take, assessed against observable behavioral evidence rather than inferred from general sentiment
- **Posture strength:** the depth of the stakeholder's engagement with the relevant arguments — whether their current posture is grounded in active cognitive processing (central-route, durable) or superficial exposure (peripheral-route, fragile) (ELM)
- **Enabling conditions:** whether the stakeholder has the capacity, resources, and autonomy to take the Actions the governance function requires of them (TPB/RAA)
- **Behavioral history:** the record of the stakeholder's prior actions and engagements with the organization, providing the evidential basis for posture assessment and the context for current engagement
- **Intelligence citations:** the validated, traceable source citations that ground every posture assessment in observable evidence

Posture in the Stakeholder record is assessed against specific Actions — the behaviors the stakeholder must perform — not against a general disposition score. A Stakeholder may have a positive posture toward the organization generally but a cautious posture toward a specific required Action; the governance function must capture this distinction to avoid the measurement error the Principle of Compatibility identifies.

### Messaging Framework

The Messaging Framework record translates the intelligence captured in Stakeholder and Action records into structured, deployable communications. It is the operationalization of classical rhetoric's logos requirement: the claim, the evidence, and the reasoning that connects them, designed for the specific audience at the specific stage of the engagement.

The foundational intelligence construct feeding the Messaging Framework is the **Gap** — a sub-entity of the Stakeholder record, classified as either an *information gap* (something the Stakeholder does not know that, if known, would move their posture toward the required Action) or a *messaging opportunity* (a values-based, emotional, or motivational lever that, if engaged, would advance the required Action). Gaps may be organization-level — relevant to the stakeholder relationship broadly — or action-specific, linked to a particular Action the Stakeholder must take. Gaps are identified and managed by Subject Matter Expert record owners within the Stakeholder and Action entry forms; they are the mechanism through which the intelligence record directly seeds the communications function.

The Messaging Framework constructs its output from Gaps through a **three-tier point hierarchy**:

1. **Information Points** address information gaps — providing the factual content, evidence, and argument required to close what the Stakeholder does not know. Each Information Point must link to a specific Gap.
2. **Messaging Points** address messaging opportunities — developing the values-based, emotional, or motivational arguments that engage the Stakeholder's disposition levers. Each Messaging Point must also link to a specific Gap.
3. **Talking Points** are the synthesized, deployable communications artifact — drawn from combinations of Information and Messaging Points — that practitioners use in direct stakeholder engagement. Talking Points are authored from the Information and Messaging Point layer, not independently of it.

Messaging Frameworks may be linked to a **Stakeholder record** — when the communication approach is tailored to a specific actor whose values, interests, and interpretive framework shape how arguments must be constructed — or to an **Action record** — when the required behavior is shared across multiple stakeholders and a common argumentative approach can be designed around the action itself. Both linking schemas are valid; the appropriate choice depends on whether the communication challenge is primarily audience-specific or action-specific. A Stakeholder with multiple assigned Actions may have both a Stakeholder-level Messaging Framework for organization-wide communication and separate Action-level Messaging Frameworks for the specific behavioral contexts each Action requires.

The development of a Messaging Framework uses a **hybrid ownership model** that distributes authorship according to domain expertise rather than assigning the record to a single owner. The **Communications Lead** user type holds Primary Ownership of each Messaging Framework record and has exclusive write access to the Talking Points tier, where Information and Messaging Points are synthesized into deployable output. The **Priority Lead** and **Relationship Manager** user types hold Delegate Ownership and are responsible for contributing Information Points and Messaging Points derived from the Gaps they manage. This is the same group of Subject Matter Expert record owners who identified and maintain those Gaps: they carry the gap intelligence forward into the first layer of communicative response.

This division of authorship is architecturally significant. SMEs who manage stakeholder relationships and own strategic Objectives are best positioned to develop the substantive building blocks — what the Stakeholder needs to know, what values resonate with them — because that knowledge lives in the intelligence they maintain. The Communications Lead is best positioned to synthesize those raw building blocks into coherent, consistent, deployable Talking Points that maintain signal coherence across the portfolio. The hybrid ownership model enforces this division structurally: neither domain can unilaterally control the full output. The Communications Lead cannot bypass the SME intelligence layer, because the Information and Messaging Points they are synthesizing from were authored by the people closest to the stakeholder relationship. The SMEs cannot directly control the deployable communications output, because Talking Points authorship is reserved for the function responsible for cross-portfolio coherence.

AI Assist supports the Delegate Owners in developing Information and Messaging Points by generating structured draft candidates from the Stakeholder record's intelligence picture and the linked Gaps — reducing the initial analytical burden from open-ended synthesis to review and refinement.

### Activity

The Activity record captures observable events in the engagement — things that happen between the organization and its stakeholder environment that advance, impede, or provide evidence about progress toward Objective Requirements. Activities are **actor-neutral**: they may be conducted by the organization (a briefing delivered, a submission filed, a meeting convened) or by Stakeholders (an approval granted, a public statement made, a vote cast, a meeting attended).

Activity records may be linked to an **Objective record** — when the event is relevant to tracking progress toward a specific Requirement — or to a **Stakeholder record** — when the event is relevant to the intelligence picture of a specific actor's posture and behavior. Both linking schemas are valid; a single Activity may be linked to both, reflecting its dual relevance to objective tracking and stakeholder intelligence.

The actor-neutral design of the Activity record is theoretically important. It means the architecture captures not only what the organization does in pursuit of its objectives, but what stakeholders do in response — or independent of — those efforts. Stakeholder Activity is the observable evidence on which posture assessment depends; without systematic capture of stakeholder behavior, posture assessments are inference rather than intelligence.

**Discovery Intelligence** is the mechanism that makes Stakeholder Activity capture viable at scale without imposing continuous manual monitoring on distributed record owners. Discovery Intelligence monitors external information sources — regulatory filings, public records, news, legislative calendars, social platforms, and other domain-specific APIs — for signals that a Stakeholder has conducted an Activity that impacts an Objective. When such a signal is identified, Discovery Intelligence generates a proposed Activity record and routes it to the owner of the relevant Stakeholder record for validation. The record owner confirms, modifies, or rejects the proposed Activity; accepted Activities are incorporated into the stakeholder's intelligence picture and linked to the relevant Objective or Stakeholder records.

This design converts the most burdensome aspect of distributed ownership — continuous environmental monitoring — from an active responsibility into a validation responsibility. The cognitive difference is substantial: validation of a proposed record presented with source citations requires a fraction of the analytical effort required to identify, assess, and document a stakeholder signal from scratch.

## III. The Posture Construct as Operational Instrument

The posture construct, defined theoretically in Part II, is operationalized in the Stakeholder record. Its five theoretical dimensions become the observable, assessable components of the intelligence record:

**Structural position assessment** answers: what power does this stakeholder have over the outcomes the organization is pursuing, and why? What resources, decisions, or institutional positions give them that power? This assessment is derived from RDT's dependence analysis and from observable evidence — institutional roles, documented decision authority, historical exercise of influence.

**Attitudinal orientation assessment** answers: what is this stakeholder's current evaluation of the Actions they are being asked to take? Is their disposition favorable, cautious, neutral, resistant, or hostile? This assessment is grounded in behavioral evidence — what the stakeholder has said, written, voted for, opposed, or funded — not in inferred sentiment from general relationship quality.

**Posture strength assessment** answers: how durable is the stakeholder's current posture? Is it grounded in active engagement with the relevant arguments (central-route, ELM), or in superficial cues such as source credibility and peer behavior (peripheral-route)? A stakeholder with a favorable but peripheral-route posture is strategically unreliable; their posture will shift under adversarial pressure. The governance function must assess strength as well as direction to accurately predict behavioral reliability.

**Enabling conditions assessment** answers: does this stakeholder have the capacity, resources, and autonomy to take the Actions required? A stakeholder who is favorably disposed but institutionally constrained — whose governing board, political leadership, or regulatory environment prevents them from acting on their disposition — represents a different governance challenge than one who is favorably disposed and unconstrained. The Theory of Planned Behavior's perceived behavioral control dimension and its separation of capacity from autonomy provide the framework for this assessment.

**Behavioral specificity** is the requirement that ties all four assessments together: every posture assessment is evaluated against the specific Action (or Actions) the stakeholder must take. A stakeholder's posture toward the organization in general is not the relevant measurement. Their posture toward the required behavior — this vote, this approval, this public endorsement, this continued partnership — is what the governance function needs to predict and manage.

## IV. The Evidence Lifecycle

The evidence lifecycle is the process through which raw information from the stakeholder environment is converted into validated, traceable intelligence that supports defensible governance decisions. It has four stages.

**Continuous ingestion** is the collection of observable signals from the stakeholder environment: news coverage, public statements, regulatory filings, legislative records, social platform activity, direct engagement reports, and Discovery Intelligence outputs. Ingestion is continuous because stakeholder posture is dynamic — it changes in response to organizational signals, environmental events, and the actions of other actors — and because governance decisions made on stale intelligence are governance decisions made on fiction.

**AI-assisted extraction and citation** applies AI capabilities to the ingested signal stream to identify what is relevant, extract the substantive content, and generate proposed citations for record owners to evaluate. This stage converts raw information volume — which in complex stakeholder environments is too large for manual processing — into structured, assessable evidence proposals. AI-assisted extraction does not replace human judgment; it prepares the evidence base on which human judgment can be exercised efficiently.

**Human validation** is the record owner's review, confirmation, modification, or rejection of AI-extracted evidence and proposed record updates. Validation is the step that maintains the epistemological integrity of the intelligence record: the record owner — the person closest to the stakeholder relationship, with contextual knowledge that AI extraction cannot supply — confirms that the evidence is accurate, relevant, and appropriately characterized before it becomes part of the posture assessment.

Human validation is the step that increases cognitive load on distributed record owners, as distinct from AI Assist (which reduces it). The evidence lifecycle deliberately imposes this load because the alternative — automated evidence incorporation without human review — would convert the governance architecture into a mechanism for unvalidated intelligence. The governance claim that posture assessments are evidence-grounded depends entirely on human validation occurring at this step.

**Audit-trail traceability** is the structural requirement that every posture assessment, every Messaging Framework argument, and every governance recommendation be traceable back through the validation record to the original source citations. Traceability is not primarily a compliance mechanism. It is the architectural guarantee that the governance function's outputs are evidence-grounded rather than opinion-based — that when a record owner or executive reviews a posture assessment, they can follow the evidential chain from conclusion to source and evaluate the quality of the inference for themselves.

## V. The Distributed Ownership Model

Distributed ownership is the design principle that resolves the structural contradiction the JIC's failure exposed: the integration imperative requires governance to span all organizational functions that produce signals, but concentrating governance in a single function reproduces the silo it is designed to eliminate.

The distributed ownership model assigns accountability for each intelligence record to the organizational actor best positioned to maintain it: typically, the person whose functional role gives them the closest relationship with the relevant stakeholder, the deepest domain knowledge of the relevant environment, and the most direct access to the observable evidence that grounds the posture assessment. Government affairs staff own Government domain Stakeholder records. Investor relations staff own Market domain records for financial stakeholders. Communications staff may own Information domain records for media actors. Community relations staff own Civil Society domain records for local stakeholder groups.

This distribution means that the intelligence picture maintained by the governance architecture is constructed and validated by the people who know most about each part of the stakeholder landscape — not by a central function interpreting reports from the field. It is the mechanism through which the JIC's integration imperative is satisfied without requiring a new organizational function with authority over all signal-producing activities.

**The operability problem and its solution.** Distributed ownership is the theoretically correct architecture, and it is the architecture that failed in the DoD context. The failure was not architectural; it was operational. Maintaining rigorous intelligence records — assessing posture, developing messaging frameworks, documenting evidence, monitoring stakeholder activities — is analytically demanding work. When this work is assigned to actors for whom it represents an additional duty rather than a primary responsibility, participation rates and record quality degrade unless the cognitive burden is actively managed.

The DoD's implementation relied on word processing documents and spreadsheet tracking. These tools provided no analytical support: every intelligence record required the record owner to perform the full analytical work independently — researching the stakeholder, assessing posture, developing messaging, monitoring for activity signals — without assistance. The cognitive burden was prohibitive.

SCI's architecture addresses this through two AI capabilities designed specifically to reduce record-owner cognitive load:

**AI Assist** supports the development of Messaging Frameworks — the most analytically demanding record type — by generating structured draft arguments based on the Stakeholder record's intelligence picture and the required Actions. The record owner reviews, modifies, and validates the draft; they do not construct it from a blank page. This reduces the cognitive demand of Messaging Framework development from synthesis to evaluation — a substantially lower burden that sustains participation without sacrificing analytical quality.

**SCI Assistant** provides contextual guidance for record owners navigating the intelligence architecture: explaining record structures, surfacing relevant intelligence from across the record system, identifying where posture assessments may be inconsistent or evidence may be stale. SCI Assistant reduces the orientation and navigation burden that makes unfamiliar governance processes feel prohibitive, particularly for record owners who engage with the system infrequently.

Together with Discovery Intelligence's conversion of activity monitoring from active surveillance to validation, these capabilities reduce the net cognitive burden of distributed ownership sufficiently to sustain participation among additional-duty record owners. They do not eliminate the burden — the evidence lifecycle's human validation requirement ensures that — but they manage it to a level consistent with sustained organizational commitment. This is the architectural solution to the problem the DoD's experience identified.

## VI. The Alignment Cycle

The intelligence record architecture provides the structure for governance. The alignment cycle provides the process. Without a recurring mechanism that forces review, accountability, and adaptation, the record architecture becomes a static repository rather than a dynamic governance function.

The **Strategic Communications Brief** is the institutional mechanism through which the alignment cycle operates. Convened on a regular cadence — typically weekly — the Brief brings together the record owners across the organization's strategic communications portfolio to perform three governance functions simultaneously.

**Posture reassessment:** Are stakeholder postures moving toward or away from the Actions required? For each Priority, the Brief reviews the current posture across relevant Stakeholders, compares it against the posture target, and identifies where the gap is widening, stable, or closing. Postures that have shifted — positively or negatively — trigger review of the evidence supporting the assessment and, where the shift is adverse, review of whether the current engagement approach requires adaptation. This is the JIC's "monitor and measure effects" step, institutionalized as a recurring accountability requirement.

**Signal coherence review:** Are all organizational signals — communications, engagements, operational decisions — consistent with the posture management requirements across the portfolio? The Brief is the mechanism through which cross-domain coherence is assessed: are the arguments being made to the regulatory agency consistent with what is being communicated in the community engagement process? Is the investor relations narrative consistent with the labor relations posture? Are operational decisions — announced or anticipated — creating signal contradictions that will reach shared stakeholders simultaneously? This review is the governance function that prevents the say-do gap from opening silently across distributed functions — the structural failure that Part I identifies as endemic and that the decoupling mechanism produces predictably without architectural intervention.

**Accountability reporting:** Are record owners maintaining intelligence currency and evidence discipline? The Brief reviews the freshness of posture assessments, the completeness of evidence citations, and the currency of Activity records. Record owners whose intelligence has not been updated within the defined cadence are accountable for that lapse; the Brief is the institutional moment at which the accountability is exercised. This accountability function is what distinguishes the governance architecture from a well-designed information system: the records are not merely available; their maintenance is required and reviewed.

**Normative conditions review:** The Brief's accountability function extends, at a higher level of cadence than weekly, to the organization's overall compliance with the normative conditions that Part IV specifies as prerequisites for legitimate deployment of the architecture. Specifically: Is the organization maintaining institutional integrity — are there observable say-do gaps between stated commitments and operational behavior that the communications program is papering over rather than closing? Are any engagement approaches using means that work around stakeholder rational agency rather than through it — manufactured urgency, concealed provenance, selective evidence? Are there current engagements whose intensity or asymmetry is disproportionate to the legitimate stakes at issue? This review is not a weekly checklist item. It is a governance obligation that the organization's senior leadership and the owners of the Brief must take up periodically — at minimum, at each quarterly planning cycle — and that the Brief's architecture supports by surfacing the pattern of posture assessment, signal design, and engagement activity that such a review requires. The normative conditions are not self-enforcing; their satisfaction depends on organizational character and the willingness of governance participants to exercise the accountability the Brief structure makes possible.

The Strategic Communications Brief closes the adaptive loop that the intelligence record architecture opens. The architecture provides the structure within which posture is assessed and signals are designed; the Brief is the process through which assessments are reviewed, coherence is enforced, and the approach is adapted. Together, they constitute the full JIC adaptive cycle — audience intelligence, signal coordination, effect monitoring, iteration — instantiated as a sustainable organizational practice.

## VII. Worked Example: The Architecture in Operation

The following example traces the SCI architecture through a complete governance scenario. The scenario is deliberately generic — applicable across regulated industries including energy, healthcare, infrastructure, financial services, and telecommunications — to illustrate the architecture's sector-agnostic character.

**Scenario:** A regional organization seeks to expand a significant operational facility. The expansion requires regulatory approval, generates media attention in the Information domain, and is subject to community assessment in the Civil Society domain. Three stakeholder domains are engaged simultaneously; the cross-domain coherence challenge is inherent to the scenario.

---

**Priority record:** *Expand manufacturing capability.*

Owner: Chief Operating Officer. Strategic justification: the organization's strategic plan requires sustained growth in operational capacity to meet projected demand and maintain competitive position; capacity expansion is an enduring commitment spanning multiple planning cycles, of which this facility expansion is the current primary expression. Stakeholder domains: Government (regulatory agency), Information (regional and trade media), Civil Society (community groups near facility).

The Priority does not expire on the completion of this expansion. A favorable regulatory outcome advances it; an adverse one requires a revised approach to advancing it. The Objectives below define what must happen within this specific expansion cycle to move the Priority forward.

---

**Objective record 1:** *Obtain affirmative regulatory decision from [Agency] by [date].*

Requirements: (1) Technical review completed with favorable staff recommendation; (2) Public comment period navigated without material adverse submission; (3) Commission vote secured.

**Objective record 2:** *Maintain neutral or favorable community posture through the regulatory process.*

Requirements: (1) Primary community opposition group does not escalate to formal regulatory opposition; (2) Local elected officials make no adverse public statements during the comment period.

---

**Action records linked to Objective 1:**

- *Action 1a* (linked to Requirement 1): [Lead Regulatory Analyst] submits favorable technical review recommendation to Commission. Owner: Government Affairs Director.
- *Action 1b* (linked to Requirement 3): Commission members vote in favor at [date] meeting. Owner: Government Affairs Director.

**Action records linked to Objective 2:**

- *Action 2a* (linked to Requirement 1): [Community Group Leader] refrains from filing formal regulatory opposition. Owner: Community Relations Manager.
- *Action 2b* (linked to Requirement 2): [Local Official] makes no adverse public statement during comment period. Owner: Government Affairs Director.

---

**Stakeholder record — Lead Regulatory Analyst:**

Structural position: controls the technical review that determines Commission recommendation; high criticality, single point of authority for Requirement 1.
Attitudinal orientation: currently cautious; prior project approvals were granted with conditions; recent public statements indicate concern about cumulative environmental impact of facility operations. Evidence: two prior approval records, public statement at [date] agency hearing (cited).
Posture strength: central-route; has engaged directly with technical materials in prior reviews; arguments must be substantively sound to move posture.
Enabling conditions: high autonomy — recommendation is within sole discretion; no known institutional constraints.
Posture assessment against Action 1a: cautious; conditional approval likely absent additional technical documentation addressing cumulative impact concern.

*Discovery Intelligence has surfaced a proposed Activity record:* [Analyst] attended an industry technical conference session on cumulative impact assessment methodology ([date]). Proposed link: Stakeholder record (Analyst) and Objective 1 (signals heightened attention to cumulative impact as a review criterion). Record owner validates and accepts.

---

**Messaging Framework record — linked to Stakeholder (Lead Regulatory Analyst):**

*Primary Owner: Communications Lead. Delegate Owners: Government Affairs Director (Priority Lead), Community Relations Manager (Relationship Manager).*

The Government Affairs Director has identified two Gaps on the Analyst's Stakeholder record, both action-scoped to Action 1a:

- *Gap 1 (information gap):* The Analyst has not been provided with the organization's cumulative impact modeling data. Evidence of Gap: public statement at [date] agency hearing indicates concern about cumulative impact; no organizational response in the activity record to date.
- *Gap 2 (information gap):* The Analyst is unaware that the organization's proposed technical review framework aligns with the cumulative impact assessment methodology discussed at the industry conference ([date]). Evidence of Gap: Discovery Intelligence-validated Activity record confirms the Analyst's attendance at the relevant conference session.

**Information Points** (contributed by Government Affairs Director as Delegate Owner):

- *Information Point 1 (linked to Gap 1):* Phase 2 represents a net improvement in cumulative environmental impact relative to current operations because [specific technical basis]. Evidence citations: [independent environmental assessment report], [prior agency finding on comparable project], [industry benchmark data].
- *Information Point 2 (linked to Gap 2):* The organization's proposed technical review framework employs cumulative impact assessment methodology consistent with the approach discussed at [conference session]. Evidence citations: [conference session materials], [technical review framework submission].

**Talking Points** (authored by Communications Lead from Information Points 1 and 2):

Synthesized into a coherent briefing narrative for the technical engagement with Agency staff — sequencing the environmental performance data before the methodological alignment argument, in accordance with the Analyst's established preference for empirical evidence over procedural framing.

*AI Assist* generated initial Information Point candidates from the Analyst's Stakeholder record, identifying Gap 1 as the primary posture barrier. The Government Affairs Director identified Gap 2 from the Discovery Intelligence activity record, added Information Point 2, refined the evidence citations for both points, and finalized the Delegate Owner contribution. The Communications Lead authored the Talking Points synthesis, applying the sequencing judgment that SME domain knowledge alone does not supply.

---

**Activity records:**

- *Technical briefing conducted with Agency staff* ([date]): linked to Objective 1, Requirement 1; linked to Stakeholder (Analyst). Organization-initiated. Outcome: Analyst requested supplemental documentation on cumulative impact modeling. Posture assessment updated: cautious, pending supplemental submission.
- *Supplemental documentation submitted* ([date]): linked to Objective 1, Requirement 1. Organization-initiated.
- *Community forum attended by [Community Group Leader]* ([date]): Discovery Intelligence surfaced; linked to Stakeholder (Community Group Leader). Analyst observed posture as engaged but not yet adversarial; updated posture assessment.

---

**Alignment cycle — Signal coherence review:**

At the weekly Strategic Communications Brief, the Government Affairs Director and Community Relations Manager surface a potential signal coherence issue: the technical Messaging Framework for the Regulatory Analyst emphasizes improved environmental performance in operational metrics that differ from the language being used in community engagement materials. The community materials describe the expansion in terms of economic benefit and job creation; the regulatory materials describe it in terms of technical compliance and environmental performance. A community group leader with access to both sets of materials — likely, given the public comment process — may perceive inconsistency between the organization's regulatory posture ("we are environmentally responsible") and its community posture ("we create economic value"), creating a credibility gap neither frame individually justifies.

The Brief generates an action item: Community Relations Manager to revise community engagement materials to incorporate the environmental performance narrative, creating consistency across the two audiences without abandoning the economic benefit frame. This is the architecture's anti-decoupling function in operation: the Brief surfaces the coherence gap before it reaches external audiences, generates accountability for closing it, and creates a record of the resolution.

---

**Outcome of the worked example:**

The architecture has done three things simultaneously that no individual function, tool category, or informal coordination mechanism could do together: it has maintained specific, evidence-grounded posture assessments for each consequential Stakeholder across three domains; it has captured observable Stakeholder Activities through Discovery Intelligence and surfaced them for validation without imposing continuous monitoring on record owners; and it has identified a cross-domain signal coherence gap through the alignment cycle and generated accountability for resolving it before external damage occurred.

These three functions — posture intelligence, activity monitoring, and coherence assurance — are the operational expression of the governance architecture's theoretical foundations: RDT's requirement that legitimacy be managed as a strategic resource, rhetoric's requirement that institutional ethos be maintained through signal coherence, and the JIC's requirement that the adaptive cycle be sustained through institutional discipline rather than ad hoc coordination.

## Conclusion: The Architecture as Theoretical Derivation

The SCI model is not a product design that has been given a theoretical rationale after the fact. It is a governance architecture that derives from theoretical requirements, each of which is traceable to established intellectual traditions and to the structural features of the governance problem itself.

The Priority record exists because governance without strategic warrant is coordination without purpose — and because the warrant must be enduring, not bounded by any single outcome. The Objective and Requirements structure exists because behavioral specificity is the precondition for posture assessment that predicts outcomes rather than merely measuring sentiment. The Action record is stakeholder-facing because the architecture's purpose is to produce stakeholder behavior, and every structural element must remain oriented toward that purpose. The Stakeholder record operationalizes the posture construct because posture is the central unit of governance — the thing that changes when influence succeeds and degrades when the say-do gap opens. The Messaging Framework is Gap-grounded and evidence-accountable because rhetoric's logos requirement and the anti-sophistry constraint make any alternative a governance mechanism for unverified claims; its hybrid ownership model — assigning Information and Messaging Point development to the Subject Matter Experts who manage the intelligence, and Talking Point synthesis to the Communications Lead — enforces the division of labor that prevents SME knowledge from bypassing communicative coherence, and prevents the communications function from bypassing substantive intelligence. The Activity record is actor-neutral because the organization's actions and stakeholders' responses are both constitutive of the engagement intelligence that posture assessment requires. Discovery Intelligence makes distributed ownership viable by converting active monitoring into validation. AI Assist makes Messaging Framework development viable for additional-duty record owners by converting synthesis into evaluation. The alignment cycle closes the loop that the record architecture opens, making the governance function adaptive rather than static.

Each element is a derivation. The architecture is complete when every theoretical requirement is satisfied and every design choice is warranted.
